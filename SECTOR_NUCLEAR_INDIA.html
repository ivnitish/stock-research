1. The Theme: Why Nuclear, Why Now?

India has announced one of the most ambitious nuclear expansion programmes in history:

Metric Current Target Timeline Multiple
Installed nuclear capacity 8.8 GW 100 GW By 2047 11x
Intermediate target 8.8 GW 22.4 GW By 2031-32 2.5x
Under construction now — 13.6 GW Various stages —
Nuclear share of total power ~2% ~15-20% (est.) By 2047 8-10x

Why this is different from past announcements: - SHANTI Bill 2025 — for the first time in India's history, private companies can own and operate nuclear plants (49% equity cap). This breaks the NPCIL monopoly. - Budget allocation: ₹20,000 Cr for Small Modular Reactors (SMRs) alone - Liability reform: Removal of supplier "right of recourse" — this was THE blocker for international vendors (Westinghouse, GE). Now resolved. - Fleet mode execution: 10 identical 700 MW PHWRs being built simultaneously to reduce cost through learning curves - Repurposing old thermal plants: Government will use retired coal plant land for nuclear — removes land acquisition bottleneck

The maths of the opportunity: - Adding ~91 GW of new capacity (from 8.8 GW to 100 GW) - At ₹15,000-25,000 Cr per GW (indigenous to foreign-assisted) - Total capital expenditure: ₹14-23 lakh Cr ($170-280 billion) over 21 years - Even the intermediate target (22.4 GW by 2032) implies ~₹2 lakh Cr of spending in the next 6 years

This is comparable to India's entire defence capital budget for 5 years. The companies positioned in this supply chain will see order books swell over the next decade.

Key insight for stock picking: The first four categories (Labour + Mechanical + NSSS + Electrical) account for 65% of capex. Companies positioned here have the most revenue leverage.

Nuclear plants need heavy equipment transported, and the broader energy infrastructure buildout supports shipyard demand. Additionally, nuclear-powered submarines are a separate but related opportunity for defence shipyards (GRSE, Mazagon Dock).
