## Key Investment Principles

### 1. Risk vs Uncertainty — Know the Difference
- **Risk** is quantifiable — you can assign probabilities. Risk is like weather: might rain, carry an umbrella.
- **Uncertainty** is unknowable — like climate change. You can't calculate it.
- Most investors confuse the two. They treat risk as uncertainty and get paralyzed, waiting for "certainty" before investing.
- **"There is no certainty in investing. That's the most fundamental principle."**
- Investing = using imperfect information to make probabilistic decisions about an unknowable future.

### 2. The DCF Frame of Mind
- Not just a spreadsheet — use DCF as a **mental model**.
- When crisis hits, ask: "Will this war/event impact the **long-term cash flows** of my business?"
- If NO → opportunity (price dropped, value didn't). Buy more.
- If YES → re-evaluate thesis, be willing to sell at a loss.
- **"All models fail in the face of what really happens on ground."**

### 3. Correction Taxonomy
- 10% = healthy correction (where we are now — his words during the Iran war period)
- 15% = correction
- 20% = bear market
- 30% = crisis
- 40% = crash
- 50% = panic
- **"Don't panic before 50%."**

### 4. Kal Chakra vs Linear Time
- Western finance treats time as linear (start here, compound for 20 years, straight line up).
- Indian concept: **Kal Chakra** — cycle of creation, preservation, destruction, recreation (Brahma, Vishnu, Mahesh).
- You cannot have creation without destruction. Bear markets lay the seed of future growth.
- **"People who understand it's not a straight line — it's a cycle — will survive."**

### 5. Active Patience (Not Buy and Forget)
- Buy and hold ≠ buy and forget.
- **Active patience:** Review thesis once a year or when material events occur.
- Be willing to change your mind when facts change.
- S. Naren's principle: "Be contrarian with a calculator" — contrarianism needs math behind it, not just stubbornness.

### 6. The Chauffeur vs Planck Knowledge
- From Charlie Munger's mental model.
- **Planck knowledge:** Actually understanding the business, having lived through cycles, doing the real work.
- **Chauffeur knowledge:** Surface-level — knowing the jargon (NIMs, CASA, phases) without understanding what drives them.
- Most retail investors have chauffeur knowledge. Differentiate and move toward Planck knowledge.

### 7. Survivorship Bias
- For every one Warren Buffett, there are 99,000 who invested the same way but didn't survive (family reasons, lost interest, blew up at a critical moment).
- **"Nobody who is not a professional investor should look at stock markets to become rich. They should look at stock markets to keep themselves rich."**

### 8. Non-Comparison as a Competitive Edge
- "Who am I when I'm not comparing? I am dull because I'm comparing. I am average because I'm comparing."
- Stop showing P&Ls on social media. You're only answerable to your spouse, kids, and family.
- 99% of Indians earn ₹2,000 to ₹1 lakh/month. At ₹1L/month, saving 50%, at 12% returns → **30 years to reach ₹10 Cr.**
- Chasing unrealistic numbers leads to detrimental decisions.

### 9. Asset Allocation — Always
- Never 100% equities, even if you're a professional.
- Think of yourself as a stock: if your career (human capital) is tied to markets, your financial capital should have more debt. If your income is stable, take more equity risk.
- His own allocation: ~40% direct stocks, ~50% mutual funds (3 funds: balanced advantage, flexi cap, large cap), ~10% PMS.
- **"How much peaceful sleep can you take at night? If not even with 10% in stocks, don't pick stocks at all."**

### 10. When Markets Fall — What He Actually Does
- If he has capital, he **deploys**. Doesn't try to time bottom.
- Not in the "100% invested all the time" camp.
- When he sees froth/bubble: doesn't sell, but **slows down equity allocation**, moves to liquid/debt.
- Doesn't sell stocks because "I cannot time the market."
- **Thinks like a business owner:** Is the business going to be fine in 3-5 years? If yes, the stock price is noise.

### 11. Spreadsheets Are Overrated
- "If a spreadsheet tells me I should buy or sell a stock, that stock is not worth buying."
- The business case should be **obvious** — within circle of competence, great margin of safety, then just wait.

### 12. Five-Year Test for Stock Pickers
- Give yourself 5 years. Pick stocks AND invest in 3 good mutual funds simultaneously.
- After 5 years, compare. If the fund manager did better, give them your money. If you did better, allocate more to direct stocks.
- Most people will find the fund manager wins.

### 13. Delayed Gratification — But Not Everything
- Money is a medium of exchange that gives you options.
- **"Compounding is a Western concept. Life is not just about compounding. It's also about living in the present."**
- "Die with Zero" philosophy (Bill Perkins): fulfill responsibilities, but don't defer everything to a future that may not come.
- "One thing Buffett cannot get back is time."
