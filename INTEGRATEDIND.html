1. Business Summary

A formerly dormant holding company shell that transformed overnight into a food products company via acquisition. In FY23/24, it acquired Nurture Well Food Limited — a biscuit manufacturing plant producing brands Richlite, Funtreat, and Crunchy Craze. Also trades organic/inorganic food products and processed food items.

The transformation: ₹5 Cr revenue (FY23) → ₹1,067 Cr TTM. This is a 200x jump driven entirely by the acquisition, not organic growth.

Listed on: BSE only (not NSE). Low liquidity. Hard to exit in size.

3. The Three Open Questions

Question 1: Why is the tax rate 3%? (Critical)

YearPBTTaxEffective Rate
FY23₹5 Cr₹3.65 Cr73% (normal)
FY24₹27 Cr₹1.89 Cr7% (suspicious)
FY25₹68 Cr₹2.04 Cr3% (very suspicious)

The rate drops exactly when revenue explodes. Two explanations:

Benign: The acquired biscuit unit (Nurture Well Food) had large accumulated losses from its prior loss-making years. Indian tax law allows set-off of brought-forward losses against current profits (Section 72). If ₹200-300 Cr of losses exist in the acquired entity, the tax shield could last 3-5 more years.

Concerning: The ₹68 Cr "profit" includes inflated revenue from related-party trading that is circular — recognising revenue without real economic substance, with the tax payment being deferred or avoided via creative accounting.

How to verify: Annual report → Note on Deferred Tax Assets and Brought Forward Losses schedule. If there's a DTA of ₹50-100 Cr on the balance sheet — benign. If there's no DTA and no explanation — red flag.

Impact when tax normalises: At 25% tax rate, FY25 PAT would be ₹51 Cr (not ₹67 Cr). P/E re-rates from 11.3x to 19x. Still cheap, but less so.

Question 2: Why are payable days 760? (Very Concerning)

Normal food companies: 30-90 days payable.

Integrated Industries: 760 days payable (FY25).

This means the company is not paying its suppliers for over 2 years. There are two interpretations:

Benign: The "trading" part of the business — if they're a distributor/aggregator for food products, they may have very long credit from large principals (like a consignment model where they only pay when they sell). Some agri-commodity traders operate this way.

Concerning: Related-party payables that are never actually paid — circular transactions where a subsidiary "supplies" goods, the parent records revenue, but the payable never settles. This is a classic small-cap fraud structure in India.

How to verify: Annual report → Related Party Transactions note. If the 760-day creditor is a single related party entity — exit immediately. If it's spread across multiple unrelated suppliers — benign.

Question 3: FCF is deeply negative despite "profits" (Important)

In FY25: profits ₹67 Cr, but FCF is -₹81 Cr. The company spent ₹129 Cr on capex (expanding the biscuit facility?) while generating only ₹48 Cr from operations.

Financing bridge: They raised ₹87 Cr through financing (warrants, borrowings) to cover the gap. This is sustainable only if the capex is creating earnings-generating assets. If the capex is real factory expansion — this is fine, growth capex is expected. If it's not verifiable — concerning.

6. The Bear Case (If Questions Raise More Red Flags)

The 760-day payable + 3% tax + related-party vagueness + BSE-only listing is the exact template for a small-cap accounting fraud in India. Companies like Ruchi Soya, Vakrangee, Manpasand Beverages all had:

  • Explosive revenue growth via acquisition
  • Low/zero tax rates
  • Inflated receivables or payables via related parties
  • No institutional ownership

Not saying this is a fraud — but the checklist overlap is enough to require annual report verification before committing capital.
