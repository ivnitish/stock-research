- **Biggest realized wins:** ASTERDM (+₹21.3K, +74%), OIVL (+₹17.6K, +47%), HERITGFOOD (+₹15K, +21%)

### Pattern 1: Too Many Positions — Theme Chasing Without Moat Check

**The problem:** 150+ unique stocks touched in 22 months. At this scale, no single position gets proper diligence. The portfolio reads like a thematic ETF shopping list:
- Power theme: SUZLON, INOXWIND, NHPC, IREDA, SWSOLAR, KPEL, JPPOWER, RTNINDIA, GENUSPOWER
- Defence/PSU: GRSE, MAZDOCK, COCHINSHIP, KERNEX, RVNL, IRCON, NBCC, BHEL, HUDCO
- Railways: KERNEX, TEXRAIL, RVNL, IRCON
- Fiber/Telecom: STLTECH, HFCL, RPPINFRA
- Fintech: GROWW, PAYTM, UGROCAP, RBLBANK
- EMS: KAYNES, NETWEB, EIMCOELECO

**Munger would say:** "Diversification is protection against ignorance. It makes little sense if you know what you're doing." With 150 stocks, nothing gets real conviction.

**Better approach:** Max 15 positions. Deep work on each. Every entry needs a written thesis before buying.

### Pattern 2: Sold Winners Too Early, Held Losers Too Long

The closed position P&L distribution is stark:
- **Winners were small:** Most profitable exits were +1-10%. The one big win (ASTERDM +74%) was a small ₹28K position.
- **Losers are big:** STL (open, -40%), SWIGGY (open, -48%), EPACK (open, -30%), PARADEEP (open, -38%). These were held through massive drawdowns without thesis review.
- **Root cause:** No pre-defined exit triggers. Trades were entered without a stop or a "what would change my mind" framework.

### Pattern 3: IPO Bias Creates Overvalued Entries

- SWIGGY: Bought at IPO ₹390 (now ₹281, -28%). IPO investors pay a freshness premium.
- BLUESTONE: IPO entry, exited near flat (-0.1%). Lucky.
- OLAELEC: Bought IPO at ₹73 avg, exited at -4.8%. EV hype trade with no moat.

**Pattern:** IPOs are designed for sellers, not buyers. When a company IPOs at maximum valuation with maximum hype, it's usually the worst time to buy. SWIGGY at ₹390 IPO vs current ₹281 is the clearest example.

**Better approach:** Wait 6-12 months post-IPO. Let the hype deflate and the first 1-2 earnings cycles show real performance. The business doesn't change — only the price does.

### Pattern 4: Cyclicals Bought at the Wrong Point in the Cycle

- PARADEEP: Bought fertilizer at peak phosphoric acid cycle → now -38%
- STLTECH: Bought fiber at peak demand narrative → revenue collapsed -42% as fiber glut set in
- SUZLON, INOXWIND: Bought renewable energy at peak order inflow excitement → small gains but barely broke even (+0.8%, +0.9%)

**Pattern:** Cyclicals require cycle-timing. Buying a commodity/cyclical at peak earnings multiples is a mistake. When everyone is bullish on a sector and the stock is "cheap on P/E" using peak earnings — the P/E is lying. Normalised earnings are lower.

**Better approach (Munger):** Don't buy cyclicals at all unless the moat is structural, not just cyclical tailwind. STLTECH has no moat — just commodity fiber. PARADEEP has no moat — just a port location in a regulated sector.

### Pattern 5: High-Quality Exits and Low-Quality Holds (Backwards)

Looking at what was exited profitably vs what's still held at losses:

| Sold at profit | Still holding at loss |
|---|---|
| ASTERDM (+74%) | SWIGGY (-48%) |
| HERITGFOOD (+21%) | STLTECH (-40%) |
| OIVL (+47%) | PARADEEP (-38%) |
| GODFRYPHLP (+7.5%) | ETERNAL (-33%) |

The sells show better stock-picking instinct than the holds. The problem is behavioural: selling winners (locking in gains feels good) while holding losers (avoiding realising a loss feels necessary). Classic loss aversion bias.

**Better approach:** Grade holdings quarterly. Anything Grade C that hasn't improved thesis in 2 quarters: exit regardless of P&L. Reframe: "if I had cash, would I buy this today?" If no → sell.

**Better approach:** Position size = conviction × margin of safety. Grade A = up to 15% of portfolio. Grade B = 5-10%. Grade C = 0-5% (hold existing; no new buys).
