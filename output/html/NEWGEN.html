Newgen is something rare in Indian listed software: an actual product company. It owns a workflow-automation platform called NewgenONE that banks, insurers, and government agencies in 70+ countries use to run core operational processes — loan origination, KYC, trade finance, claims, regulatory compliance. Once a bank embeds it, replacing it isn't a technology decision; it's a multi-year operational risk that puts every teller, relationship manager, and compliance officer through retraining. That's why annuity revenue (annual maintenance + SaaS subscriptions) is now 68% of the mix, customer retention runs near 100%, and the company has compounded profit at 21% over five years while remaining nearly debt-free.

The stock is still 67% below its Dec 2024 peak of ₹1,379 because the market spent FY26 H1 worried that AI would obsolete workflow automation — CIOs paused large deals to figure out their AI strategy, Q1 FY26 OPM crashed to 14%, and FII holding bled from 19.4% to 14.5% over the year as the price went from ₹1,379 to ₹401. The Q4 FY26 print is the first decisive piece of evidence that the AI-driven deal pause is thawing rather than entrenching. Revenue back at ₹453 Cr (a new quarterly high), margins back at 34%, subscription revenue +24% for the year (SaaS subset +36%), US +20% — every single metric the bear case predicted would break has held or improved.

The one real concern is something that wasn't in the prior thesis at all: working capital days exploded from 72 to 244 over FY26. That's a 3.4x increase in cash tied up in receivables and unbilled revenue. Two readings are possible — either Newgen has won several large enterprise deals with backloaded payment schedules (read: growth investment, normalises in 2-3 quarters), or collections are quietly deteriorating in a way the headline numbers don't show. The Q4 print made the first reading more credible (OCF was ₹232 Cr for the year, still positive and growing), but the next two quarters need to show this normalising or the entire FCF-quality story changes. This is now the load-bearing watch for the position.

At ₹459, the asymmetry is roughly 4x with a 50% margin of safety against the base case — high enough to add aggressively below ₹430 and to hold full position at current price.

Summary Verdict

Why this business?

India produces hundreds of IT services companies that sell developer hours. Newgen is the rare one that owns a product — a unified low-code platform that handles the workflow logic, document management, content services, and compliance audit trails for large enterprises. The customer is a bank that needs its loan origination to run consistently across 5,000 branches, an insurer processing 50,000 claims a month, or a government agency tracking citizen applications. The product is sticky because the workflows themselves are how the customer operates — once you embed Newgen, your business processes are partly inside Newgen. That's why the company holds 70+ country deployments, 17 verticals of pre-built industry logic, and an annuity revenue mix that has climbed from ~40% a decade ago to 68% today.

What makes this interesting at ₹459 is the gap between perception and the underlying business. The market read FY26 H1 as evidence that AI replaces this category. The actual FY26 H2 print shows the opposite — revenue +12% YoY in H2, OPM crossing 30%, US growing 20%, subscription revenue compounding at 24%. The stock has barely moved against this evidence (down 2.86% today; flat over 6 months) while FY26 full-year EPS came in at ₹21.12 against the ₹484 from a month ago. P/E is now 19.7x trailing on a business that just printed 25.8% OPM and 25.8% ROCE, with 36% YoY SaaS revenue growth and US revenue compounding 20%. That's the dislocation the position is sized against.

Detailed Analysis

Deep Dives

#### A. The working capital blowup — what does it actually tell us?

In Mar 25, Newgen's working capital days stood at 72 — meaning 72 days of revenue tied up in receivables, inventory, and unbilled items net of payables. In Mar 26, that number is 244. That's a 3.4x expansion over twelve months, and it's the single most concerning data point in the rewrite. Let me work through what could be causing it and what each possibility means.

Working capital days is computed roughly as (receivables + unbilled revenue + inventory − payables) ÷ daily revenue. For a software business there's no real inventory and payables are small, so the dominant driver is receivables (debtor days = 164, up from ~120 a year ago) plus unbilled revenue. Unbilled revenue rises when a project has been delivered but the customer hasn't accepted the invoice yet — typically because milestone-based billing schedules tie payment to customer acceptance of a deliverable, not to Newgen's delivery date.

Three plausible causes. The first is structural growth — Newgen has signed several large enterprise deals (the four international wins announced March-April 2026 are visible examples) where payment terms are backloaded to project milestones and the unbilled revenue accumulates until milestones are hit. This is growth-investment working capital; it normalises as the projects move to billing phase. The Q4 FY26 OCF of ₹232 Cr supports this reading — OCF didn't collapse, which means cash is still being generated, just at a lower conversion ratio than before. Second is customer-mix shift — winning more government or large-bank business shifts the average payment terms from 90 days (private mid-market) toward 150-180 days (government, regulated banks, large enterprise procurement). This is a deliberate trade-off Newgen has been making for years. Third is collection deterioration — actual late payment, customer financial stress, or weakening enforcement. This is the bear case and the one that would justify reducing position size.

Distinguishing between these in the next two quarters is straightforward. If Q1 FY27 (Jun 2026) shows WC days dropping back toward 180 with OCF/PAT above 0.8x, the first cause is dominant and the position should be added to. If WC days stay at 244+ with OCF/PAT below 0.5x, the third cause becomes the central concern and the position should be trimmed by half. The middle case — WC days at 200-240 with OCF/PAT 0.6-0.8x — is the second cause and means the business has structurally changed customer mix but is still healthy; hold full position without adding.

The single most important quarterly check from here until further notice is the receivables + unbilled revenue line in the balance sheet, scaled against quarterly revenue. The headline P&L numbers are now insufficient — cash flow quality is the new thesis variable.

#### B. The SaaS transition — what re-rate does it justify?

Subscription revenue grew +24% YoY in FY26, with the SaaS subset within it growing +36%. Total subscription revenue is now somewhere around ₹180-200 Cr (~12% of total revenue), with the SaaS portion at ~₹90-110 Cr (~6-7%). The trajectory matters more than the current mix: at +36% SaaS growth against total revenue growth of +6%, SaaS share is expanding by roughly 200-300bps per year. By FY30 (4 years out), SaaS could plausibly be 18-22% of revenue and total recurring revenue (annuity + SaaS) could be 78-82%.

The re-rate math: Indian SaaS compounders with 70%+ recurring mix and 20%+ growth trade at 35-50x P/E. Newgen at 68% recurring and current trailing P/E of 19.7x has a multiple discount of 15-30 P/E points relative to that benchmark. Closing half that gap (to ~30x) on FY30 PAT of ~₹530 Cr produces a market cap of ~₹15,900 Cr, or 2.4x current. Closing the full gap (to ~40x) produces 3.3x. Neither is a multi-bagger in the 5x sense, but both are well above the broader market expectation embedded in the current price.

The non-obvious part: the re-rate doesn't happen continuously. It usually happens in two or three step-functions as specific milestones are crossed — typically when recurring revenue mix crosses a round-number threshold (75%, 80%) and when growth rate of recurring revenue is sustained above a round-number threshold (25%, 30%) for 4-6 consecutive quarters. The Q4 FY26 print is the first concrete data point that the second condition is being satisfied. The first condition (75% recurring) is realistically 2-3 years out.

1. Business Summary

Newgen Software Technologies (NSE: NEWGEN, BSE: 540900) is a Noida-based enterprise software product company founded in 1992 by Diwakar Nigam. It builds and sells a unified low-code/no-code platform called NewgenONE that enables large organisations — primarily banks, insurance companies, and government agencies — to design, automate, and manage complex business workflows. Think of it as the operating system for how a bank processes a loan, or how an insurer handles a claim: documents flow in, rules are applied, decisions are made, compliance is tracked — all on Newgen's platform.

The company operates across 17 verticals in 70+ countries. Revenue is split between product licenses (new customers, one-time), annuity contracts (ATS/AMC — annual maintenance on existing deployments), SaaS subscriptions (cloud-hosted Newgen), and professional services (implementation). The shift toward SaaS and annuity revenue is the most important structural transition underway.

Key distinction from Indian IT peers: Newgen is a product company, not a services company. It does not sell developer hours. It sells access to its platform and earns margins accordingly — 25-28% OPM vs 15-18% for typical Indian IT services firms. Competitors are global: ServiceNow ($150B market cap, 8% OPM at $1.4B revenue scale), Pegasystems ($5B market cap, 8% OPM, 10% growth), Appian ($2B market cap, -5% OPM, 20% growth), and IBM's BPM suite. Newgen competes in their markets and wins — which is exceptional for an Indian software company.

3. The Compounding Engine — Q&A

Q1. Where does the profit come from, and is the headline ROIC sustainable?

Newgen earns ~25.8% ROCE on about ₹1,400 Cr of capital employed (₹1,775 Cr reserves + small debt − ~₹400 Cr of liquid investments). Total operating capital actually deployed in the business is closer to ₹1,000 Cr, generating ~₹260 Cr of operating profit (16.5% of revenue at full-year average, 34% at Q4 FY26 peak) — that's an actual operating-asset ROIC closer to 26%. The headline 25.8% ROCE is real and high.

The harder question is whether the incremental ROIC matches the trailing one. The reinvestment over FY22-26 (capex of ~₹620 Cr cumulative against PAT of ~₹1,250 Cr) is producing the FY26 revenue base of ₹1,574 Cr vs FY22 base of ₹779 Cr — incremental revenue of ₹795 Cr on incremental capital of ₹620 Cr (at roughly the same margin) implies incremental ROIC around 30-35%. That's better than the trailing average, which is the right sign — the new investment is earning more than the old, suggesting the business is in the middle phase of compounding rather than the late one.

Q2. What is Newgen reinvesting in, and how long does the runway last?

Newgen reinvests in three buckets. The first is R&D — primarily the NewgenONE platform, new vertical modules (banking, insurance, government), AI integration. Capex shows up here partly as capitalised software development (which is why the FY24-26 capex of ₹147-217 Cr looks high for a software business). The second is geographic expansion — sales hires, partner enablement, regulatory compliance for new markets, particularly US. The third is acquisitions / strategic capability additions — the company has done small acquisitions historically (Asparity 2021) but no large ones.

The runway is genuinely long. India + global BPM/workflow automation TAM is somewhere around $50B and growing at high-teens CAGR. Newgen's $190M revenue is below 0.4% of TAM. Even if growth in the TAM moderates and Newgen's relative share doubles to 0.8%, that's a $400M+ business — twice current size — without any market-share leadership. The competitive structure does not favour displacement (ServiceNow is dominant in IT service management; Pegasystems in case management; Newgen owns BPM + content services for mid-tier global banks and EM enterprises). Realistic 7-10 year runway.

Q3. What does Newgen look like in 2 and 5 years if base case plays out?

2 years (FY28): Revenue ₹2,000-2,200 Cr, PAT ₹400-450 Cr, OPM ~27%. Recurring revenue mix 72-75%. SaaS subset around 12-15% of revenue. US revenue ~₹500 Cr (vs ~₹330 Cr today). Working capital days back to 150-180. Promoter holding stable at ~53%. Management's challenge: scaling sales execution in US without diluting product margin; deciding whether to attempt a large acquisition with the cash pile or return capital via buyback.

5 years (FY30): Revenue ₹3,000 Cr, PAT ₹530 Cr at 26-28% OPM. Recurring revenue ~80% of mix. SaaS subset ~20%. US is the largest single geography. Market cap re-rated to ~₹15,000 Cr at 28-30x P/E. The thesis fork happens around FY28 — if SaaS mix is by then >18% and US is >25% of revenue, the multiple re-rate continues. If not, the business is still high-quality but caps out at ~25x P/E and the position becomes a 2-3x outcome rather than 3-4x.

Glossary

  • ACV (Annual Contract Value): The annualised value of a customer's subscription / contract. Industry-standard SaaS metric.
  • Annuity revenue: Recurring revenue from annual technical support (ATS), annual maintenance contracts (AMC), and SaaS subscriptions. Predictable, sticky, valued at higher multiples than one-time license revenue.
  • ATS (Annual Technical Support): Annual support contract that customers pay to receive bug fixes, patches, version updates on their installed Newgen deployment. Typically 18-22% of original license value, per year, perpetually.
  • BFSI: Banking, Financial Services, and Insurance — the largest customer vertical for enterprise workflow software.
  • BPM (Business Process Management): The category of software that helps enterprises model, automate, and monitor business processes. Newgen's core product.
  • CWIP (Capital Work in Progress): Capital investment that's been made but not yet capitalised as a productive asset. For software companies, mainly capitalised internal software development.
  • D/E (Debt to Equity): Total borrowings divided by shareholder equity. Below 0.3x is generally considered safe.
  • ECM (Enterprise Content Management): Document and content management software. Adjacent to BPM. Newgen offers both as part of NewgenONE.
  • FCF (Free Cash Flow): Operating cash flow minus capital expenditure. The cash actually available to shareholders after the business has been re-invested.
  • FII (Foreign Institutional Investor): Foreign mutual funds, pension funds, hedge funds owning Indian equity. Their flows tend to set marginal prices for mid-caps like Newgen.
  • Low-code / No-code: Software that lets users build applications and workflows by configuring components visually rather than writing code. Reduces dependence on developers — major selling point for enterprise customers.
  • MOS (Margin of Safety): Discount of current price to estimated fair value. Buying at MOS protects against analytical error.
  • NewgenONE: Newgen's unified low-code platform. Combines BPM + ECM + AI + analytics into one product.
  • OCF (Operating Cash Flow): Cash generated from core business operations. Strong OCF/PAT ratio (>1x) indicates clean earnings.
  • OPM (Operating Profit Margin): Operating profit divided by revenue. Newgen runs at 25-28%, materially higher than IT services peers at 15-18%.
  • P/B (Price-to-Book): Market cap divided by book value. Useful when ROE can normalise.
  • P/E (Price-to-Earnings): Market cap divided by trailing PAT. Newgen at 19.7x trailing.
  • PEG (Price/Earnings to Growth): P/E divided by forward EPS growth rate. Below 1.0x = potentially undervalued.
  • ROCE (Return on Capital Employed): EBIT divided by capital employed. Measures how efficiently total capital generates operating profit.
  • ROE (Return on Equity): PAT divided by shareholder equity. Measures return for owners specifically.
  • SaaS (Software as a Service): Software delivered via the cloud on subscription, vs perpetual license + maintenance. Higher multiple because revenue is more predictable.
  • TAM (Total Addressable Market): The total revenue opportunity available if a company captured 100% of a market.
  • Working capital days: (Receivables + inventory + unbilled revenue − payables) ÷ daily revenue. Measures cash tied up in operations. Newgen's jump from 72 to 244 days is the central concern in this rewrite.
