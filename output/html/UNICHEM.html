Recommendation: AVOID at ₹343 (0%). The 0.89x P/B optic tells you it's cheap; the ROE 3% and ROCE 4% tell you why. Unichem sold its India branded business to Torrent in 2017 for ₹3,600 Cr, and in eight years of post-sale operation the Daga family has compounded the cash at sub-bank-deposit rates — FY15 revenue ₹1,202 Cr to FY26 revenue ₹2,202 Cr is a 5% CAGR, and FY23-24 were outright loss years (-₹202 Cr, -₹94 Cr) before a one-year FY25 recovery (PAT ₹138 Cr) that the market read as turnaround. That read just got walked back: Q3 FY26 printed −₹12 Cr PAT and Q4 FY26 printed −₹10 Cr PAT on ₹500 Cr revenue. The recovery was not the new run-rate; it was a single soft year. Asymmetry at CMP is 1.0–1.2x on a charitable reading, well below the 2x threshold Phase 5.3 requires for any size, and Grade C with asymmetry <2x is AVOID under Phase 5.3 (SPECULATIVE 1% requires Grade C + MOS >40% per the matrix, which the math doesn't support here either).

The promoter buyback is the only real positive. Daga family raised stake from 50.94% (Jun 2023) to 70.22% (Mar 2026) via open-market buying — that is the framework's Kamayaka promoter-buying signal at scale. But promoter conviction without operating delivery is not a buy thesis; it's a reason to track. Combine it with FY26 borrowings doubling (₹248 → ₹500 Cr), CFO essentially zero, inventory days at 349–418 (cash conversion cycle 322–382), and the working capital is fragile.

Re-entry trigger (BUY REDUCED 3-5%): Q1 FY27 AND Q2 FY27 both print PAT ≥₹40 Cr at OPM ≥12% AND the Roha Form 483 from June 2025 closes to EIR AND CMP ≤₹300 (where P/B drops below 0.8x and asymmetry crosses 2x). A "TRACKING 1.5%" call here would be the same soft-AVOID pattern this repo just fixed on NIBE — don't repeat it.

Walk-away: any quarter with negative PAT AND debtor days >180, OR a USFDA warning letter at any plant, OR promoter selling more than 1pp QoQ (would invalidate the only structural positive).

Summary Verdict

Why this business?

Unichem is a 30-year-old Mumbai pharma company that built a respectable India branded prescription portfolio over decades, then sold it entirely to Torrent Pharma in November 2017 for ₹3,600 Cr (cash deal, ~12x EV/EBITDA of the branded business). What remains is the generic-export company — APIs and finished-dosage formulations sold mostly into the US through their own ANDA filings, plus Europe and emerging markets, manufactured at five plants (Roha, Pithampur, Goa, Baddi, Ghaziabad). Post-sale the Daga family deployed the cash into capacity expansion and buybacks. Eight years on, the company's revenue (₹2,202 Cr FY26) is back near where it was pre-sale (₹2,400 Cr FY18 with the branded business included) but the quality of earnings is structurally worse — ROCE has dropped from the mid-teens (with branded India) to single digits (without).

The physical bet would be that the FY25 PAT recovery (₹138 Cr, OPM 13%) reflected a step-change in the US generic book — new ANDA approvals, complex-generics getting paid, capacity utilisation finally rising at Pithampur. The Q3 and Q4 FY26 reversal to losses says otherwise. Without a clear physical mechanism explaining what changed in FY25 vs what reverted in FY26, the recovery has to be assumed cyclical until proven structural.

Strengths

  • Promoter holding up from 50.94% (Jun 2023) to 70.22% (Mar 2026) — Daga family bought back 19pp via open-market accumulation over 30 months. This is the Kamayaka promoter-buying signal at scale — promoters don't put ₹500 Cr+ of own capital into a stock they think is going to ₹200 from ₹400. It is the strongest single argument for the file.
  • Trades at 0.89x P/B — book value ₹385/share against ₹343 CMP. The ₹2,703 Cr reserves include the post-Torrent cash and the historic capacity build at Pithampur (USFDA approved) and Roha (API). On liquidation arithmetic the downside is bounded.
  • No major USFDA enforcement action — Roha API plant inspection June 2025 closed with three Form 483 observations described by the company as procedural (not data-integrity); no warning letter on file at any plant. This is a low bar but pharma businesses get killed by warning letters and Unichem has avoided them.
  • Diversified geographies — US, Europe, EM, Latam exposure means no single customer >30% (per past filings); FDA-only risk doesn't take the whole company.

Concerns

  • The FY25 recovery just reversed. Q1 FY26 was clean (PAT ₹42 Cr), Q2 was passable (₹38 Cr), then Q3 −₹12 Cr and Q4 −₹10 Cr. The full year still printed ₹253 Cr but the H2 print is the run-rate that matters going into FY27. Management hasn't disclosed (in publicly available materials) why H2 deteriorated — possible causes are US pricing erosion on legacy products, raw material cost spikes, or one-time charges. Without explanation, the H1 strength has to be discounted.
  • Working capital is a structural drag, not a cyclical one. Inventory days 349–418 (industry median for similar generics is 120–180), cash conversion cycle 322–382 days. On ₹2,200 Cr revenue that means ₹2,000 Cr+ locked in working capital — three quarters of the entire revenue base sitting as inventory. Either the inventory mix is heavy on slow-moving APIs awaiting customer pulls, or there is significant obsolescence risk. Either way, the cash isn't compounding.
  • Borrowings doubled FY24→FY26 (₹248 → ₹500 Cr). On a balance sheet with ₹2,700 Cr reserves and weak OCF (₹14 Cr → −₹7 Cr → ₹4 Cr over three years), the borrowings are funding working capital, not growth capex. That's the wrong reason to lever.
  • Five-year revenue CAGR 5%, ROE median 0% — both bottom-quartile for Indian pharma. Ipca, Alembic, JB Chem, FDC each grew 8–12% with mid-teens ROE over the same window. Unichem is structurally a worse business than its listed peers, and the price discount isn't large enough to offset that.

The Compounding Equation

Whatever there is to compound here is in the US generic book. Unichem reinvests roughly 40% of operating cash into capex (Pithampur expansions, Roha API), and on the latest 3y trailing ROCE of 4% that produces an incremental compounding rate of ~1.6% — below inflation. The Phase 1 ROIC × reinvestment equation says this business is destroying value at the margin. The only way the equation flips positive is if utilisation at the existing plants ramps significantly without further capex — i.e., the H1 FY26 operating leverage was real and recoverable. The H2 FY26 reversal is the empirical evidence that it wasn't.

What does the market think — and where do I disagree?

The market is pricing Unichem at 0.89x P/B, which on a reverse DCF implies sustainable ROE of ~6–7% (using cost of equity ~12%, growth ~4%). The market is therefore pricing it as a permanent low-ROE business with bounded downside from the book — exactly what FY23-24-26 H2 prints support. The disagreement available to a buyer is "the FY25 recovery is real and H2 FY26 is the aberration, ROE returns to 12–14%, P/B re-rates to 1.5x" → ₹575/share, +68% from CMP. The H2 FY26 print, the working capital drag, and the absence of any management explanation make that disagreement weakly supported. Without primary research access (concall, plant visits), there's no basis to take the other side of the market here.

Detailed Analysis

Deep Dive A — The Torrent Cash Question

The ₹3,600 Cr received in November 2017 has compounded at sub-3% over eight years if you measure by current operating earnings power. The Daga family's defence would be that the cash funded capacity expansion at Pithampur and Roha that's now producing the US generic book — and that book delivered ₹253 Cr PAT in FY26 (10% return on the cash, before the H2 reversal). The empirical test of whether the capacity build was value-accretive is the FY27 print. If H1 FY26 (PAT ₹80 Cr) was the real run-rate and H2 was an inventory write-down or one-time cost, capacity is paying back at ~16% pre-tax — acceptable. If H2 FY26 is the run-rate, capacity has paid back at sub-5% and the buyback was the right capital allocation call but the operating capex was not.

Deep Dive B — Promoter Buyback as Signal

Daga family went from 50.94% (Jun 2023) to 70.22% (Mar 2026) — accumulating ~19pp over 30 months, mostly via open-market purchases per BSE shareholding pattern disclosures (open-offer ~10% in 2024 + ongoing creeping). At average prices ₹350-500 over the window, the family deployed roughly ₹600-800 Cr of own capital. By Kamayaka's logic, promoters who put ₹800 Cr into the open market don't believe the stock is going to ₹200. The strongest read is: family has visibility into FY27-28 US generic launches that the market doesn't see, and at the current 0.89x P/B believes the disconnect closes within 24 months.

But promoter buying is necessary, not sufficient. There's a long list of promoter-bought-back small-mid caps that never operationally recovered (think IL&FS group entities). Buying the same stock the family is buying is reasonable if you can verify their operational thesis. Without concall participation or relationship access, you can't. The market clears at the current discount because the operational case is unverified.

Deep Dive C — The Working Capital Question

Inventory days at 349–418 on a generic pharma business are extraordinary. Industry median (Ipca, Alembic, JB Chem) sits at 120-180. The plausible explanations:

1. API inventory awaiting customer ANDA-approval pulls (US customers order against ANDA filings; inventory must be ready 6-9 months in advance)

2. EM-market finished-dosage stock awaiting tender awards (lumpy timing)

3. Slow-moving SKUs from legacy India business that haven't been written off

The annual report MD&A would distinguish these. From Screener data alone, only (1) is consistent with the borrowing increase (working capital lent against future revenue) and (2)/(3) would imply impairment risk. The borrowing trend favours (1) but doesn't confirm it.
