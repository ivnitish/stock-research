Recommendation: AVOID at ₹42.6. Watchlist below ₹28.

Newly-listed Vedanta demerger entity — iron ore mining plus ESL steel plant plus Liberia asset. Segment profits are collapsing in 1H FY26 (combined EBITDA down ~55% YoY) while the stock is up ~110% since listing on liquidity flow, not earnings. Summary Verdict

Why this business?

VISL is the Iron & Steel arm carved out of Vedanta Ltd through the June 2026 demerger. It brings three assets under one roof: Sesa Goa iron ore (mines in Goa, Karnataka, Odisha with roughly 4 billion tonnes of resources), ESL Steel at Bokaro in Jharkhand (currently 1.6 MTPA hot metal, targeting 3.5 MTPA), and the Western Cluster iron ore project in Liberia (a magnetite concentrator not yet operational). On paper the pitch is compelling — a captive miner feeding a growing steel plant, with 50+ years of raw material security and an eventual 15 MTPA aspiration. The core bet, if there is one, is that at fair value this business is worth more as a standalone than it was as a footnote inside Vedanta Ltd's consolidated numbers. That's the bull case in one sentence. The problem is that FY25 segment economics were already thin — 11% blended EBITDA margin, ROCE well below cost of capital for a capital-intensive miner-steelmaker — and 1H FY26 has seen both segments' EBITDA halve at the same time the stock has doubled. What you're being asked to underwrite at ₹42.6 is a full-cycle mid-cycle EBITDA recovery, delivery of the ESL expansion, and enough distance from parent-level leverage that dividends don't get upstreamed. All three are possible; none is priced with margin of safety.

Strengths

  • Genuine resource base: ~4 billion tonnes of iron ore reserves and resources across Goa/Karnataka/Odisha/Liberia. In FY25 the mining business produced 6.2 mn DMT of saleable ore, up 12% YoY. Karnataka delivered 5.3 mn DMT, Goa restarted at 0.9 mn DMT after regulatory clearances. This is a physically real, operating asset — not a shell.
  • Near-zero debt at demerger: Per Kotak Institutional Equities and Investec, VISL was allocated only ~$0.2 bn (~₹1,700 Cr) of the parent's $5.5 bn consolidated debt. Balance sheet flexibility is real. (Caveat below: ESL Steel is raising ₹2,000 Cr in NCDs which changes this arithmetic materially if the proceeds don't stay in the entity.)
  • Steel expansion in flight: ESL is executing a hot-metal capacity expansion from 1.5 to 3 MTPA — total approved capex of $349 mn, of which $185 mn had been spent through 1H FY26, leaving $164 mn to commission. This is Phase 0.5 pre-existing capability — the plant exists, the expansion is under construction, not a promise on a slide.
  • Insider validation, sort of: Premji Invest's PI Opportunities AIF V bought 4.84 Cr shares at ₹21.02 (~₹102 Cr) around listing. Not nothing — but this was a listing-discovery trade at half the current price, so it should be read as "someone smart thought ₹21 was fair", not "someone smart thinks ₹42 is fair."

Concerns

  • 1H FY26 earnings collapse: Iron ore segment EBITDA fell to $36 mn in 1H FY26 from $39 mn in 1H FY25 despite production rising 11% — clear pricing/cost headwind. Steel segment EBITDA fell to $6 mn from $27 mn on 4% production decline and a coking coal/margin squeeze. Combined 1H FY26 segment EBITDA of ~$42 mn (₹350 Cr) annualises to ~₹700 Cr, versus ₹1,528 Cr for FY25 — a ~55% collapse in run-rate profits at the same time the stock is on a listing melt-up.
  • Parent group governance overhang: Vedanta Resources (the UK holdco owning 56.4% of VISL through Vedanta Ltd) carries roughly $5 bn of debt with historical dependence on Indian listco dividends to service it. Hindustan Zinc's aggressive payouts and repeated delisting attempts are the case study. Viceroy Research has already flagged that ESL Steel's ₹2,000 Cr NCD proceeds may route to parent debt reduction. This is a specific, named governance mechanism — not generic "Anil Agarwal risk."
  • No standalone financial history: VISL as a listed entity has no P&L, no cash flow, no operating quarterly disclosure of its own yet. Everything the market is trading on is imputed from Vedanta Ltd segment reports. The first standalone quarterly result is pending and is the actual validation test. Buying before this print is buying blind.
  • Thin-float driven price discovery: 56.4% promoter, ~30% institutional accumulation post-listing, leaving a small tradable float. The 110% pop from ₹20 to ₹42 in nine sessions is not price discovery of intrinsic value — it is mechanical supply/demand imbalance with the T2T (trade-to-trade) surveillance flag lifted on June 30. Prices set by thin floats revert when floats loosen.

How does this actually compound? (one-line summary; full breakdown in Section 3 below)

It doesn't, at the level demanded by a multi-bagger frame. Iron ore mining and integrated steel are commodity cyclical businesses where ROIC = commodity price minus cost curve position, and VISL's cost curve position is unclear — its Karnataka mines are C1 cash-cost competitive but the Goa restart economics and ESL's coking-coal exposure are pressure points. Full breakdown in Section 3, but the summary is that this is a cyclical value stock at best and a re-rating trade at worst, not a compounder.

What does the market think — and where do I disagree?

The market is pricing VISL at ₹16,674 Cr equity value plus ~₹1,700 Cr net debt = ~₹18,374 Cr enterprise value. On FY25 segment EBITDA of ₹1,528 Cr that is 12x EV/EBITDA — richer than JSW Steel (~10x), Tata Steel (~8x), and dramatically richer than NMDC (~5x with 3.85% dividend yield). On 1H FY26 annualised EBITDA of ~₹700 Cr the multiple balloons to ~26x, and on Investec's FY26E EBITDA of $140 mn (~₹1,160 Cr) it is ~16x. To justify ₹42 with any margin of safety you need mid-cycle EBITDA of ~₹2,000 Cr sustained (which requires both commodity recovery and successful commissioning of the ESL expansion by FY28) AND multiple durability at 10x+ despite parent leverage overhang. The market appears to be paying for the composite of the demerger unlock optionality, the Premji anchor, the Vedanta group's history of eventual value delivery, and the thin-float momentum — none of which is a fundamental anchor. Where I disagree: the implied "fair value" of ₹42 requires the steel expansion delivering at ROIC above cost of capital, and I have no evidence yet — no quarterly disclosure, no analyst-day guidance, no ESL Steel standalone history strong enough to underwrite it.

Second-Order Stress Test

5-Whys on the ROIC engine:

Why does this business earn returns today?

1. It has captive iron ore feeding its own steel plant, and a large mining book selling ore in the open market.

2. Why does that produce returns? → Iron ore mining margins are ~16% at the FY25 print, driven by low C1 cash cost in Karnataka mines and open-market realisations tracking global iron ore prices.

3. Why is C1 cost low? → Karnataka mines are open-cast, high grade (58-62% Fe), close to rail infrastructure; the resource base was acquired at low historical cost.

4. Why isn't this a moat? → Because iron ore is a commodity — the marginal producer sets price. Karnataka lump ore competes on quality but not on structural pricing power. NMDC, government-owned, is a lower-cost, larger-scale competitor with 5x the market cap. When iron ore prices fall (as they did through 1H FY26), realisations fall for everyone; VISL's earnings drop >proportionately because fixed costs don't.

5. Why doesn't the steel arm rescue this? → Because ESL is a sub-scale (1.6 MTPA) integrated plant competing with JSW Steel (35 MTPA), Tata Steel (34 MTPA domestic + Europe), SAIL (20 MTPA), and JSPL (~10 MTPA). None of the fifth-layer answers is durable — they all point to "this is a commodity player without scale advantage."

The world in 2 years if base case plays out:

By FY28 VISL should have commissioned the ESL 3 MTPA hot metal expansion, closed the DI Pipe plant at Goa (420 KTPA), and progressed the Liberia magnetite concentrator toward first production. If the steel cycle turns and coking coal normalises, combined EBITDA could reach ₹2,000-2,500 Cr with steel contributing a rising share (management targets 70% steel in EBITDA mix by FY29 vs 30% today). Management's agenda in this window is heavy — commissioning a plant expansion that is behind schedule (spent $185 mn of $349 mn, still on the "in progress" list years after approval), executing the 5 MTPA aspiration on top of that, and re-negotiating Goa mining approvals as they scale up.

What success itself triggers:

If VISL genuinely delivers, valuation catch-up brings it into JSW's peer set (10-12x EV/EBITDA) — which is roughly where the stock trades today. Success priced in advance is not a re-rating trade. And success brings the ugly second-order problem: a cash-generating listco is exactly what Vedanta Resources UK needs to service parent debt. Expect a dividend policy that upstreams meaningfully to Vedanta Ltd, then further to VRL — cash that doesn't get reinvested at 20% incremental ROIC because the parent's needs come first. This is the mechanism that killed HZL as a compounder for minorities over the last decade.

Detailed Analysis

Deep Dives

A. Is VISL an integrated miner-steelmaker, or a mining company with a steel plant attached?

This distinction matters more than the marketing collateral suggests. A true integrated player like Tata Steel or JSW captures the full commodity chain value — captive ore feeds captive steel at cost, so consolidated margin equals the finished-steel spread over raw-material cost curves. VISL is not this. In FY25, the iron ore segment reported ₹6,086 Cr revenue and ₹1,006 Cr EBITDA — a segment that mostly sells ore into the open market (5.4 mn DMT sold externally out of 6.2 mn DMT produced, per Vedanta's sales summary). The steel segment reported ₹7,928 Cr revenue and only ₹522 Cr EBITDA on 1,337 kt of finished steel — a standalone-plant economic. There is no meaningful transfer-price benefit visible in the segment prints. The two businesses live under one legal entity but the operating integration is limited.

Why this matters for the valuation: the integrated peers (Tata, JSW) earn 15-20% consolidated EBITDA margin at mid-cycle because captive ore compresses the input cost curve. Sub-scale integrated players like ESL Steel — 1.6 MTPA in a market where JSW is 35 MTPA — earn less. On the mining side, standalone iron ore miners like NMDC earn 30-40% EBITDA margins at mid-cycle because they don't have the steel capex drag. VISL sits in the middle: too small in steel to earn integrated margins, too capex-heavy in steel to earn pure-miner margins. The FY25 blended 11% margin is the result of that middle-ground position. Unless the ESL 3 MTPA expansion delivers scale economics — and the 5 MTPA follow-on materialises — this margin ceiling is structural.

B. The Vedanta Resources debt question, and why it matters at the VISL listco level

Vedanta Resources plc (VRL) is the UK holdco that owns 56.38% of VISL via Vedanta Ltd. VRL carries approximately $5 bn of standalone debt and has historically depended on Indian listco dividends to service it — the most-documented case being Hindustan Zinc, where minority shareholders watched a ~₹40,000 Cr special dividend flow up the chain in FY23, followed by repeated delisting attempts. The mechanism is transparent: VRL orders Vedanta Ltd to declare a large dividend; Vedanta Ltd, controlled by VRL, complies; a 63% share of the dividend flows to VRL; VRL uses it to pay coupons on the $5 bn debt stack.

VISL as a new listco is exactly the vessel this mechanism needs — a fresh legal entity with iron ore cash flows and (per the demerger scheme) minimal inherited debt, meaning maximum distributable surplus. The Viceroy Research allegation on the ₹2,000 Cr ESL NCD raise is a specific application of this concern, but even absent that specific transaction, minority investors in VISL should assume that free cash flow gets distributed rather than reinvested. This changes the compounding math meaningfully. A commodity business earning 15-20% ROIC that reinvests can compound; the same business that pays out 80% of profits cannot. The multi-bagger frame requires reinvestment. Without it, VISL becomes a yield stock at best, and only when the commodity cycle turns.

The load-bearing question for anyone considering ownership: is the dividend policy going to look like NMDC (predictable, minority-friendly, ~40% payout) or Hindustan Zinc (parent-serving, opportunistic special dividends)? There is no answer yet because there is no dividend policy yet.

C. Why the base-case still shows a loss from CMP

The base case assumes the steel cycle turns, ESL expansion delivers, and VISL earns mid-cycle EBITDA of ₹1,600 Cr by FY28 — a 130% recovery from 1H FY26 run-rate. At peer-average 8x EV/EBITDA (blended miner + integrated steel multiple), enterprise value = ₹12,800 Cr. Subtract net debt of ~₹1,700 Cr and you get equity value of ~₹11,100 Cr, or ~₹28/share. That's 34% below CMP.

For the CMP of ₹42 to be fair on the base case, one of three things has to be true: (a) mid-cycle EBITDA is meaningfully above ₹1,600 Cr — say ₹2,200 Cr, which requires 5 MTPA steel already delivered and margins at Tata Steel's level; (b) the peer multiple should be 10x+ not 8x — which requires either sustained iron ore pricing above trend or the demerger commanding a durable premium; (c) net debt gets paid down materially — which contradicts the parent-upstreaming concern above. None is impossible. All three together are not the base case; they are the bull case, priced today.

1. Business Summary

VISL is a demerged entity from Vedanta Ltd (listed June 15, 2026) that houses three iron-and-steel businesses: (1) Sesa Iron Ore — mines in Goa, Karnataka, Odisha producing 6.2 mn DMT of saleable ore in FY25 with pig iron plants in Goa; (2) ESL Steel Ltd — 1.6 MTPA integrated steel plant in Bokaro, Jharkhand, producing TMT bars, wire rods, DI pipes, and billets; (3) Western Cluster Ltd — magnetite iron ore project in Liberia, pre-commercial, with a 3 MTPA concentrator plant approved at $280 mn capex. The mining business is FY25's earnings backbone (66% of combined EBITDA); the steel arm is loss-making at 1H FY26 margins. Management targets scaling to 5 MTPA steel and 15 MTPA aspirational iron ore in the medium term. Chairman is Anil Agarwal, founder of Vedanta Resources.

3. Compounding Engine — Q&A

Q0. Operating Leverage Test — does growth create margin leverage?

  • Iron ore production growth (FY25 vs FY24): +12%; EBITDA change: -40%. Reverse leverage — price fell faster than volume grew.
  • Steel production growth (FY25 vs FY24): -4%; EBITDA change: +132% (₹225 → ₹522 Cr) — cost normalisation, not volume leverage.
  • 1H FY26: iron ore volume +11%, EBITDA -8%. Steel volume -4%, EBITDA -78%.
  • Operating leverage present? No. The relationship between volume and margin is dominated by commodity price and coking coal cost — not scale.
  • ESL capacity utilisation: ~85% at 1.6 MTPA hot metal — running close to full, hence the expansion. This is a constraint that argues for the 3 MTPA expansion, but it also means further volume growth requires capex, not incremental leverage.
  • CWIP trend: material. ESL expansion $185 mn spent of $349 mn approved; Liberia $280 mn unspent. Capex cycle is early-mid stage.

Q1. Is incremental ROIC genuinely high (>20%) — and is it structural, not cyclical?

At the segment level, the FY25 return on capital was mediocre and 1H FY26 turned negative. Iron ore FY25 EBITDA of ₹1,006 Cr on mining capex + inventory + receivables in the ₹4,000-5,000 Cr range implies mid-teens ROCE; steel with ₹522 Cr EBITDA on ESL's ₹6,000 Cr+ asset base implies single-digit ROCE. Neither clears cost of capital at 12-13%.

The structural question is worse than the cyclical one. Iron ore mining returns are entirely dependent on where iron ore prices sit relative to the global cost curve. VISL's Karnataka assets are competitive but not lowest-cost — NMDC's Bailadila mines produce at lower C1 than Sesa's Karnataka operations, and the parent government-ownership structure means NMDC gets first call on rail slots. Steel returns for a 1.6 MTPA plant against 30+ MTPA peers are structurally sub-scale. The framework's incremental-ROIC test asks whether the next rupee of capital earns above cost of capital — for VISL, the answer is: only if the steel cycle is favourable AND the ESL expansion delivers scale economics that shrink per-tonne fixed costs. Two conditions, both cyclical. Answer: no, incremental ROIC is not structurally high.

Q2. How much capital can be redeployed at these returns — what's the reinvestment runway?

Approved capex in flight (per parent's 1H FY26 disclosure): ~$550 mn including ESL 3 MTPA hot metal ($349 mn), Liberia concentrator ($280 mn), and Goa DI Pipes ($81 mn). Additional 5 MTPA aspiration would layer on another $1-1.5 bn over 3-5 years. That's a real reinvestment runway of ~₹15,000-20,000 Cr over the next 5-7 years. The question is not runway size — it is return on that runway. If mid-cycle ROIC on the deployed capital is 10-12% (the framework's cost-of-capital hurdle), the reinvestment doesn't compound intrinsic value, it merely maintains it. That is the honest assessment.

Q3. What's the implied compounding math?

At Grade C with sub-scale steel and cyclical mining, the sustainable growth rate = reinvestment rate × ROIC = 50% × 10% = 5%. Not a multi-bagger math. Combined with a P/E and EV/EBITDA already above peer average, the 5-year total return math from ₹42 is negative. Smell test: Even the aspirational 5 MTPA steel + 15 MTPA iron ore scenario gets to ~₹2,500 Cr EBITDA by FY29 — at 10x that is ₹25,000 Cr EV or ~₹58/share, a 38% total return over 4 years, 8% CAGR. This is the bull case. The base case is negative.

Q4. What breaks the thesis?

The compounding breaks if: ESL Steel's ₹2,000 Cr NCD proceeds are utilised for anything other than the ESL 3 MTPA expansion capex, OR if iron ore realisations remain below $85/t (62% Fe, CFR China proxy) through FY27 while coking coal stays above $220/t.

Leading indicator I'd see before the stock price tells me: Quarterly steel segment EBITDA margin — if it stays below $20/t for two consecutive quarters, cyclical recovery is not happening.

Q5. Can I hold through a 40% drawdown? What's my conviction anchor?

I would not hold this through a 40% drawdown because I don't have a conviction anchor at CMP. The anchor for a commodity name is a demonstrable cost-curve position — VISL doesn't have one that clearly beats NMDC or Tata's captive ore. My anchor sentence at ₹42: none. At ₹25 or below, the anchor becomes "asset value of the iron ore reserves and near-zero debt provide a floor while I wait for the steel cycle" — a different bet, buyable at a different price.

What I will NOT do in a drawdown: buy more before standalone quarterly results validate run-rate EBITDA. If the position ever exists, hold size at 1-2% until a full year of standalone financials is on the record.

Haircut valuation on management's own numbers. Take management's $526M FY29 EBITDA, apply a realistic execution/cycle haircut to 60-70% ($315M-$368M), and value it at a peer 8x EV/EBITDA. 6. Competitive Landscape

Market Position

VISL competes in two distinct markets. In iron ore mining it faces NMDC (largest listed miner, government-owned, ~40 mn DMT annual production), Adani-controlled Rungta Mines, and captive-mining players (Tata Steel's Joda-East, JSW's captive base). Sesa's ~6 mn DMT puts it at ~5th-largest merchant iron ore player. In integrated steel, ESL's 1.6 MTPA is a sub-scale plant against JSW (35 MTPA), Tata Steel (34 MTPA domestic + Europe), SAIL (20 MTPA), and JSPL (10 MTPA). VISL is not top-5 in either market segmentation.

The demerger deck reframes the steel scale ambition — and in doing so introduces a disclosure inconsistency worth flagging. The earlier Vedanta Ltd segment decks (the basis for the rest of this file) describe ESL at 1.6 MTPA hot metal expanding toward 3.5 MTPA. The demerger deck now describes ESL at ~2.8 MTPA hot metal expanding to 5.0 MTPA by FY29 (per management demerger deck), and public coverage of the demerger uses both "1.6 MTPA plant" and "3 MTPA to 5 MTPA" interchangeably. The base installed steelmaking capacity is 1.6 MTPA; the ~2.8-3.0 MTPA figure appears to fold in blast-furnace hot-metal capacity or already-commissioned expansion rather than finished steel, and I cannot reconcile the two to a single clean number from public sources. That the endpoint itself moved from 3.5 to 5.0 MTPA between two management decks issued months apart — on the single most important physical number in the thesis — is a yellow flag on disclosure consistency, not a bullish upgrade (General Instruction #6). Even at the aspirational 5 MTPA by FY29, VISL would remain a fraction of JSW/Tata/SAIL scale; the deck's own claim that steel margins reach ~16% by FY29 asks a sub-scale plant to earn what 20-35 MTPA integrated peers earn, which the peer table below does not support as a structural outcome.

Structural Advantages vs Peers

1. Captive iron ore for steel: ESL sources iron ore from Sesa's Karnataka and Odisha mines — a real cost advantage of ~$15-20/t vs merchant procurement. Quantified effect: steel segment margin would be 200-400 bps lower without captive ore. Replication barrier: iron ore mining licences are scarce. This is a real advantage but is roughly matched by Tata Steel and JSW's captive positions.

2. Diverse mining footprint: Presence in Goa, Karnataka, Odisha, and Liberia — geographic diversification means single-mine regulatory issues (Goa 2012 ban was the reference case) don't threaten the whole business. Quantified effect: earnings volatility is lower than a single-geography miner. Replication barrier: modest — Vedanta bought its way in over 15 years.

3. Balance sheet flexibility post-demerger: Near-zero debt (~₹1,700 Cr net) enables capex without over-leverage. Quantified effect: interest cost saving vs peers with more debt on their steel arms. Replication barrier: this is a one-time demerger consequence, not a durable advantage.

For steel EBITDA to nearly 9x in three years, three physical things must all happen roughly on schedule: (1) ESL commissioned to 5 MTPA finished steel — a plant the existing file already notes has been "in progress" since ~FY22 with a slipping timeline; (2) Value-Added Products mix taken from 1.7 to 5 MTPA, management targeting ~100% of production as VAP (High Silicon Iron, DI Pipes, Wire Rods, Rebars) — VAP is what lifts realisation per tonne; (3) steel EBITDA margin to ~16%, versus $11/t (roughly 3-4% margin territory) in 1H FY26. That is a move from cyclical trough margins to Tata/JSW mid-cycle margins AND a near-tripling of volume AND a full mix upgrade, all inside 36 months. Each is individually plausible in a favourable steel cycle; all three together on time is the definition of flawless execution stacked on a cycle turn. The 1H FY26 print ($6M steel EBITDA annualising to ~$12M) is going the wrong way relative to a $41M FY26 guide, let alone $366M by FY29.

Other deck items (management guidance, mostly unverified specifics): iron ore capacity expansions — Karnataka 5.9→6.5 MTPA (+Janthakal 0→0.5), Goa 3.5→5.5, Orissa 5.5→6, Liberia/WCL 0→10 MTPA high-grade concentrate by FY32; beneficiation 2→6 MTPA plus a new 2 MTPA pellet plant by FY28; R&R ~4 bn t / 50+ years (confirmed in public coverage); land bank ~2,300 acres at ESL + 700 acres Karnataka; India steel demand 8.4% CAGR 2023-2032e, per-capita consumption 102 kg vs world avg 215 kg; logistics moat claim (captive river fleet, ports, rail sidings); ESG (23% gender diversity, green-steel via National Gas Grid proximity, waste-heat recovery). The Liberia 10 MTPA concentrate target is dated FY32 — beyond the thesis horizon and, per the file's own note, currently a near-stalled project ($1M spent through 1H FY26), so I give it no weight in the FY29 math.

Does this change the Grade or the AVOID call? No. The deck makes the bull case legible; it does not add margin of safety. If I haircut management's $526M FY29 EBITDA to 60-70% ($315M-$368M) — reasonable given the execution/cycle stack and the file's documented slippage — and apply a peer 8x EV/EBITDA, EV lands at ~$2.5-2.9Bn (~₹21,000-24,500 Cr at ~₹84/$). Subtract the rising capex debt (the $1.3Bn programme is largely debt/parent-funded, so net debt grows from ~₹1,700 Cr toward, say, ₹6,000-8,000 Cr as the build proceeds), and FY29 equity is ~₹13,000-18,500 Cr — roughly flat to modestly below today's ₹16,674 Cr mcap, and that is three years out, undiscounted. Discounted at 14% over three years, the haircut-FY29 case is worth ~₹22-31/share today. Even taking management's own undiluted $526M at face value and 8x gets EV ~₹35,300 Cr, less say ₹7,000 Cr grown net debt = ~₹28,300 Cr equity = ~₹72/share in FY29, ~₹49 discounted — a ~15% three-year gross return for a flawless-execution, cycle-turn, no-dilution, no-upstreaming outcome. That is the bull case earning a mediocre return; the base case is still a loss from ₹42.6. Grade stays C (11/25); recommendation stays AVOID at CMP, watch below ₹28.

The single most important skeptical insight: the $526M FY29 target is ~87% dependent on the steel arm going from $41M to $366M, and the steel arm is simultaneously the thing furthest behind (trough margins today, a capacity number that keeps changing across decks, and a plant "in progress" since FY22) AND the thing whose funding ($1.3Bn capex against a <2x leverage cap that only permits ~$266M on FY26 EBITDA) forces reliance on the exact parent whose incentive is to pull cash out, not put it in. The target isn't just aggressive — its funding mechanism is in direct conflict with the governance risk that already anchors the AVOID call.

Glossary — Key Industry Terms

DMT (Dry Metric Tonne)

The unit of iron ore quantity used in mining — a tonne of ore stripped of moisture content, since ore is typically shipped wet from mine. Prices are quoted per DMT to normalise for water content that varies by season and mine.

Hot Metal / Pig Iron

The output of a blast furnace — molten iron with high carbon content (~4%), ready to be either cast as pig iron (solid ingots for external sale) or fed directly into steelmaking (billets, TMT bars, wire rods). ESL Steel's "1.5 to 3 MTPA hot metal" expansion refers to increasing blast furnace capacity.

Billet

A semi-finished steel product — a long, roughly square-cross-section bar of steel — that is subsequently rolled into finished products like TMT bars, wire rods, and structurals. Billet production is the interim step between hot metal and finished steel.

TMT Bar

Thermo-Mechanically Treated reinforcement bar used in construction (concrete rebar). This is the highest-volume finished-steel product for infrastructure and building.

DI Pipe (Ductile Iron Pipe)

A pipe made from ductile iron used mainly for water supply and sewage networks. Municipal contracts drive demand. Higher margin than TMT bars because of specifications and lower competition.

MTPA (Million Tonnes Per Annum)

Capacity measure for iron ore mines and steel plants. VISL's "15 MTPA aspiration" refers to iron ore mining capacity; ESL's "3 MTPA expansion" refers to hot metal / steel capacity.

EV/EBITDA

Enterprise Value divided by EBITDA. Enterprise value = market cap + net debt. EBITDA = earnings before interest, tax, depreciation, amortisation. Used as the primary valuation multiple for capital-intensive commodity businesses because it neutralises capital structure and depreciation policy differences.

Coking Coal

Metallurgical coal (as distinct from thermal coal used in power) used to make coke, which is the reducing agent in blast furnaces. India imports most of its coking coal from Australia — the coking coal price is a key input cost driver for integrated steelmakers.

T2T (Trade-to-Trade)

An NSE/BSE surveillance category where a stock can only be traded on a delivery basis (no intraday), applied to newly listed or highly volatile stocks. VISL was T2T from June 15-30 2026 — its removal on June 30 enabled intraday trading and was one trigger for the price surge.

NCD (Non-Convertible Debenture)

A debt instrument that does not convert to equity. ESL Steel's ₹2,000 Cr NCD raise is a bond issue at the subsidiary level. The concern raised by Viceroy Research is that proceeds could be routed to parent Vedanta Resources for its own debt service rather than staying at the operating subsidiary.

VRL (Vedanta Resources Limited)

The UK-based holding company that owns 56.38% of VISL via Vedanta Ltd. Founded by Anil Agarwal. Not itself listed — its debt is held by external creditors and is serviced primarily from dividends upstreamed from the Indian listcos.
