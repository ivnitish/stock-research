KRSNAA — Krsnaa Diagnostics Ltd

Verdict: BUY REDUCED (3-5% initial, add path to 8-10%) | Date: 2026-07-25 | CMP: ₹541 | MCap: ₹1,740 Cr | P/E: 17.4 | Grade: B (15/25)

India's largest PPP-model diagnostics chain — 190 CT/MRI centres, 140 pathology labs, 4,000+ patient collection centres, 1,500+ tele-radiology centres across 18 states, mostly run in partnership with state governments and municipal bodies rather than as private urban labs. FY26 delivered ₹773 Cr revenue (+8%), PAT ₹101 Cr (+31%), OPM 27%, and — most importantly — cash flow from operations of ₹143 Cr against PAT ₹101 Cr (141% cash conversion), which is the metric that separates a real diagnostics business from an accounting one. The stock is down 39% from its 52-week high of ₹894 to ₹541 while EPS grew 31% — a genuine price-business disconnect that has compressed the multiple to P/E 17 for a healthcare compounder with 27% operating margins. Trades at less than half the multiple of private-lab peers (Dr Lal 45-55x, Metropolis 50-60x). Two real risks — low promoter holding (27.1%) and heavy government-receivables working capital (140 debtor days) — cap the position size and require ongoing verification.

Summary Verdict

Krsnaa is not a private urban diagnostics chain; it's the largest player in the PPP (public-private-partnership) diagnostics model. State governments and municipal bodies contract Krsnaa to operate imaging and pathology services inside government hospitals — the government provides the space and referral flow, Krsnaa provides the equipment, radiologists, and operations, and revenue is either fee-per-test billed to the state or user-pay with subsidised tariffs. This is a fundamentally different economic model from Dr Lal Path Labs or Metropolis, which serve walk-in urban middle-class customers on a private-pay basis.

The economics of the PPP model are worth understanding, because they explain both the valuation gap versus private peers and the working-capital burden. Because the government provides captive patient flow, Krsnaa doesn't need to fund a marketing engine or build brand recall — a CT centre inside a district hospital fills up on referrals, not advertising. So OPM is genuinely high (27-28% is Metropolis-tier despite realisation per test being lower). But because the payer is a state government, receivables run at 140 days versus 40-50 days for private-pay labs. The business generates cash — CFO/PAT 141% in FY26 confirms it — but the balance sheet has to be big enough to carry the receivables float, which is why debt sits at ₹534 Cr and FCF is still negative during the expansion phase.

FY26 was a re-rating candidate that never re-rated. Revenue grew only 8% (in-line, not exciting), but PAT jumped 31% because operating leverage kicked in on the fixed radiologist and imaging cost base as utilisation rose across mature centres. Q4 FY26 alone delivered ₹42 Cr PAT on ₹193 Cr revenue — a 22% net margin quarter. Cash conversion moved from 87 Cr CFO (FY25) to ₹143 Cr (FY26), which is the print the market should have noticed and didn't. Instead the stock fell 37% over the last year because (a) the initial Rajasthan PPP contract was scaled back after a court dispute, cutting FY27 revenue guidance from that project from ₹200-250 Cr to ₹100-150 Cr, (b) FIIs cut positions on the Rajasthan uncertainty, and (c) broad-cap healthcare rotated out during the January 2026 mid-cap correction.

FY27 has a specific set-up. Capex is planned at ₹500 Cr, largely funded by internal accruals + ₹304 Cr sanctioned bank facilities (ICRA A/A1 reaffirmed June 2026) — going into MRI expansion, Rajasthan project completion, and retail infrastructure. Krsnaa Retail (a subsidiary) delivered an 8x revenue jump in FY26 and expanded to 3,500 touchpoints across 5 states, providing a private-pay diversification against the government-payer concentration. Recent order wins include Himachal Pradesh (CT expansion from 12 to 34 institutions) and continued Rajasthan National Health Mission contracts. The Karnataka + UP + MP PPP contracts remain the base business.

Two structural concerns keep this from being a >15% core: promoter holding is only 27.11% (borderline the framework's 30% concern threshold, though stable across four quarters), and government-PPP receivables at 140 debtor days is a real cash trap if any state contests payment (as Rajasthan did briefly). The 39% price fall has already priced in these risks; the current P/E 17 is not asking you to pay for anything but the existing business as-is.

Bull Case

The PPP diagnostics model has structural tailwinds that most investors underappreciate because they've internalised the private-lab economics of Dr Lal / Metropolis. India's public healthcare system is the primary care-provider for 70% of the population but its diagnostic infrastructure is grossly under-invested — most district hospitals have no MRI, many have no functional CT, and pathology is manual and slow. The Ayushman Bharat framework, state-level Mukhya Mantri health missions, and NHM funding have created a decade-long pipeline of state-financed PPP diagnostics contracts. Krsnaa is the incumbent leader with 18 states of operating experience, meaning the next state that decides to franchise its district-hospital diagnostics has a very short list of qualified bidders. This is a genuine capital-and-operations barrier.

FY26 showed the operating-leverage arithmetic working. Revenue grew 8% but PAT grew 31% because the incremental patient throughput on already-installed CT/MRI equipment goes almost entirely to gross profit — the radiologist is salaried, the machine is depreciated, and the marginal test is priced at pre-negotiated PPP tariffs. Q4 FY26 alone delivering ₹42 Cr PAT on ₹193 Cr revenue (22% net margin) is what a mature-utilisation quarter looks like. If FY27's ₹500 Cr capex adds 40-50 new centres and existing centres continue their utilisation ramp, FY28 PAT of ₹150-200 Cr becomes the base-case not the bull-case.

The Retail subsidiary is the free option most analyst notes ignore. Krsnaa Retail scaled from ~250 to 3,500 touchpoints in one year — 14x — with 8x revenue growth off a small base. If retail matures to a ₹200-300 Cr revenue business in 2-3 years at private-lab margins (18-20% net), it adds ₹40-60 Cr PAT that would be valued by the market at private-lab multiples (35-45x), i.e. a ₹1,500-2,700 Cr sub-business. That's larger than the current market cap. Not baked into any sell-side model.

Bear Case

The Rajasthan setback is the specific risk to underwrite. Krsnaa won a large Rajasthan National Health Mission contract in 2023-24; a subsequent court dispute (procurement-process challenge by a competing bidder) forced a downward revision of the project scope, and management guided FY27 Rajasthan revenue to ₹100-150 Cr against an earlier ₹200-250 Cr expectation. This is manageable as a single-project event but signals two systemic risks: (a) PPP contracts can be re-litigated by losers of the bid, extending or shrinking the contract mid-execution, and (b) revenue guidance for individual projects can be revised sharply, which reduces confidence in the multi-year forecast. If a second state contract runs into a similar dispute, the market will de-rate the whole business's contract-revenue reliability.

Government-payer receivables at 140 days is not going to shrink structurally — this is how state governments pay. In good years the ₹534 Cr debt covers the receivables float comfortably. In a stress scenario (state fiscal deterioration, government transition, disputed invoices), receivables could stretch to 180-200 days, forcing further debt or equity raise. Given promoter holding is already only 27%, an equity raise dilutes it further and the market punishes the alignment story.

The 60% discount to private-lab peers is not fully unearned. Dr Lal and Metropolis have brand recognition, walk-in demand, superior receivables (private-pay), and higher operating leverage on marketing spend. Krsnaa's ceiling multiple is probably 25-30x, not 45-55x, no matter how well the business executes. That caps the re-rating case.

Second-order stress test

Five-Whys on the ROIC engine:

1. Why does KRSNAA earn 12.7% ROCE today? Because operating profit on capital employed is decent but not exceptional.

2. Why not higher? Because expansion capex is not yet fully productive — new centres take 12-18 months to reach mature utilisation.

3. Why is mature utilisation eventually 27%+ OPM? Because the government referral flow fills the CT/MRI schedule automatically without marketing spend.

4. Why doesn't this attract competition? Because state PPP contracts are 7-10 year exclusive within a district, and the bidding process rewards operating scale + prior state-level track record — new entrants can't credibly bid.

5. Why is that structural? Because state governments' procurement rulebooks embed "prior similar experience" as a mandatory eligibility criterion; without a portfolio of running PPP contracts, you cannot pre-qualify for the next one.

The fifth answer is a structural moat rooted in government procurement rules. Not as durable as, say, RDSO or Naval certification (those don't renew per contract), but real and hard to compete against. Moat is B-grade, not A-grade.

World-state under the base case (2 years and 5 years):

Margin behaviour through stress (Phase 4.5.3): No stress period in recent history — margins have been stably improving, not compressing. FY26 OPM 26-28% throughout despite the Rajasthan setback. This is a "stable through stress" reading, which is the strongest of the three valid framework states.

Market label vs reality: Market label is "diagnostics company that lost the Rajasthan contract" — that's why the stock is down 39%. Our label would be "misunderstood PPP compounder with cash-conversion proof and expanding physical footprint." Label-change catalyst: two consecutive quarters showing revenue growth >15% and OPM holding above 25%, or announcement of a large new-state PPP contract. Conclusion from three lenses

Primary decision from Lens 1: the 65% discount to same-business peers is roughly 30-40 percentage points wider than the fundamentals justify. Fair discount is 25-35% (for PPP-payer risk); actual discount is 65%. That gap is the alpha. Base case target of 22-25x P/E on FY28 PAT ₹150 Cr = ₹3,300-3,750 Cr MCap = ₹1,032-1,175 per share, a doubling.
