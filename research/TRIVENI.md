## Summary Verdict

### Why this business?

Triveni is really two businesses sharing a listing. The visible one is a large integrated sugar-ethanol-cogeneration player in Uttar Pradesh — cyclical, capital-intensive, sensitive to cane pricing set by the state government, currently earning 8-9% ROCE on 5,689 Cr of revenue. The hidden one is Power Transmission — India's dominant maker of engineered gearboxes for Siemens, GE Aerospace, MAN, Doosan, Rolls Royce, and the Indian Navy — a business earning 34% PBIT margins on ₹370 Cr of FY25 revenue and about ₹127 Cr of segment profit. On May 30, 2026 Triveni completed the NCLT-approved carve-out of this second business into Triveni Power Transmission Ltd (TPTL); the record date fell on June 3 and listing is expected in August 2026. The bet is that once TPTL trades separately, the market will pay a capital-goods multiple for the gear business (Elecon at 34x P/E, Shanthi Gears at 45x) instead of hiding it inside a sugar-cycle multiple, and the sugar stub will simultaneously trade closer to peers like Balrampur Chini on cash-flow terms. What you're really buying is a corporate action, not a compounder.

### Strengths

- **The Power Transmission Business is a genuine capital-goods asset:** 34.3% PBIT margin, 12,500+ installations in 80+ countries, approved supplier status with Siemens, MAN, Mitsubishi, Atlas Copco, Doosan, GE Aerospace, Rolls Royce; 11 new OEM customers added in 9M FY26; ₹485 Cr closing order book at end-FY26 (+25% YoY). This is not a "hidden gem" story any more — it is being separated so the market prices it correctly.
- **Physical capability already built:** ₹400 Cr revenue-capacity plant plus a defence-manufacturing bay commissioned December 2025 that scales potential PT revenue to ₹700 Cr by September 2026; Swiss subsidiary (Triveni Power Transmission GmbH) acquired for Europe. This meets Phase 0.5 — the thesis rides on capacity already in the ground, not capacity management still has to build.
- **Distillery capacity ramp is real:** Rani Nangal multi-feed distillery commissioned in FY25; FY26 distillery revenue net-of-excise reached ₹1,550 Cr with highest-ever production and sales volumes. Ethanol blending mandate now 20% and targeted at 27%, so the demand floor is regulatory rather than commercial.
- **Promoter at 60.98%, stable across four quarters, zero encumbrance disclosed in FY26.** Sawhney family; the same group ran the 2010 Triveni Turbine demerger, which compounded roughly 30% CAGR over 15 years to a ₹18,000 Cr market cap — a directly relevant prior with an operator (Dhruv Sawhney) still in place.
- **Balance sheet not stretched despite the sugar cycle:** ₹2,161 Cr borrowings against ₹3,343 Cr equity (D/E 0.65x), interest coverage above 4x, and the cash cycle absorbs sugar inventory rather than being funded by structural debt.

### Concerns

- **The consolidated business is not a compounder — the sugar stub is a low-return cyclical.** Consolidated ROCE has collapsed from 21% in FY22 to 8-9% in FY25/FY26, PAT grew at −19% CAGR over 3 years and −1% CAGR over 5 years, and free cash flow has been negative in 4 of the last 5 years (FY22 −465, FY24 −255, FY25 −404, FY26 +6). Sugar MSP has been frozen at ₹31/kg since 2019 while cane prices keep rising. Once PT is stripped out, what remains is a business earning single-digit ROCE on 6,000 Cr of assets.
- **PT is only 5-6% of consolidated revenue but 30-35% of consolidated pre-tax profit.** The market has partially seen this — CMP already trades at 35x — so the pure re-rating trade is not obviously mispriced. Combined SOTP in a 20-25x PT multiple case sits at roughly ₹10,000-11,000 Cr versus current mcap ₹9,945 Cr. That is 0-10% upside on the trade, not a multi-bagger.
- **Ethanol economics are worsening, not improving.** Industry ethanol capacity is now 2,300+ Cr litres against demand of ~1,050 Cr litres. Maize-based ethanol (lower margin) is displacing molasses-based (higher margin), and internal molasses transfer pricing is hurting Triveni's own distillery margins.
- **No holdco cushion — you get TPTL directly, or the stub stands alone.** This is a mirror demerger: TPTL shares are issued straight to TRIVENI shareholders (1 for every 3 held), and the residual sugar company retains no stake in TPTL. So there is no parent-held TPTL value propping up the stub — the sugar business must be valued on its own single-digit-ROCE cash flows, and all of the PT re-rating accrues only to the TPTL shares held directly. *(Correction: an earlier version of this note assumed TRIVENI retains a 30% TPTL stake and applied a 40-60% holdco discount — that is wrong for a mirror demerger and has been removed.)*

### How does this actually compound? (one-line summary; full breakdown in Section 3 below)

There is no organic compounding thesis at the consolidated level — return comes from a corporate-action revaluation, not a ROIC×reinvestment engine. Post-demerger, TPTL is the only piece with a compounding story (34% margins, order book +25%, capacity doubling), and it will not be held via TRIVENI but as a separately-listed share. See Section 3 for the walk-through.

### What does the market think — and where do I disagree?

At ₹446.90 and FY26 EPS of ₹12.28, the market is paying 36x for a business whose consolidated ROCE has fallen from 21% to 9% in three years, whose 5-year PAT CAGR is roughly zero, and whose sugar segment is running against a frozen MSP. That multiple is not being paid on the sugar cycle — it is being paid because the market has already partially recognised the PT value that will be split out. Balrampur Chini, a better-run pure-sugar operator, trades at 31x with a 15%+ ROE; Triveni's 8.65% ROE at 36x implies the market is capitalising both businesses inside one number. My disagreement is not with the direction — the demerger will unlock value — but with the size. The full SOTP at a 25x PT multiple, 12-14x sugar/ethanol EBITDA, and a 2x book Water multiple sits at roughly ₹10,000-11,000 Cr, or ₹450-495 per share. That is 0-10% upside, not 30-50%. Anyone paying ₹446.90 today is paying close to fair value for the split, betting on TPTL trading at 30x+ (not 25x) once separately listed, and on the sugar stub not collapsing on the ex-demerger date.

### Second-Order Stress Test

**5-Whys on the ROIC engine:**

> Why does this business earn its returns today?
> 1. Because the Power Transmission segment earns 34% margins on engineered gears while sugar earns single-digit returns.
> 2. Why is PT margin so high? Because Triveni is the largest domestic gear maker with approved-supplier status on multiple global OEM lists — a certification set built up over decades.
> 3. Why is that hard to replicate? Because each OEM qualification (Siemens, MAN, GE Aerospace, Rolls Royce) requires 2-3 years of testing on running installations, and the physical reference set of 12,500+ deployed gearboxes is not something a new entrant can synthesise.
> 4. Why hasn't this scaled faster? Because it was buried inside a sugar company where capital allocation, management attention, and investor visibility all defaulted to the larger business. Also because gearbox demand is tied to global capex cycles that are lumpy.
> 5. Why is that changing now? Because the demerger removes the conglomerate discount, aligns management incentives, and lets TPTL raise capital and hire talent as a capital-goods company. The 5th-Why answer is structural — an OEM certification set plus a physical reference base — which makes PT's moat durable. But that moat lives in TPTL, not in TRIVENI post-August 2026.

**The world in 2 years if base case plays out:**

TPTL listed as a separate ₹2,000-3,000 Cr market cap capital-goods company earning ~₹120-135 Cr of PAT on ₹550-700 Cr revenue, with a ₹700+ Cr order book and Swiss subsidiary contributing early Europe revenue. TRIVENI as a stub — sugar plus ethanol plus water — doing ₹6,000-6,500 Cr of revenue at 8-10% consolidated EBITDA margin, with no retained stake in TPTL (mirror demerger), and back to being valued the way Balrampur or Dwarikesh are valued (12-18x on cycle earnings). Management's next challenge is capital allocation on the sugar side: what to do with ethanol overcapacity, whether to expand co-gen, whether the water business is worth keeping.

**What success itself triggers:**

If PT re-rates to Elecon-style 34x on standalone listing, that gain accrues entirely to the directly-held TPTL shares — the residual sugar company keeps no stake in TPTL (mirror demerger), so none of the re-rating lifts the TRIVENI stub. And a successful sugar cycle brings its own problem: cyclical sugar peaks attract policy attention (export bans, price caps) that compress the very margin the market extrapolates. The pattern repeats every 4-5 years and management history through prior cycles is what will decide whether the sugar stub is a hold or a trim.

## Deep Dives

### A. What the Power Transmission demerger actually does — the arithmetic

Under the composite scheme sanctioned by NCLT on December 16, 2025, Triveni transfers ₹373 Cr of assets and ₹114 Cr of liabilities (net ₹259 Cr of net assets) — comprising the Mysuru gear plant, defence bay, IP, employees, contracts — into a new entity called Triveni Power Transmission Ltd (TPTL). Every existing TRIVENI shareholder on the record date (June 3, 2026) gets 1 TPTL share of face value ₹2 for every 3 TRIVENI shares of face value ₹1 held. TPTL will list on BSE and NSE around August 2026.

The mechanical effect on TRIVENI is that on the ex-demerger date, the market cap should drop by roughly the fair value of the PT business — call that ₹1,900-2,850 Cr on 20-30x FY25 PAT of ₹95-100 Cr, or ₹85-130 per TRIVENI share. So a ₹446.90 TRIVENI share should re-price to ₹315-360 as a "sugar stub", and the shareholder simultaneously receives one-third of a TPTL share whose expected listing value is ₹85-130 per TRIVENI share held.

The gap between combined value and current price is small — closer to 0-10% than to the 30%+ that a genuine multi-bagger demerger unlock would require. Two things could still make this interesting: TPTL listing above 30x on defence/aerospace excitement (Shanthi Gears trades at 45x, so the ceiling is real), or the sugar stub not falling as much as the split implies because sugar sentiment turns.

The promoter holds 60.98% of each entity directly. Because this is a mirror demerger, the residual sugar company holds no stake in TPTL — shareholders receive TPTL shares directly (1 for every 3 held), so all of the PT re-rating accrues to those TPTL shares and none of it lifts TRIVENI-the-stub. *(An earlier version of this note assumed TRIVENI retained a 30% TPTL stake subject to a holdco discount; that is incorrect for a mirror demerger and has been removed — the practical effect is that the stub should be valued purely on its own sugar/ethanol/water cash flows.)*

### B. Why the sugar stub isn't a value trap — but isn't a compounder either

Post-demerger TRIVENI is a sugar-and-ethanol business at book value around ₹150/share with ₹280-350 Cr of normalised PAT, running at 8-10% ROCE. That is not the profile of a business that compounds intrinsic value at 15%+ — but it is not the profile of a broken business either. Interest coverage is comfortable, working capital is tied up in cane inventory (an asset, not a liability), and the ethanol capacity ramp gives a mildly deflationary tailwind on margins once maize-vs-molasses mix stabilises. A rational post-demerger multiple sits in the 12-16x range, which puts fair value on the stub at ₹150-230 depending on where in the cane cycle you catch it.

The two things that would move the stub materially: a sugar MSP revision from ₹31 to ₹37+ (industry lobby ask, no timeline), and cane pricing rationalisation via SAP (State Advised Price) reform in Uttar Pradesh. Neither is imminent. Neither can be underwritten. What you can underwrite is that the sugar stub is a cyclical that will trade like Balrampur or Dhampur on the same set of variables, and that the multiple compression from the current 35x on blended earnings to a stub-appropriate 12-16x is the risk that isn't fully priced into ₹446.90 today.

## 3. Compounding Engine — Q&A

### Q0. Operating Leverage Test — does growth create margin leverage?

- Revenue growth (FY26 vs 3Y avg): 5,733 Cr → 6,290 Cr = +10% CAGR
- EBITDA margin now vs 3 years ago: 15% (FY22) → 9% (FY26), −600 bps
- **Operating leverage present?** No — the consolidated business shows negative operating leverage. Revenue up 47% over 4 years (₹4,291 → ₹6,290 Cr), but EBITDA has fallen from ₹636 Cr to ~₹565 Cr in the same window. Sugar cost inflation is absorbing all of the volume growth.
- Capacity utilization: sugar mills run near full capacity in season; PT gear plant at 60-70% utilisation with capacity being scaled to ₹700 Cr revenue potential; distillery capacity utilisation lifted in FY25/FY26 on Rani Nangal ramp.
- CWIP trend: ₹257 Cr (FY22) → ₹32 Cr (FY25) → ₹14 Cr (FY26). Capex cycle has largely absorbed onto fixed assets. New PT capex is smaller (~₹100 Cr additional bays).

The consolidated operating-leverage picture is unattractive. Read the PT sub-segment separately and it looks different — PT revenue +26.8% YoY in FY25, PBIT margin held at 34.3%, so on the gear side there is real leverage. That subsidised the sugar drag in the consolidated headline. The demerger separates these two engines going forward.

### Q1. Is incremental ROIC genuinely high (>20%) — and is it structural, not cyclical?

On the consolidated business, no. Incremental ROIC over the FY22-FY25 window is negative — capital employed rose from about ₹4,300 Cr to ₹6,200 Cr while consolidated EBIT fell from about ₹560 Cr to about ₹380 Cr. That is capital destruction at the aggregate level.

On the PT segment alone, incremental ROIC is exceptional. FY25 PBIT of ₹127 Cr against transferred assets of ₹373 Cr and liabilities of ₹114 Cr, so net assets ~₹259 Cr → PBIT/net-assets of ~49%. Even at more conservative segment-capital-employed of ₹400 Cr (adding working capital) the ROCE is 30%+. The mechanism is structural: engineered gearboxes are sold on OEM approval and technical performance, not price. Once an OEM certifies you as an approved supplier (Siemens, MAN, GE, Rolls Royce), you are on their bill of materials for a decade or more, and each certification takes 2-3 years to earn.

What breaks this: a shift in gear technology (direct drives, magnetic gears) that would obsolete the current install base. Not a near-term risk — the reference base is 12,500+ units in 80+ countries with 30-year design lives.

### Q2. How much capital can be redeployed at these returns — what's the reinvestment runway?

At the consolidated level, the reinvestment story is a distraction — capital is being pushed into a low-return sugar business. The relevant question is only about the PT segment. Global industrial gear TAM is roughly $60 bn; India's domestic gear market is ~₹8,000 Cr and Triveni PT has 15-20% domestic share. Runway comes from three vectors: continued domestic industrial (steel, cement, mining) demand, export expansion via Swiss subsidiary into Europe, and defence — the December 2025 defence bay commissioning and the GE Aerospace + Rolls Royce agreements suggest a materially different revenue mix by FY28.

Capital deployment for the PT business is modest — ~₹100 Cr of additional capex to move from ₹400 Cr to ₹700 Cr revenue capacity. On 34% PBIT margins, that additional ₹300 Cr of revenue converts to ~₹100 Cr of PBIT — a one-year payback on the incremental capex. This is the compounding engine that justifies the demerger.

### Q3. What's the implied compounding math?

For the consolidated pre-demerger business, the compounding math does not work. Reinvestment rate is negative (dividend payout + working capital absorption > operating cash flow), ROCE is 9%, so intrinsic value compounds at 5-7% at best.

Post-demerger, TPTL alone: if PT reinvests 60% of earnings at 30% incremental ROCE, intrinsic value compounds at 18% a year. Over 5 years that is 2.3x. Applied to a listing valuation of ₹2,400 Cr, that projects to ~₹5,500 Cr by FY31. Combined with re-rating from the reference 25x to a Shanthi-Gears-like 35x, total return can hit 3x over 5 years. That is the multi-bagger case — but it lives in TPTL, not in TRIVENI shareholders who own the stub.

For the sugar stub, the compounding math is 5-8% real return at best — cane cycles offset each other, MSP catches up with cost eventually, and reinvestment is limited by policy caps on ethanol offtake pricing.

### Q4. What breaks the thesis?

**The demerger arb breaks if:** TPTL lists at a market cap below ₹1,900 Cr (implying 20x or lower on FY25 PAT) — because that would mean the market is not paying a capital-goods multiple for the gear business, in which case the sum-of-parts has already been correctly reflected in the pre-demerger 35x consolidated multiple, and there is no re-rating to capture.

**Leading indicator to watch:** grey-market indication of TPTL listing price around July 2026 once the entity approaches listing, and TPTL's own peer benchmarking — is management guiding investors to Elecon (34x), Shanthi Gears (45x), or to a discount? Their first standalone concall will set expectations.

### Q5. Can I hold through a 40% drawdown? What's my conviction anchor?

Not really applicable in the standard framing — this isn't a long-hold compounder. It is a special situation with a 6-9 month timeline built around the TPTL listing.

**My conviction anchor:**
> "The PT business has ₹485 Cr of contracted order book at 34% PBIT margins, a defence facility commissioned, and 11 new OEM customers added last year — the earnings quality of the gear segment is real and contracted, regardless of what the market pays for it on day one of TPTL trading."

**What I will NOT do in a drawdown:** average down aggressively pre-listing. The size is capped at 1-2% because the payoff structure is bounded (0-10% base case, 40% bull, −33% bear), and averaging into a bounded-payoff bet destroys the risk-reward math.

**Method:** Sum-of-the-parts on the combined entity, then Owner Earnings multiple on each block — Grade B

| **r** (required return) | 13% | Financials score 2/5 (sugar cycle drag, negative FCF history) → 13% |

**Quick reverse DCF (implied growth at current price):**
```
At current P/E = 36x, r = 13%, n = 5 years, Terminal Multiple = 15x:
  Implied g ≈ 14-16%
  Shortcut: P/E 36 > Terminal Multiple 15 → market implies high growth
```

The market at ₹446.90 is implying 14-16% earnings CAGR for 5 years. The actual 5-year historical PAT CAGR is 0%. ## 6. Competitive Landscape

### Market Position

Two distinct market positions. In sugar, Triveni is a top-5 UP mill operator with 61,000 TCD capacity — smaller than Balrampur (~80,000 TCD) but larger than most private mills. In gears/Power Transmission, Triveni is the largest domestic maker of engineered gearboxes with a 15-20% share of a ₹8,000 Cr domestic market, and one of the few Indian companies on the approved supplier lists of Siemens, MAN, GE Aerospace, Rolls Royce, Doosan, Mitsubishi, and Atlas Copco. Post-demerger these become two separate competitive stories.

### Structural Advantages vs Peers

1. **PT — OEM certification set:** Approved-supplier status with 8-10 top global OEMs, built over 20+ years, each requiring 2-3 years of testing per certification → structural entry barrier → PBIT margin 34% vs Elecon 20%+ and generic Indian gear makers at 12-15%.
2. **PT — reference install base:** 12,500+ units in 80+ countries with 30-year design lives → replacement demand annuity + reference-based pricing power → durable order flow visible in 25% YoY closing order book growth.
3. **Sugar — UP scale + integrated cogen:** 7 mills with 105 MW cogen exports → margin buffer versus non-integrated mills → but the same advantage held by Balrampur, Dhampur, and DCM Shriram, so it's parity within UP, not a differentiator.
4. **Distillery — multi-feed flexibility:** Rani Nangal and other new distilleries can run molasses, cane juice, and grain — matches OMC demand curves better than single-feed distilleries → higher realised distillery margin in favourable years.

**TRIVENI at 36x vs Balrampur at 31x on a sugar-heavy business:** the ~5-turn premium reflects the embedded PT value.

**Re-rating thesis:** If TPTL trades at 30x+ on standalone listing (in Shanthi Gears territory), the SOTP arithmetic works and combined value re-rates from ₹9,945 Cr toward ₹10,500-11,000 Cr. If TPTL trades at 20x (Elecon minus discount), the re-rating fails and TRIVENI stub compresses to ~₹300, giving up 30%+.

### Competitive Dynamics

- **Market share trend:** PT gaining share (11 new OEM customers added in 9M FY26). Sugar stable. Overall Triveni is not a share-taker in sugar.
- **Primary competitive threat:** For PT, direct-drive technology adoption in wind and marine could shift addressable share, but on a decade-plus horizon.
- **Key vulnerability:** Sugar segment vulnerability to MSP policy freeze; ethanol vulnerability to OMC procurement pricing.
- **Policy tailwind/headwind:** Ethanol blending mandate raising to 27% (positive but industry overcapacity dilutes); sugar MSP frozen since 2019 (negative); demerger sanctioned by NCLT (special situation catalyst).

## Glossary — Key Industry Terms

**Power Transmission / Engineered Gearbox**
A gearbox is a mechanical device that converts the speed and torque of a rotating input (motor, turbine) into a different speed and torque for a driven output (compressor, propeller, generator). An "engineered" gearbox is custom-designed for a specific application — not off-the-shelf. Triveni PT makes high-value engineered gearboxes used in industrial power trains (steel mills, cement plants, mining), wind turbines, ship propulsion, and defence (naval submarine and surface ship applications). Each gearbox can cost ₹50 lakh to ₹5+ crore.

**OEM approval / approved-supplier status**
Original Equipment Manufacturers like Siemens, GE, and Rolls Royce publish an approved-supplier list for critical components. To be added, a supplier must pass 2-3 years of qualification testing on materials, manufacturing processes, and on-installation performance data. Once on the list, the supplier is on the bill of materials for that OEM's products for a decade or more. This is what gives the gear business its moat.

**TCD (Tonnes Crushed per Day)**
Sugar mill capacity metric. A mill with 8,000 TCD crushes 8,000 tonnes of sugarcane in a 24-hour period during the crushing season (October-May in North India). Triveni's aggregate capacity is ~61,000 TCD.

**KLPD (Kilolitres per Day)**
Distillery capacity metric. A distillery with 100 KLPD produces 100,000 litres of ethanol/alcohol per day.

**SAP (State Advised Price) vs FRP (Fair and Remunerative Price)**
FRP is set by the central government as the floor price mills must pay for sugarcane. SAP is set by state governments (Uttar Pradesh, Punjab) and typically higher than FRP. Sugar mills in UP pay SAP, which has risen faster than sugar MSP (Minimum Support Price for sugar sold in the domestic market), squeezing margins.

**Sugar MSP (Minimum Support Price)**
The floor price at which mills sell sugar in the domestic market. Set by central government. Frozen at ₹31/kg since February 2019 despite cane cost inflation. Industry lobby is asking for a revision to ₹37-38/kg.

**Molasses / Cane Juice / Maize (ethanol feedstocks)**
Ethanol can be made from three feedstocks: (1) molasses — the syrupy by-product of sugar refining, cheapest and traditionally highest-margin; (2) cane juice — direct from cane, higher yield per tonne of cane but competes with sugar production; (3) maize/grain — used when sugar feedstock is short, but lower margin. Triveni's new distilleries are multi-feed so they can switch based on economics.

**E20 / Ethanol Blending Programme (EBP)**
Government of India programme to blend ethanol into petrol. Target has moved from 10% (2022) to 20% (2025-26) to a stated 27% target. This creates demand-side floor for distillery output but the aggregate industry has over-invested against actual OMC procurement.

**Cogeneration**
Sugar mills burn bagasse (the fibrous residue of cane) to generate steam and electricity. Excess electricity is sold to the grid. Provides an ancillary revenue stream that partially decouples mill economics from sugar prices.
