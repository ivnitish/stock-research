**Recommendation:** WATCHLIST · do not buy at CMP ₹7,951. Buy zone ₹4,000-4,500 (~130x trailing, ~80x FY27E if PAT doubles to ₹180 Cr). At today's 252x P/E with 30% promoter holding and declining, you are paying for perfection in three different stories simultaneously, none of which can compress without taking the multiple with it.

MTAR is sold as defence + nuclear + space. The income statement is something else: **Bloom Energy fuel-cell components are 70% of FY26 revenue**, aerospace and defence is 12%, and civil nuclear is **3% of current revenue but 26% of the order book**. The order book is genuinely strong at ₹2,582 Cr (3x FY26 revenue) and the Q4 FY26 rebound was real — PAT +223% YoY, EBITDA margin 20.2%. The problem is that the market has already capitalised all three optionalities — the SMR nuclear ramp, the Bloom AI-datacentre demand wave, and the aerospace order book — into a single 252x multiple that has no room for any of them to disappoint.

The single largest unspoken risk in this name is **customer concentration on Bloom Energy** (NYSE: BE). Bloom is up 1,200% since May 2025, currently $302 at an $86B market cap and triple-digit forward P/E. The Motley Fool, the Oracle 2.8 GW partnership, and the AI-datacentre power narrative have all already happened — Bloom is no longer a story stock, it is a fully-priced expectation. Any deceleration in Bloom's product revenue (currently +208% YoY) flows directly into MTAR's revenue line, and MTAR has no second customer of comparable size to absorb the shock. This is the kind of single-customer dependency that the framework's Phase 0 threshold check explicitly flags.

## Summary Verdict

### Why this business?

MTAR is a precision-engineering supplier to four end-markets, ranked here by FY26 revenue contribution: (1) **Bloom Energy fuel cell components** — they fabricate the hot-box assemblies and metal-ceramic seal stacks that go into Bloom's solid-oxide fuel cells, currently ~70% of revenue; (2) **Civil nuclear power** — fuel machining heads, coolant assemblies, and high-precision reactor components for India's PHWR program and the upcoming Small Modular Reactor (SMR) wave, 3% of revenue but 26% of order book; (3) **Aerospace & defence** — precision components for ISRO, DRDO, and tier-1 aerospace OEMs, 12% of revenue; (4) **Products & others** — ball screws, roller screws, machined components, 15% of revenue. The bet is that civil nuclear and Bloom both ramp simultaneously over the next three years to convert a ₹2,582 Cr order book into ₹2,000+ Cr annual revenue at 22% OPM. The bet against is that the multiple already prices that exact outcome.

### What does the market think — and where do I disagree?

The implied growth at 252x trailing P/E with 13% required return and 100x terminal multiple is about **38% annualised earnings growth over 5 years, sustained**. That requires PAT to compound from ₹94 Cr (FY26) to ₹470 Cr (FY31) — a 5x in five years. The order book at ₹2,582 Cr supports revenue scaling to ~₹2,000 Cr by FY29 (assuming 18-24 month conversion windows on civil nuclear), which at 20% OPM is ₹280 Cr PAT, not ₹470 Cr. So either the multiple compresses (most likely scenario at any point in the next two years) or the order book accelerates beyond the current trajectory. The market is pricing the latter and the framework forces us to price the former.

### Concerns

1. **Promoter holding 30.4%, down from 46.6% in 3 years** (-16.2 pp). Below the framework's 40% concern threshold. Promoters have been steady net sellers through the run. This is the single most disciplinary signal in this name.
2. **Customer concentration on Bloom Energy** at ~70% of revenue with no second customer of comparable size. Bloom is itself up 1,200% YTD 2026 at a fully-priced expectation; any deceleration flows straight through. This is the threshold-check failure that should force position sizing to 1% maximum even at a much lower price.
3. **252x P/E is not a defence-tech multiple, it is a perfection multiple.** Data Patterns at 84x with 2x the margin and 1.5x the ROCE is the right anchor for what this category should trade at. MTAR is 3x that level.
4. **Cash cycle at 419 days** is structurally onerous. Even with the FY26 OCF of ₹197 Cr, the business absorbs roughly four months of revenue as inventory and another five months as receivables before cash comes back.
5. **0% dividend payout since FY23.** Capital is reinvested into capacity and working capital. The strategy is fine; the lack of return-of-capital culture at this valuation is a flag.

### When do I buy?

- CMP below ₹4,500 (corresponding to ~80x FY27E PAT of ₹180 Cr) AND no further promoter selling for two consecutive quarters
- OR civil nuclear segment converts to ₹150-200 Cr of annual revenue from the current ₹24 Cr (would re-rate the segment mix from a Bloom-dominated story to a multi-engine compounder)
- OR Bloom Energy formally diversifies MTAR's customer concentration below 50% (announcement of a comparable second client)

### When do I exit (if accidentally held)

- Q1/Q2 FY27 PAT does not sustain above ₹40 Cr per quarter (the FY26 Q4 baseline)
- Promoter holding drops below 28%
- Any disclosure of Bloom Energy contract renegotiation or volume cut
- Order book stops growing for two consecutive quarters

## Bottom line

**Story: real. Price: not.** The order book is genuine, the Q4 rebound is genuine, the SMR-nuclear option is genuine. The 252x multiple has already priced all three plus a Bloom Energy continuation that requires Bloom itself to keep compounding at +200% revenue growth. At ₹4,000-4,500 the asymmetry starts to make sense; at ₹7,951 it doesn't. **Data Patterns is the better-quality compounder in the same basket. Raymond aerospace is the cheaper way to own this theme if it's aerospace specifically. Walchandnagar / KSB / Patels Airtemp are the cheaper plays on the nuclear sub-theme** (see `research/SECTOR_NUCLEAR_INDIA.md`).
