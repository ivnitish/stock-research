### Why this business?

Adisoft Technologies is a Pune-based industrial automation integrator. Its job is to take a customer's manufacturing floor — typically an automotive assembly plant — and physically build the automated production line on it: conveyors, robotic arms, custom machines that bolt parts together (Special Purpose Machines, or SPMs), the wiring panels, the PLCs (Programmable Logic Controllers — the small computers that tell motors when to run), and the SCADA software that lets a supervisor see the whole line from one screen. They do not invent any of this technology; they buy components from large global suppliers (Siemens, Schneider, Allen-Bradley, Fanuc, ABB) and assemble them into a working line on the customer's site. The economic value they add is the engineering design, project management, and on-site commissioning — i.e. they are a systems integrator, not a manufacturer.

Why does this business deserve a place in a portfolio? Honestly, on this evidence, it does not. The business is real and the headline numbers look excellent: revenue grew from ₹75.5 Cr in FY23 to ₹131.7 Cr in FY25 (32% CAGR), profit grew faster (63% CAGR), RoE is 39% on paper. The structural backdrop is also genuinely supportive: every car maker is shifting to electric vehicles, which means re-tooling assembly lines; PLI schemes in electronics, white goods, and auto components are pushing capex; "Industry 4.0" is a real spending category. So in principle, an Indian integrator with capacity to execute could ride a multi-year wave.

The problem is that Adisoft is not in a position to ride that wave durably. Almost half its revenue (48.48%) comes from one customer. 78.61% comes from auto. Its working capital cycle has stretched so far that operating cash flow has been negative for two consecutive years even as accounting profit grew — meaning the customers are not paying on time, and the business is funding its growth by borrowing and stretching payables. When a project-based business with that customer concentration and that cash-flow profile lists on the SME platform with a 1,200-for-1 bonus issue executed three weeks before filing the DRHP, the right action is to step back, not to subscribe.

### Strengths

- **Real headline growth and reported margins.** Revenue ₹75.5 Cr → ₹103.3 Cr → ₹131.7 Cr (FY23/FY24/FY25). EBITDA margin expanded from 11.0% to 16.5% over the same period. PAT margin from 8.0% to 12.2%. If the cash conversion supported these, this would be a genuinely strong P&L.
- **Reported return ratios are high.** Weighted-average RoNW 32.92% (FY23–FY25), FY25 RoCE 29.12%, RoE 39.11%. RoNW per the DRHP was 28.43% / 35.47% / 32.71% across FY23/FY24/FY25.
- **Real industry tailwind exists.** Automotive electrification requires re-tooled assembly lines (battery pack, e-axle, motor assembly are physically different from ICE engine assembly). PLI for auto components and electronics is creating greenfield capex. Make-in-India defence corridor pushing factory automation. The market for SPMs and integrated lines is growing in the high single digits to low teens.
- **Promoter-driven, no PE/VC overhang.** No prior institutional investors with rights to dump. The dilution risk is what's in the IPO objects, not what's hidden in shareholder agreements.
- **Lower P/E benchmark vs peer is ambiguous.** Patil Automation (the only listed comparable) trades at 27.28x. Adisoft has higher reported margins, RoE and revenue than Patil. On a like-for-like reported basis the company will argue for parity or a small premium — but parity also means anchoring to a peer that itself has questionable durability metrics.

### Concerns

- **Customer concentration is at the disqualifying level.** Top customer 48.48% of FY25 revenue. Top 5 customers a much larger share. The Multi-Bagger framework's Phase 0 threshold is "single customer >30% of revenue = concern." 48.48% is well past that line. If this customer reduces purchases by 25%, Adisoft's revenue falls 12% and profit falls more (operating leverage runs in both directions). For an integrator, single-customer dependence is structural — one OEM standardising on a different vendor is enough to break the business model.
- **Sector concentration compounds the customer concentration.** 78.61% of revenue from automotive. The two are not independent risks; they multiply. If Indian auto OEM capex slows for any reason (cyclical downturn, EV adoption pause, regulatory shift), Adisoft has no other leg to stand on. The DRHP itself flags "diversification into non-automotive sectors" as a *strength*, which is a tell — it means the diversification is aspirational, not yet executed.
- **Operating cash flow has been negative for two consecutive years despite rising profits.** This is the single most important red flag. Reported PAT grew from ₹6 Cr to ₹16 Cr. But trade receivables roughly quadrupled in two years. Operating cash flow was negative in FY24 and FY25. In simple terms: Adisoft is booking profit on the income statement that has not arrived as cash. The company is funding its growth by stretching payables and drawing on debt — exactly the pattern that precedes a working capital crunch when growth slows. The Phase 0 framework treats "cash conversion ratio < 0.5x for 2+ years" as a concern. Adisoft's cash conversion is *negative*.
- **Governance and disclosure quality is below the bar for a public company.** Partnership-to-public conversion only in September 2025 — the company has been a public limited company for less than three months at DRHP date. Restated financials were prepared by a non-statutory auditor (a separate firm than the regular auditor), which is allowed under SEBI rules but is a softer audit standard than statutory restatement. A 1,200:1 bonus issue was executed on September 5, 2025 — three weeks before the DRHP date — for the obvious purpose of inflating share count to make the IPO price band optically lower. The company flags compliance issues with the Companies Act in its risk factors. None of the directors has experience running a listed company.
- **No defensible operational moat.** All manufacturing premises are rented. No ISO 9001 / ISO 14001 / IATF 16949 certifications (the standard credentials for an industrial supplier to auto OEMs). Trademarks are unregistered. There are no listed patents or proprietary IP. The business is execution + relationships, not technology + scale.

### The Compounding Equation

A multi-bagger compounds intrinsic value at 20%+ for 7–15 years. The math is ROIC × reinvestment rate, sustained over a long runway. Adisoft fails on every leg of this equation, and the failures compound rather than offset.

**The ROIC story doesn't survive cash-flow scrutiny.** Reported RoCE of 29% and RoE of 39% looks great on paper. But RoCE is computed using accounting capital and accounting profit. When operating cash flow is negative and trade receivables are growing 4x in two years, the *cash* return on capital is far lower than the accounting return — possibly close to zero in cash terms. A business where the accounting profit and the cash profit diverge persistently is either (a) a working capital trap that will correct violently when growth slows, or (b) something worse. Either way, the 29% RoCE headline cannot be the basis of a compounding thesis.

**The reinvestment runway is short and customer-locked.** The IPO objects are: ₹41.10 Cr for a new factory, ₹10 Cr for debt repayment, ₹10 Cr for working capital, and the rest for general corporate purposes. A new factory increases capacity but not differentiation. The bigger problem is the demand side — even if capacity doubles, growth depends on whether (i) the top customer increases orders proportionally or (ii) Adisoft acquires comparable new customers fast enough. Neither is guaranteed; both are riskier than the DRHP narrative suggests.

**The runway, if it exists, is for the industry, not for Adisoft specifically.** Industrial automation in India is a real multi-year story. But the winners will be either (a) global integrators with deep technology partnerships, or (b) listed pure-plays with scale, IP and certifications. Adisoft is neither. It is a Tier-2 integrator betting that proximity to one auto OEM in Pune is durable. That is a fragile bet.

### What does the market think — and where do I disagree?

The implicit pitch of this IPO is: "We are a smaller, faster-growing, higher-margin Patil Automation. Trade us at a small discount to Patil's 27x P/E and you get a 25%+ growth business at 18–20x — that's a multi-bagger setup." 

Where I disagree: Patil itself is not a high-quality multi-bagger compounder; it is a moderate-quality SME with a moderate P/E. Anchoring to it is anchoring to a fair price, not to a cheap price. And the disagreement on the *quality side* is much sharper than on the *valuation side*. Patil reports cleaner cash flows, has more diversified end-markets, and didn't execute a 1,200:1 bonus three weeks before filing. Anchoring Adisoft to Patil at parity is overpaying for governance differences alone.

The market may also be assuming the new factory + diversification will materialise. Both are claims, not facts. New factories take 18–24 months to commission and longer to ramp. Diversification away from auto requires winning customers in segments where Adisoft has no track record. A reasonable view is that FY27 numbers will be *worse* than FY25 in profitability terms, before they get better, as the new factory ramps and concentration risk is being actively managed. The market's implicit growth assumption (continuation of 32% revenue CAGR) is unlikely to be realised on the path the company is on.

## 1. Business Summary

Adisoft Technologies Limited is a Pune-based industrial digital automation solutions provider, incorporated originally as a partnership in 2013 and converted to a public limited company on September 11, 2025 in preparation for its IPO. The company designs, integrates and commissions automated production systems for manufacturing customers — primarily Indian automotive OEMs and Tier-1 auto component suppliers. Its product/service set includes:

- **Assembly lines** — physical conveyor + workstation systems for sequential assembly
- **Material handling systems** — automated movement of components between stations
- **Robotic work cells** — 6-axis robotic arms for welding, picking, dispensing, painting (using third-party robots from Fanuc, ABB, etc.)
- **Special Purpose Machines (SPMs)** — custom-built machines for specific operations (e.g., a press-fit machine designed for one specific component)
- **PLC, SCADA and IoT solutions** — control systems and supervisory software using third-party hardware (Siemens, Schneider, Allen-Bradley) and bespoke logic
- **Maintenance and AMC** — minor recurring revenue stream

Critically, Adisoft is an **integrator, not a manufacturer**. It does not produce robots, PLCs, drives, motors, or sensors; it sources these from global OEMs and assembles them into a working system on the customer's premises. The economic value-add is in design, engineering, project management, on-site commissioning, and warranty service. This is a service-and-execution business with the cost structure of a manufacturer (working capital tied up in materials and receivables) but without the IP-driven moat of one.

**Promoters:** First-generation, founder-led. No public information on listed-company experience.

**Operations:** All factory and office premises are rented, including the manufacturing site. The IPO partly funds the construction of a new owned factory unit (~₹41 Cr of objects).

**Customers:** Top customer 48.48% of FY25 revenue. Auto sector 78.61% of FY25 revenue.

**Subsidiaries / Group:** The DRHP discloses a small set of group entities; restated *consolidated* financials are used.

## 3. Compounding Engine — Q&A

### Q1. Is incremental ROIC genuinely high — and structural?

**Reported ROIC (RoCE proxy):**
- FY25 EBITDA ₹21.66 Cr; FY25 PAT ₹16.11 Cr
- FY25 Net Worth ₹49.25 Cr; debt modest (₹10 Cr to be repaid from IPO)
- Capital Employed ≈ ₹60 Cr
- **Reported RoCE: 29.12%** | **Reported RoE: 39.11%**
- **Incremental RoCE FY23→FY25:** ΔPAT ≈ ₹10 Cr / ΔCapital ≈ ₹28 Cr ≈ **36%**

**Why this is misleading:**

The incremental ROIC of ~36% is computed on *accounting* PAT and *accounting* capital. The reality:
1. **Trade receivables grew roughly 4x** between FY23 and FY25, while revenue grew ~1.7x. Receivable days have stretched from a normal level to a stressed level.
2. **Operating cash flow was negative in FY24 and FY25**, despite PAT rising. This means the entire ₹10 Cr+ of incremental "profit" did not become cash; it became more receivables and inventory.
3. **The implied cash ROIC is much lower** — possibly close to zero or negative, since the business is consuming cash rather than generating it.

**This is structural, not cyclical.** It is structural because:
- Customer concentration → top customer dictates payment terms → receivable days extend
- Project-based business → revenue is lumpy and recognised on milestones, but cash arrives later → working-capital lag is intrinsic
- Material cost is a high % of project cost → inventory must be pre-built → working capital tied up before revenue

A 30%+ accounting RoCE that does not produce cash is not a compounding engine. It is a working-capital trap.

### Q2. Reinvestment runway?

The IPO will fund a new factory (₹41.10 Cr), debt repayment (₹10 Cr), and working capital (₹10 Cr). The new factory increases capacity. It does not change the customer mix, the IP, the certifications, or the cash conversion problem. The runway, narrowly defined as "can Adisoft find more projects to bid on," is real — the addressable industrial automation spend in India is large. But the runway broadly defined as "can Adisoft compound capital at 20%+ for 7–15 years" requires a fundamentally different business model.

### Q3. Why now?

The DRHP claims diversification and capacity expansion as the strategic rationale. The market reality is more prosaic: SME IPO valuations have been generous (FY25 saw multiple Patil-class listings at 25–30x P/E) and a partnership-converted-to-public-limited-three-weeks-before-filing is the textbook profile of a promoter cashing in on a friendly tape. This is not necessarily disqualifying on its own, but combined with the other concerns it changes the prior on management intent.

## 5. Outlook

**Industry:** Industrial automation in India is a real growth sector (high single digits to low teens CAGR) driven by:
- Auto OEM re-tooling for EV transition (battery pack assembly, e-axle assembly are physically different from ICE)
- PLI scheme spillovers (electronics, white goods, auto components driving capex)
- Make in India / Atmanirbhar Bharat → defence and aerospace component manufacturers automating
- Rising labour costs making automation ROI shorter

**Adisoft's ability to capture this:** Limited. The company has neither the IP-led differentiation of an ABB / Siemens nor the scale of a larger listed peer. It is a regional Tier-2 integrator dependent on relationships with auto OEMs in the Pune cluster.

**Likely 3-year trajectory (analyst view):**
- Revenue CAGR of 12–18% (slower than reported FY23–FY25 trajectory due to base effect and concentration)
- Margin compression as the company tries to acquire non-auto customers (lower-margin entry pricing)
- Working capital crunch likely visible in FY26 or FY27 if growth slows
- A potential minor crisis or governance event at some point in the first 24 months of listing — historical SME pattern
