## Summary Verdict

### Why this business?

Rain Industries is the world's number-two producer of calcined petroleum coke (CPC) and the world's largest merchant producer of coal tar pitch (CTP) — the two carbon inputs that go into the anode block every aluminium smelter consumes to make metal. About 73% of revenue sits in this Carbon segment; the rest is split between Advanced Materials (resins and downstream chemicals from coal tar) and a small South India-focused Cement business. Every tonne of primary aluminium needs roughly 0.4 tonnes of CPC and 0.1 tonnes of CTP — there is no substitute in the Hall-Héroult process — so Rain's business is, at the bedrock, a derivative bet on aluminium production volumes outside China. That demand is genuinely growing as Indonesia, the Gulf, and India build new smelters to replace ageing European capacity that's been killed by power costs. The bet on Rain is that the 2022-2024 cycle low is in the rear-view and the carbon business returns to its 12-15% EBITDA margin range while net debt comes down, leaving today's depressed earnings looking like trough.

### Strengths

- **Genuinely dominant position in a critical, irreplaceable input.** Rain Carbon is described in its own marketing as "the leading, single-point, global supplier of CPC and CTP" — and it operates 13 plants across 8 countries on 3 continents, with both CPC kilns and CTP distillation. No Indian competitor has anything close to this footprint. Closest pure-play peer Goa Carbon has ₹349 Cr market cap vs Rain's ₹6,629 Cr; closest US/global peers are private (Oxbow, Aminco).
- **Cycle recovery is genuinely visible in the prints, not just guidance.** EBITDA margin printed 14% in Q2 2025, 14% in Q3, 12% in Q4, 15% in Q1 2026 — versus 6-10% through 2024. Q1 2026 PAT swung to +₹158 Cr from −₹115 Cr in Q1 2025. Net-debt/EBITDA fell from 3.9x to 3.2x year-on-year. India CPC plants are running at "optimum capacity following import relaxations" per Q1 2026 commentary.
- **Promoter behaviour at this point of the cycle is constructive.** Promoter holding 41.35% as of March 2026 with **0% pledge** — a meaningful change from the historical pattern, where Jagan Mohan Reddy had at one point pledged a major portion of his stake. Promoter group entities Sujala Investments and Nivee Holdings made small open-market buys in March 2026. Not a "loading the truck" signal, but it's the right direction for a stock the market has spent three years writing off.
- **No major debt wall until 2028.** Management has stated there are no significant term debt maturities until 2028, which removes the most acute refinancing risk in an environment where the business is still rebuilding earnings power.
- **Pre-existing physical capability that the thesis depends on:** 2.4 million tonnes per annum of CPC capacity across India and the US, 1.1 million tonnes of CTP distillation in Belgium/Canada, 1.3 million tonnes of cement capacity in Andhra Pradesh, plus the CARBORES, CARBORES-P, LIONCOAT specialty chemicals know-how that drives the Advanced Materials segment. None of this depends on management building something new — it's all built and operating.

### Concerns

- **The balance sheet is still the load-bearing risk.** Borrowings of ₹8,494 Cr against equity of ₹6,637 Cr (debt/equity ~1.28x), net debt/EBITDA 3.2x, interest coverage 1.5x — these are the numbers that killed the company's operating flexibility through 2022-2024. The 2025 recovery has improved them but not fixed them. If aluminium demand softens or GPC (raw material) availability tightens before debt comes down further, the same cycle dynamic repeats. The framework's Phase 0.2 capital structure threshold (D/E < 1.5x, interest coverage > 3x) explicitly fails here. Rain has been over this line for years — but it remains a live risk, not a healed one.
- **Cycle position is no longer cheap.** The stock is up 100% from January and 38% in three months. RSI 74.8, MFI 85.7 — both well above the framework's "phase your entry" threshold of 80/70. Price is 45% above the 200-day SMA of ₹135.9. New 52-week high last week. Whatever the cycle does next, the easy money on the recovery print has been made. The market is now pricing in margin sustainability, not surprise.
- **Cyclical, commodity, single-end-market exposure.** 73% of revenue is calcined petroleum coke and coal tar pitch sold into aluminium smelters. Aluminium itself is cyclical; CPC supply is cyclical; GPC raw-material availability is cyclical. The business runs at 8% ROCE and 0.6% ROE at the bottom of its three-year window. Five-year revenue CAGR is 10%; five-year profit CAGR is **−40%**. This is not a compounder by any honest reading.
- **The "AI/semiconductor exposure" is mostly storytelling.** The only AI/semi-adjacent angle is a $2.2 million, 24-month R&D joint development agreement with Northern Graphite (announced October 2024) for natural-graphite battery anode material — venture-stage optionality on a tiny base, no revenue today, no production commitment. Rain does not make semiconductor-grade synthetic graphite. The user is right to be cautious about this framing — see Deep Dive A below for the full debunk.
- **Three-year track record argues against multi-bagger framing.** Net profit was −₹450 Cr in calendar 2024. ROE has been negative on a 3-year average. EPS is barely positive on TTM basis. A multi-bagger needs sustained 20%+ ROIC; Rain has earned that intermittently across cycles, but the cycle volatility is structural to the input-commodity business model.

### How does this actually compound? (one-line summary; full breakdown in Section 3 below)

It doesn't compound in the classical sense — it cycles. Profit growth from here is a recovery from a depressed base to normalised mid-cycle earnings (₹600-900 Cr PAT, my estimate), not a sustained reinvestment-driven compound at 20% per year. The "math" is closer to 2-3x earnings recovery from 2024's loss, plus debt paydown, plus a possible 0.5x-to-1.0x P/B re-rating — not a 25% compounding engine. See Section 3 for the full ROIC walk-through.

### Second-Order Stress Test

**5-Whys on the ROIC engine:**

> Why does this business earn high returns (when it does)?
> 1. Because Rain controls a large share of global merchant CPC and CTP supply, which gives it pricing leverage when aluminium smelters need anode inputs.
> 2. Why does Rain have that supply share? → Because it spent two decades acquiring CPC kilns (CII Carbon 2007 — the foundational deal) and CTP distillation (RÜTGERS 2013) at scale, and the integration of upstream calcining with downstream advanced-materials chemistry is hard to replicate.
> 3. Why is that integration hard to replicate? → Because both CPC and CTP plants are capital-intensive (₹1,000-2,000 Cr per major plant), permitted under increasingly tight emissions regulation, and require decades-long relationships with green petroleum coke suppliers (the raw material — itself a refinery byproduct in shrinking supply as refining shifts).
> 4. Why is GPC supply shrinking? → Because GPC is a residual product of high-sulphur crude refining, and refinery configurations are changing as crude slates shift and emissions rules tighten. New CPC capacity outside China is therefore structurally constrained.
> 5. Why is THAT structural? → Because it is a function of global oil-refining economics and the slow pace of regulatory change in petroleum coke handling — neither of which Rain controls, but both of which benefit the existing licensed asset base.

The fifth answer points to something structural (refining economics + emissions regulation as supply governor) rather than something Rain controls. So the moat is real — but it is a *constrained-supply* moat, not a *growing-demand* moat. Rain doesn't get richer faster; it gets more pricing power per tonne when CPC supply tightens. That's a different kind of compounding — closer to a regulated utility with cyclical pricing than to a true high-ROIC compounder.

**The world in 2 years if base case plays out:**

By calendar 2027, Rain looks like a 14-15% EBITDA-margin business with ₹17,500-18,000 Cr revenue, ₹2,500-2,700 Cr EBITDA, PAT in the ₹700-900 Cr range, and net debt/EBITDA around 2.3x as cash flow chips at borrowings. The Cement segment expansion from 1.3 to 3.8 million tonnes (Q4 2027 completion target) is approaching but not yet contributing. The Northern Graphite battery-anode joint venture is exiting R&D phase, with a small revenue contribution starting. Management is talking about either a fresh capex round or a possible inorganic deal — both of which are risks in a debt-encumbered company. Stock is probably ₹230-280 in that base case.

**What success itself triggers:**

Cycle peaks bring new competition. As CPC margins normalise above 12%, idle Chinese capacity comes back online and pressures global pricing — China is the swing producer Rain has spent the last decade fighting. New capex announcements by Rain itself to capture cycle peak will re-leverage the balance sheet exactly when investors want to see deleveraging. And valuation expectations ratchet up — at ₹250, the market will start demanding sustainably positive ROE and quarterly EBITDA above 14%, both of which are far from guaranteed in a commodity business.

## Deep Dives

### A. The "AI / semiconductor proxy" claim — what's actually there and what isn't

Rain Industries gets occasional mention in retail discussion as an "AI play" or "semiconductor exposure" because (a) it makes carbon products, and (b) graphite, also a carbon product, has semiconductor and AI applications. Both are true in isolation. They don't make Rain an AI play.

What Rain actually makes in the Carbon segment (73% of revenue): **calcined petroleum coke** and **coal tar pitch**. CPC is a high-carbon material produced by calcining (heating to ~1,300°C) green petroleum coke, a refinery byproduct. CTP is a residue from distilling coal tar (a coke-oven byproduct from steelmaking). Both go into one specific end-use: the **anode block** that aluminium smelters consume to make primary aluminium. The Hall-Héroult electrolysis process passes electric current through molten alumina via a carbon anode, which is consumed at a rate of roughly 0.4 tonnes of CPC + 0.1 tonnes of CTP per tonne of aluminium produced. That's the business — supplying the consumable carbon block to aluminium smelters.

Neither CPC nor CTP has a semiconductor or AI application. They are not graphite. Graphite is a separate carbon material with a crystalline structure and electrical/thermal properties that CPC and CTP do not have. The companies that benefit from AI-driven semiconductor demand for graphite are **isostatic synthetic graphite** producers (mostly Japanese — Toyo Tanso, IBIDEN, Tokai Carbon) selling ultra-high-purity graphite into wafer fabrication, EUV photomask blanks, and CVD equipment. The Indian-listed proxies for that demand are HEG and Graphite India — and even there, the bulk of their business is graphite *electrodes* for electric-arc-furnace steelmaking, not semiconductor-grade graphite.

What Rain has that's adjacent to the AI/EV narrative is a **joint development agreement with Northern Graphite Corporation** (Canadian junior miner), announced October 2024, for a **24-month, $2.2 million R&D project** to develop battery-grade anode material from natural graphite fines. This is venture-stage R&D, not production capacity. It's optional value on a tiny base. If the technology works, scales, and finds customers, it could become a meaningful Advanced Materials revenue line in 4-6 years. In June 2026, it contributes zero revenue and is not material to the thesis.

If a user wants AI/semiconductor exposure from India: HEG (some), Graphite India (some), or more directly via global chip designers and equipment makers. Rain is not the right vehicle for that thesis.

### B. The cement segment expansion — what it adds and what it costs

Rain has a 1.3 MTPA cement business in South India that contributes about 6% of revenue. Management announced a brownfield expansion to take this to 3.8 MTPA — a 2.5x volume jump — at a project cost of ₹757 Cr, with Q4 2027 completion targeted. Waste heat recovery is included, which improves power economics in a sector where fuel is the largest variable cost.

The honest framing: cement at 3.8 MTPA is still a small player by Indian cement standards (the top five players have 20-100 MTPA each). It does not turn Rain into a cement company. It does, however, deploy ₹757 Cr at a moment when the balance sheet still carries 3.2x net-debt/EBITDA. The expansion is part-funded from internal accruals, but the timing is a tell — management is choosing capex over faster deleveraging, which is a small negative on the conservative-balance-sheet thesis.

If executed well, cement EBITDA could rise from current ~₹100 Cr to ₹250-300 Cr by 2028. That's a 5-7% incremental contribution to consolidated EBITDA — material but not transformational. It does not change the fact that this is a Carbon company first.

## 1. Business Summary

The Carbon segment, run through Rain Carbon, produces calcined petroleum coke (CPC), coal tar pitch (CTP), and a range of downstream advanced materials (CARBORES resins, naphthalene derivatives, LIONCOAT battery-anode coatings) from CPC and CTP feedstocks. ## 3. Compounding Engine — Q&A

### Q0. Operating Leverage Test — does growth create margin leverage?

- Revenue growth (TTM vs 3yr avg): +9%
- EBITDA margin now (Q1 2026): 15% vs 3 years ago (Q1 2023): ~9% → +600 bps
- **Operating leverage present?** Yes — at the segment level, margins have expanded faster than revenue. But this is cyclical recovery from a depressed base, not structural leverage on a growing fixed-cost asset. Pre-2022 the same margins were achieved at the same revenue.
- Capacity utilization: Indian CPC plants at "optimum capacity" per Q1 2026 commentary; global CPC/CTP estimated 80-90%
- CWIP trend: ₹931 Cr (2020) → ₹473 Cr (2024) — **declining**. Capex cycle is at trough, not building. Cement expansion will rebuild CWIP from 2026 onwards.

This is operating leverage of the cyclical-recovery flavour, not the compounder flavour. Reading too much into it as a sustained margin uplift would be a mistake.

### Q1. Is incremental ROIC genuinely high (>20%) — and is it structural, not cyclical?

No — at least not consistently. Headline ROCE is 8.3%, ROE 0.6%, both below cost of capital. Over the 10-year window, ROCE has cycled between roughly 4% (trough years 2023-24) and 18-20% (peak years 2017-18). The peak-cycle ROCE clears 20%; the average through-cycle ROCE is closer to 11-13%. That is below the framework's 20%+ structural ROIC threshold.

The math: incremental NOPAT in CY2025 was approximately ₹600 Cr (recovery from -₹450 Cr to ~₹150 Cr earnings + tax shield on debt service). Incremental capital employed was approximately flat. So incremental ROIC on this recovery is very high in arithmetic terms — but that's because the denominator hasn't moved. The capital was already deployed at scale years ago; what's changed is the price Rain is getting for its output.

This is **not** the high-ROIC compounder math the framework was built to evaluate. It's the operating-leverage-on-existing-capacity math of a cyclical commodity business. The honest answer to Q1 is "no, the through-cycle ROIC is mediocre."

### Q2. How much capital can be redeployed at these returns — what's the reinvestment runway?

The reinvestment opportunity is constrained. The global CPC/CTP industry is mature; new capacity is hard to permit; demand growth from non-China aluminium expansion is real but slow (3-4% CAGR globally). Rain's stated capex pipeline is:

- Cement expansion 1.3 → 3.8 MTPA: ₹757 Cr, completion Q4 2027
- Northern Graphite anode-material JDA: $2.2M R&D, optional small commercialisation capex if successful
- Maintenance capex on existing carbon assets: ₹250-350 Cr/year

Total deployable capital over the next 3-4 years is in the order of ₹1,500-2,000 Cr — about 25% of current market cap. At a mid-cycle ROIC of 12%, that adds about ₹200 Cr to annual EBITDA by 2028 — meaningful but not transformative.

The structural answer: this is not a business with a long reinvestment runway at high incremental ROIC. It's a business with constrained reinvestment opportunities at moderate cyclical ROIC. The framework's runway test fails.

### Q3. What's the implied compounding math?

If we ignore the cycle and just take through-cycle averages:
- Through-cycle ROIC: ~12%
- Through-cycle reinvestment rate: ~40% (the rest goes to debt service)
- Implied intrinsic value compound: 12% × 40% = **4.8% per year**

That's the honest answer. From a depressed base, the recovery to mid-cycle earnings can deliver one-time appreciation of 50-100% in 18-24 months — and that's what's largely already happened from ₹100 to ₹197. Beyond mid-cycle, the compound rate of intrinsic value is roughly 5% per year. No multi-bagger math works here without the explicit assumption that you're trading the cycle, not owning the compound.

**Smell test:** This is not a 25%+ CAGR business. Anyone marketing it as one is using cherry-picked peak-cycle earnings as the baseline.

### Q4. What breaks the thesis?

**The compounding (such as it is) breaks if:** Chinese CPC capacity restarts at scale, driving global pricing back below trough levels — Rain's interest coverage collapses below 1x, and the debt covenant constraints force asset sales or equity dilution.

**Leading indicator I'd see before the stock price tells me:** Global CPC prices reported in Argus or CRU benchmarks — these are public, lag spot by 30-45 days but lead Rain's quarterly earnings by 60-90 days. A 15%+ drop in benchmark CPC pricing for two consecutive months is the warning.

### Q5. Can I hold through a 40% drawdown? What's my conviction anchor?

If we held this, the anchor would be:

> "Rain's CPC and CTP capacity is irreplaceable inputs for global aluminium production. At normalised mid-cycle EBITDA, the business generates ₹2,500-2,700 Cr/year — which makes the enterprise value at any price below ₹6,000 Cr cheap relative to the asset's replacement cost. Even in a deep drawdown, the question is *when* mid-cycle returns, not *whether*."

But honestly — for a Grade C cyclical at the top of the cycle, the answer to "can I hold through a 40% drawdown" is "probably not, because the next cycle bottom is 2-3 years away and the opportunity cost is too high." Position size, if held at all, should be capped at 1.5-2% — speculative-position size — and the willingness to exit on a thesis-break signal needs to be hard-wired.

## 6. Competitive Landscape

### Market Position

Rain Carbon is one of two global majors in merchant CPC (the other large player is Oxbow Group, privately held). It is the largest merchant producer of coal tar pitch globally after the RÜTGERS acquisition in 2013 gave it Belgian and Canadian distillation capacity. Adjacent Indian peers in carbon products: HEG and Graphite India in graphite electrodes — different product, same end customer (steel and aluminium).

### Structural Advantages vs Peers

## Glossary — Key Industry Terms

**Calcined Petroleum Coke (CPC)**
A high-carbon material produced by heating "green" petroleum coke — a byproduct from oil refining — to around 1,300°C. The calcination process drives off volatile compounds and produces a dense, high-purity carbon that becomes one of the main raw materials for the carbon anode used in aluminium smelting. About 0.4 tonnes of CPC are consumed per tonne of primary aluminium produced.

**Coal Tar Pitch (CTP)**
A residue from distilling coal tar, which is itself a byproduct of coke production from coking coal in steelmaking. CTP acts as the binder that holds CPC particles together in the anode block. About 0.1 tonnes of CTP per tonne of aluminium.

**Green Petroleum Coke (GPC)**
The raw, un-calcined coke produced as a byproduct of high-sulphur crude oil refining. GPC is the feedstock for CPC. Its global supply depends on refinery configurations and crude slates — both of which are slowly changing as emissions rules tighten, making GPC supply structurally constrained over the long term.

**Hall-Héroult Process**
The electrolytic process used to produce primary aluminium since the 1880s. Alumina dissolved in molten cryolite is electrolysed to produce aluminium metal. The process requires carbon anodes, which are consumed during electrolysis — about 0.5 tonnes of carbon anode per tonne of aluminium produced. CPC and CTP are the raw materials for these anodes.

**Anode block / Carbon anode**
The consumable carbon electrode used in aluminium smelting. Typical composition: 67% CPC, 20% recycled anode butts, 13% CTP. Smelters either make their own anodes (integrated smelters) or buy them from standalone anode producers. Either way, CPC and CTP are the irreplaceable inputs.

**ELYSIS**
A joint venture between Rio Tinto and Alcoa developing an inert-anode technology for aluminium smelting that would eliminate the need for carbon anodes (and therefore CPC and CTP). Commercial deployment is still years away (current targets: late 2020s for first commercial-scale demonstration). Long-term existential risk to Rain's Carbon segment, but not a near-term threat.

**Graphite Electrodes (for context — not Rain's business)**
A different carbon product used in electric arc furnace (EAF) steelmaking, not aluminium. Made from needle coke, calcined coke, and pitch via a different manufacturing process (extrusion, baking, graphitisation). Indian producers: HEG, Graphite India. Often confused with Rain's CPC business by retail investors; the products and end markets are distinct.

**LIONCOAT**
Rain Carbon's proprietary coating technology for battery anode materials. Currently in R&D phase via the Northern Graphite JDA. Aims to convert low-value graphite fines into battery-grade anode material via specialty coating chemistry.

**CARBORES / Naphthalene derivatives**
Specialty hydrocarbon resins and chemicals produced from coal tar fractions. Used in tires, construction chemicals, water-reducing agents in concrete. Part of Rain's Advanced Materials segment.
