## May 24, 2026 update — Day 85: Waiver expired, MOU converging, ceasefire-extension signal

*Added May 24, 2026. Five days after the v3 base draft. The base case is being tested in both directions inside the same week.*

### The Russia waiver actually expired

The single highest-information event of the next 90 days has now happened, and the way it happened is itself information. Through May 12-13, Treasury Secretary Bessent publicly said the waiver would not be renewed. Within 48 hours Treasury posted a renewed licence on its website, effectively contradicting him. Inside another 48 hours, on May 16, the licence was allowed to lapse without further extension. India had formally asked for the extension on May 14, citing energy-security risk from a continued Hormuz blockade — the request was acknowledged and not granted.

Operationally India is now in the worst version of the base case. Indian refiners can no longer book new Russian cargoes without taking on secondary-sanctions risk on the financing and shipping side. The May 2.3 mbpd run-rate is a ceiling, not a floor, from here. The Brent-Dubai spread that v3 flagged as the early-warning indicator has begun to widen — Dubai is now trading at roughly a $1.50 premium to Brent, up from near-parity on May 18. Two more weeks at this rate and the re-inversion v3 named as the INR-break-below-97 signal will have happened. The INR has not waited for the spread; it printed an all-time low of 96.96 on May 22, three large figures past the v3 base-case range. The de-facto break of 97 happens on any day this week that crude moves up.

### The 14-point MOU is converging in a way it wasn't on May 19

This is the surprising part. On May 23, Iranian Foreign Ministry spokesperson Baghaei said publicly that Iranian and US positions on the 14-point memorandum are "converging." That language is different from anything Tehran said in April or early May, and matters because it was issued in Persian first — domestic audience — not just English for Western press. Two specific elements have firmed up: a uranium enrichment moratorium being negotiated at 12-15 years (three sources at 12, one at 15 per Axios reporting on May 6 that has now been corroborated by PressTV on May 23), and an enhanced inspections regime including snap inspections by IAEA. The frozen-asset release tranche has reportedly grown to $6-10B in the first phase.

Separately, Indian and global wires are reporting that a **60-day ceasefire extension** is close to being signed. The Business Standard wrap on May 22 explicitly framed Nifty's small gain that session — 23,719, up 0.27% — as a response to "traders monitoring US-Iran diplomatic talks" with the Hormuz reopening as the explicit signing condition. This is the de-escalation lane that v3 put at 20%, and the probability has clearly moved up in the last five days — though not yet enough to reprice oil. Brent climbed above $105 on May 22 even as the diplomatic signal strengthened, because the Russia-waiver expiry is the dominating near-term shock and the MOU has not actually been signed.

### Iran-Oman Hormuz toll framework — the structural twist Trump rejected

The piece worth filing carefully is that Iran is working with Oman on a framework for a permanent toll system that would formalise Iranian control over Hormuz traffic in exchange for guaranteed open passage. Trump rejected this — "free and without toll charges" — but the proposal exists and Oman is mediating it, which is exactly the channel v3 named as the underweighted diplomatic vector. The toll framework is structurally what the v1 "Iran wants control over Hormuz" sticking point would look like if it were dressed in commercial clothing. The fact that Iran is willing to convert the demand into a paid-passage model is itself a softening; the fact that Trump rejected the specific structure is not the same as rejecting the underlying trade.

## What changed since v2

The April 27 read held up for about ten days and then broke in three places at once. The frozen-conflict equilibrium we expected — Hormuz semi-open, oil at $100-110, ceasefire technically in force — held the broad shape but lost the only piece that made it tolerable for India: the assumption that Russian crude would keep flowing as the silent shock absorber. That assumption is now expiring on a calendar date.

The single most consequential development of the last four weeks is the **May 16 deadline on India's Russian oil exemption**. On February 6, the US executive order that cut India's tariff from 50% to 18% was accompanied by a White House line claiming India had committed to stop importing Russian oil. The joint India-US trade statement never repeated that commitment, and India kept buying — quietly first, then publicly. In May, Indian refineries pulled a record 2.3 million barrels per day from Russia. That worked because the US allowed a sanctions exemption. The exemption is conditional and expires this week; non-renewal forces Indian refiners onto US, West African, and Azerbaijani crude at roughly $15-25/bbl more than the Russian discount. The Moscow Times reported on March 7 that India publicly rejected the idea it needed US permission to buy from Russia, but the operational reality is that without the exemption the secondary-sanctions risk on Indian banks and shippers is real. This is the single largest variable for the next two months. v2 flagged the Feb 2 deal as a "critical blind spot" and was directionally right; it was also too pessimistic, because Modi's government held the door open through Q1 by simply refusing to convert the verbal commitment into action. That tactic runs out on May 16.

The second large development is the **UAE's exit from OPEC and OPEC+ effective May 1**. The UAE was OPEC's third-largest producer and the cartel's most reliable swing barrel beyond Saudi Arabia. The Atlantic Council framed the exit as the UAE concluding that the war "changed everything" — Iran had targeted the UAE more than any other GCC member, and OPEC's response was to coordinate symbolic production hikes that did nothing for prices. The Chatham House piece on Saudi strategy adds the other half of the story: Riyadh now views Israel as a regional threat and is irritated by Abu Dhabi's alignment with it. OPEC+ has now agreed to a 188 kbpd June increase, which Al Jazeera correctly called "symbolic." The cartel that mattered in 1973 and 2014 no longer exists in the same form. Saudi Arabia is the residual producer of last resort, and Trump's primary lever for getting more barrels to market is now a single phone call to Riyadh rather than a multilateral coordination.

The third is that **talks have stalled in a way that's different from April's stall**. The Islamabad collapse on April 12 was about three irreconcilable items — nuclear, Lebanon, Hormuz. The May version of the deal has narrowed to a one-page, 14-point memorandum that would lift Hormuz restrictions in exchange for an Iranian moratorium on enrichment, paired with US sanctions relief and frozen-asset release. Both sides briefly looked close on May 7-8 — that's why Brent dropped to $100.06 on May 7 and Indian markets staged a small rally. On May 10, Trump posted on Truth Social that Iran's counter-proposal was "totally unacceptable," with Tehran demanding war reparations, sovereignty over Hormuz, and immediate sanctions removal. The talks didn't formally collapse this time. They just stopped moving. Pezeshkian's "we will never bow our heads" statement was domestic theatre, but the underlying problem is that the US delegation cannot credibly offer meaningful sanctions relief without Congressional buy-in, and Iran cannot accept zero enrichment without losing the IRGC. This is the same Lebanon-shaped trap v2 identified, now wearing a different costume.

Operationally, **the Strait of Hormuz remains essentially closed**. Tanker transits are reportedly more than 95% below pre-war levels. The US launched a counter-blockade of Iranian ports on April 13 — Iran can't move oil out, the GCC can't move oil out, and the result is the "dual blockade" the Guardian described, where neither side can win and neither can exit. VLCC spot earnings hit $423,000-445,000/day on the MEG-China route at peak in March; they've eased but the structural premium remains. Brent has spent May in a $100-114 band rather than spiking to the $150 SocGen called possible in April, mainly because the US strategic petroleum reserve is now empty enough that traders know there's nothing left to deploy and have stopped front-running announcements. The market has priced the chronic disruption. What it has not yet priced is what happens when Indian refining margins reset on May 17.

Indian markets reflect this exactly. The Nifty closed around 23,589 on May 18, almost exactly where it sat on April 27. The eight weeks of headline-driven volatility netted out to a flat tape. The INR sits at 96.13 against the dollar, a fresh record low set this week, despite RBI burning down forex reserves from a February peak of $728.5B by more than $40B in four weeks. FII outflows now total more than $20B for calendar 2026. The CAD is tracking 1.7-2.5% of GDP, well off the v1 "doom loop" estimate of 3%+ — Russian discounts kept that number contained, which is exactly why the May 16 deadline matters.

## Three scenarios for the next 12-18 months

The right way to think about the next year is not as a continuation of v1/v2's scenario set but as a new probability tree that hinges on three near-term forks: the Russia exemption (May 16), the next negotiation cycle (likely late May into June), and whether OPEC produces a real Saudi response or just more symbolism. The four-scenario v1 framework has effectively collapsed into three states, because the "regime fracture" tail has gone nearly to zero — the IRGC has tightened control through 80 days of war without visible cracks, and that's now base-rate evidence.

**Base case — managed grind, around 55%.** The Russia exemption gets quietly extended in a face-saving form, possibly with Indian commitments to buy more US LNG or defence equipment as cover. Hormuz stays semi-permeable. Saudi Arabia delivers an additional 500-800 kbpd over the next 90 days under direct US pressure, which is enough to keep Brent in a $95-108 range without triggering a real cease-fire. Talks resume in June or July with another two-week ceasefire that becomes a 60-day ceasefire that nobody calls a peace. The Nifty drifts in a 23,000-24,500 band until Q1 FY27 earnings (mid-July to early August) quantify the actual damage to OMCs, paints, aviation, and downstream petrochem — that prints a brief trough of 22,500 or so before recovery begins. INR settles 94-96. CAD lands at 2.2-2.6% of FY27 GDP. This is the scenario you should plan around for sizing decisions; it's also the one with the least dramatic newsflow, which is partly why it's underpriced.

**Escalation — around 25%.** The Russia exemption is not extended, Indian refining margins compress sharply in late May, and an incident in Hormuz (most likely a tanker seizure, possibly a tanker actually struck rather than seized) forces a re-pricing. Brent moves into a $115-135 corridor, occasionally tagging $145 on intraday spikes. The Nifty does what v1 expected and prints 20,500-21,500 inside ten trading sessions, with airlines, paints and downstream OMCs leading the way down. INR breaches 98 and threatens 100. RBI raises rates 25-50bps in an emergency move, accepting the trade-off that growth slows in exchange for currency defence. The specific triggers that would push this from 25% to 50%: a US-flagged commercial vessel sunk or detained in the Gulf, a confirmed Israeli strike on Kharg Island, or an IAEA emergency statement on Iranian enrichment. None of these are improbable; the question is sequencing.

**De-escalation — around 20%.** The 14-point memorandum holds. Iran accepts an enrichment moratorium with face-saving carve-outs (medical isotopes continue, civilian power stations are unaffected), the US lifts sanctions on a phased schedule with the first tranche unfreezing $6-10B of Iranian assets, and Hormuz reopens to all flagged shipping by mid-June. Brent drops to $78-88 inside three weeks of the announcement. Nifty rallies 8-12% on the news and another 5% over six weeks as foreign flows return. INR strengthens to 91-93. The Russia question becomes moot because Middle Eastern crude is suddenly available again at OPEC's published prices. The trigger that would move this from 20% to 40%: a joint US-Iran communiqué naming Pakistan and Oman as guarantors and including a specific Hormuz reopening date — that's the structure Pakistan tried and failed to broker in April, and Oman's Foreign Ministry has been quietly holding deputy-level talks with Iran on Hormuz transit since late April.

The probability-weighted outcome from where the Nifty sits today is a small negative — somewhere around -3 to -5% over the next four months. That's not a market call so much as a recognition that the distribution is roughly symmetric around current levels, with fat tails on both sides, and you are not being paid much to take directional risk on the index itself. The money is in the dispersion.

## Oil price implications and where the spreads tell the truth

The headline Brent number — currently a hair above $102 — is the noisiest signal in this market. The spreads tell you what's actually happening underneath.

**Brent-Dubai** has been running at a discount through most of the war, which is the inverse of the historical pattern and is the clearest single indicator that the Asian buyer (read: China and India) is paying a premium for whatever Hormuz-origin barrels can still be moved. Pre-war the spread was Brent at roughly a $1-2 premium to Dubai; during the worst of the March-April disruption Dubai traded $3-5 above Brent. As of mid-May the spread is back near parity, which suggests Asian buyers have finally found enough non-Hormuz supply to ease the squeeze — but that easing is precisely what the Russia exemption supported, and is at risk this week. If the exemption lapses and Indian refiners switch to West African and US barrels, the Brent-Dubai spread should re-invert within ten days. That re-inversion is the cleanest early-warning signal for an INR break below 97.

**Futures curve shape** matters more than spot. In late February the curve was in modest backwardation — front-month above forward — reflecting near-term supply fear. The curve flipped to contango on the April 8 ceasefire announcement and has held there since, which is the market telling you it thinks the next twelve months will be no worse than today. A move back into deep backwardation (M1-M6 spread above $4) would be the futures market pricing escalation; the curve is currently 50-70 cents in contango, so any meaningful flip is a signal.

**The base case puts Brent at $95-108 averaged over June-August**, with intraday spikes to $115 on individual events that fade within 48 hours because the market has been trained by Trump's negotiation cycle to fade headline reactions. WTI tracks Brent at the usual $4-6 discount; the WTI-Brent spread is not the interesting signal here, the Brent-Dubai is. Escalation puts Brent at $115-135 averaged with $145 spikes; de-escalation puts it at $78-88. The asymmetry to watch is that escalation moves arrive in single sessions while de-escalation builds over weeks — markets price fear faster than relief, especially after this many cycles of failed talks.

The Trump-Saudi production lever sits underneath all of this. Riyadh's spare capacity is roughly 2.5 mbpd, of which it has indicated willingness to deploy 1.0-1.5 mbpd if pressed. That's enough to absorb a renewed Russia squeeze on India and to put $10-12 of downward pressure on Brent inside 60 days. The political question is whether MBS spends that capacity for Trump given Saudi's new posture toward Israel; the answer in May has been "yes, but slowly," which is roughly the rate this market needs.

## Indian stocks: who benefits, who gets hurt, and at what prices

The thumbnail rule is that the war creates two flows in Indian equities — a margin transfer from downstream to upstream within energy, and a cost shock to everything that uses crude derivatives or jet fuel. The defence story is real but is not actually a war trade; it's a separate structural re-rating that the war has accelerated. Treat them differently.

**Upstream beneficiaries.** ONGC closed around ₹297 on May 18. Every $10 sustained move in Brent adds roughly $1.5-1.8B to ONGC's annual realisations before tax, partially offset by the cess and any subsidy burden the government drops back on it. The subsidy-sharing risk is the single thing to watch — in 2008 and 2012 ONGC was made to absorb upstream subsidies that wiped out most of the windfall, and the Modi government has so far not signalled it'll do the same in this cycle, but a Nifty trough below 22,000 would create political pressure to reinstate it. The trade is to add to ONGC on any pullback below ₹285 as long as Brent stays above $90 and no subsidy-sharing announcement appears. OIL India is the smaller, higher-beta version of the same trade with slightly cleaner economics because its production is less dominated by ageing fields. GAIL is the cross-trade — it benefits from higher domestic gas prices but has a regulated transmission business and exposure to LNG imports that gets squeezed at high spot prices, so the net effect in this scenario is modestly positive rather than dramatically so.

**Refiners** are split. Reliance Industries' refining business has the flexibility to process the heavy sour grades that get discounted in disrupted markets, and its export franchise lets it capture European product cracks that have widened sharply. RIL is therefore a defensive name in this environment, not the canonical refiner-as-victim. The PSU oil marketing companies — IOC at ₹136, BPCL at ₹284, HPCL around ₹378 — are the structural losers, because they hold the retail price line at the pump for political reasons and absorb the cost of high crude as compressed marketing margins. Each $10 sustained move in Brent costs the OMC complex roughly ₹6,000-8,000 crore of quarterly EBITDA between the three of them. IOC at ₹136 is testing its 52-week low of ₹130; that's not a buy signal, it's confirmation that the market correctly thinks marketing margin compression continues into Q1 FY27 results. The trade is to avoid OMCs at any price until either the war ends or the government formally announces a subsidy-recovery mechanism. The cleaner short-the-sector vehicle is BPCL because its retail mix is the most exposed.

**Defence names.** BEL, HAL, BDL, and Solar Industries have all rallied through the war on order-book visibility and the structural India-defence-spend story rather than on direct war contracts. BEL's order book at ₹75,600 crore is roughly three years of revenue; BDL is targeting ₹20,000 crore of orders from a ₹50,000 crore pipeline; Solar's defence book is ₹15,500 crore. The Iran war is a tailwind to all four but not a thesis. The asymmetry is that if the war escalates, defence stocks get re-rated on multiple expansion (you're now paying for a higher growth trajectory and a higher terminal multiple), and if it de-escalates the structural story remains intact and the stocks hold. That's an attractive shape, but only if you're buying on pullbacks rather than chasing — these are not cheap names. I'd want a 10-12% drawdown in BEL or BDL specifically before adding, which historically tends to happen on any negative defence procurement headline or on broad-market risk-off days.

**Aviation.** Indigo at ₹4,315 is the cleanest single-stock exposure to the bad side of this trade. Jet fuel is 35-40% of operating costs, and Indigo has run a low-hedge book historically because the structural cost advantage of fleet age and turnaround time mattered more than fuel hedging when crude was averaging $75. At $105 crude the math reverses — the unhedged cost shock arrives in Q1 FY27 results around mid-July, and the market is going to find the numbers ugly. Indigo is not a short, because its competitive position remains intact and a de-escalation scenario rerates it sharply, but it's also not a position to size up here. The cleaner read is to wait until the July results print, watch whether the stock holds ₹4,000 on bad numbers, and treat that hold as the capitulation signal that fear has been priced.

**Paints.** Asian Paints at ₹2,266 and Berger at ₹435 have both come down through the war as the crude-derivative input shock fed through. The temptation is to call paints a defensive trade because the brand pricing power is real and the urban housing demand story hasn't broken. The reality is that paint margins compress with a one-quarter lag and recover with a two-quarter lag, so the worst is not yet in Q4 FY26 numbers — it shows up in Q1 FY27 and Q2 FY27. Asian Paints at sub-₹2,200 starts to look interesting on a 12-month view; at ₹2,266 it isn't compelling. Berger is the more leveraged play to the recovery side and is also the more painful on the way down.

**Tyres and downstream petchem** sit in the same bucket as paints, with slightly worse pricing power. The cleanest read here is to wait for the demand destruction to show up in quarterly numbers rather than try to time the input-cost cycle.

## What to actually do

The list of things worth doing in this market is shorter than the list of things worth watching, which is itself shorter than the list of headlines that will appear daily. Five concrete actions:

If Brent breaches $95 sustained for four weeks and the Russia exemption lapses, add 1-2% to ONGC on any pullback below ₹285, with the explicit kill condition being a Government of India notification on upstream subsidy-sharing. The trade size is small because the subsidy risk is real; the trade is asymmetric because if the subsidy doesn't come, the operational windfall is large and visible by Q2 FY27 results.

Hold whatever Indigo exposure you already have, but do not add at current levels. The actionable add comes only after Q1 FY27 results print in mid-July and the stock either holds ₹4,000 or breaks toward ₹3,700. The hold-at-₹4,000 outcome is the buy; the break is not, because it indicates the market still has fear left to price.

Avoid the PSU OMCs (IOC, BPCL, HPCL) entirely until either the war ends or the government announces a formal subsidy-recovery mechanism. The dividend yield is a trap in this scenario — the political economy of pump prices means the cash flow that funds the dividend is the variable being absorbed by the cost shock.

Defence names belong in the portfolio but should be added on pullbacks of 10%+ from current levels, not on breakouts or news-driven spikes. BEL on a sub-₹430 print, BDL below ₹1,650, Solar Industries below ₹13,500. If the pullback doesn't come — and in a continued grind it might not — the structural compounding case still works at higher prices, so missing the entry is a real but acceptable cost.

If the 14-point memorandum is formally signed and a Hormuz reopening date is named in a joint communiqué, deploy 4-6% of available cash into Indian financials and IT services within 48 hours of the announcement. This is the de-escalation trade and the only one where speed actually matters; everything else in this list can be done at leisure.

## What to ignore

The most expensive thing in a market like this is acting on narratives that look investable and aren't. Three patterns to filter out.

The "short-lived spike that reverts" trade. Every Hormuz incident headline triggers a 1.5-2% intraday move in oil and a 0.5-1% move in oil-linked Indian names. By the next session both have faded. The fade is not a buy or a sell signal; it's the market mechanically pricing a 48-hour half-life on geopolitical headlines that we documented in v2. Trading these moves generates commissions and confusion. Wait for sustained four-week breaks.

The "defensive paint" thesis. Two or three sell-side reports in April argued that paints were a defensive trade because brand pricing power would let them pass through input costs. The history says otherwise. Paint margin recovery lags input-cost recovery by 6-9 months, because category pricing is competitive and the dealers fight pass-through hardest when category demand is softening. The defensive frame is wrong; paints are mid-cycle cyclicals dressed in consumer-staple clothing.

The "regime fracture is coming" tail. v1 put this at 10-15% and v2 reduced it to 5-8%; the actual probability after 80 days of war with no visible crack in IRGC control is in the low single digits. Trades structured around an Iranian regime collapse — short oil, long EM consumer, long Iranian-asset-recovery proxies — are very long-tailed bets that will lose money for two years before they make money for one week. They don't belong in a portfolio sized for actual outcomes.

## Indicators dashboard

The signals worth watching, with the value or condition that flips the thesis. The frequency matters as much as the value — most of these need to hold for at least two weeks to count.

**Brent-Dubai spread.** Source: S&P Global Platts daily assessments, summarised in Reuters and Bloomberg energy markets coverage. Currently near parity. If Dubai sustains a $3+ premium to Brent for two weeks, the Asian supply squeeze is back and INR/oil-importer pressure intensifies — that's an escalation tell. If Brent moves to a $2+ premium to Dubai sustained, supply has normalised and the de-escalation scenario is starting to play.

**WTI-Brent spread.** Source: NYMEX/ICE settlements via Bloomberg. Currently $4-6 discount. Not as informative as Brent-Dubai in this cycle, but a Brent premium widening past $8 indicates the US is hoarding inventory at the expense of exports, which is a soft signal of Strategic Petroleum Reserve refill activity and indirectly a tell on Trump's tariff/sanctions appetite.

**Brent futures contango/backwardation.** Source: ICE Brent M1-M6 spread. Currently 50-70 cents contango. A move past $3 in backwardation prices in renewed war-premium and confirms escalation. A move past $2 in contango confirms the market thinks the disruption is permanent-but-bounded and prices in slow demand destruction.

**VLCC tanker spot rates (MEG-China TD3C route).** Source: Baltic Exchange daily, summarised in QC Intel and Lloyd's List. Peak hit $423-445K/day in March; eased since but still elevated versus the historical $30-60K norm. A break back above $300K/day means tanker capacity is being absorbed by re-routing — which signals Hormuz stays effectively closed. A move below $150K/day is the cleanest de-escalation indicator I know.

**US Navy 5th Fleet posture.** Source: US Naval Institute News, USNI.org daily updates, and CENTCOM press releases. The Fifth Fleet is based in Bahrain. Movement of a second carrier strike group into theatre is escalation; departure of any current asset is de-escalation. Names to watch: USS Eisenhower, USS Truman, USS Reagan, and the destroyer escort composition.

**IAEA director general statements on Iran.** Source: IAEA.org press centre and Reuters wire. The dog that hasn't barked. Any board statement or Director General report citing "unaccounted material" or "loss of continuity of knowledge" at Natanz or Fordow is the single largest single-event escalation trigger in this conflict.

**IRGC public statements.** Source: Tasnim News, Mehr News, and PressTV (state-aligned, but useful for reading official Iranian positioning). Specific language to track: "strategic patience" indicates de-escalation framing; "decisive response" is escalation framing; any reference to specific GCC assets or US bases by name is preparation for action.

**Saudi-Iran or Oman-mediated talks calendar.** Source: Oman News Agency, Saudi Press Agency, Al Jazeera regional desk. Oman's deputy-foreign-minister level talks with Iran on Hormuz transit are the most underweighted diplomatic channel right now. A move to ministerial level is the de-escalation tell most people are not watching.

**India Russian crude imports daily volume.** Source: Vortexa and Kpler shipping data, summarised weekly in The Hindu BusinessLine and Reuters India. May print of 2.3 mbpd is the high-water mark. A drop below 1.5 mbpd over a four-week rolling average is the market telling you the exemption lapsed in operational terms, regardless of what either government says publicly.

## Summary

The frozen-conflict equilibrium of v2 has held in form and broken in detail. The structural facts that mattered through April — Russian crude flowing to India, OPEC coordinating production, talks reachable in two-week windows — have each shifted in May. The Russia exemption deadline this week is the single highest-information event in the next 90 days. The probability tree has narrowed from four scenarios to three. The right portfolio posture is small additions to the upstream-beneficiary side on pullbacks, avoidance of PSU OMCs and unsized aviation exposure, a deliberate pause on paints until Q1 FY27 results, and dry powder held back for the de-escalation print that may or may not arrive in June. The dashboard above tells you when to deploy it.

The single most underappreciated risk is the same one v1 identified and got right: Russia's incentive to prolong this war is unchanged, and the May Russia-exemption deadline gives Moscow another point of leverage.
