Mangal Electrical makes the unglamorous middle of an Indian transformer: CRGO laminations and cores (the steel that becomes the magnetic heart of a transformer), then medium-voltage transformers up to 10 MVA, plus a small EPC business setting up substations. The interesting variable in the next two years is capacity — the CRGO line just went from 16,200 MTPA to 28,000 MTPA in late FY26, and a new transformer plant capable of 132 kV / 100 MVA is being built for Q3 FY27 commissioning. That puts MEIL into a higher-voltage product category where margins and customer profile both step up. The unglamorous parts are everything else. FY26 was the first reported full year after the August 2025 IPO and it printed lower PAT than FY25 (₹43.2 Cr vs ₹47.3 Cr), three of the four post-IPO quarters have been at 10-14% operating margins versus the 15-18% the company showed pre-listing, working capital days ballooned from 119 to 225 in twelve months, and operating cash flow swung from positive ₹37 Cr (FY24) to negative ₹101 Cr (FY26). The headline 5-year profit CAGR of 109% on Screener is real arithmetic — but the FY20-21 base was ₹1-3 Cr of profit on a company that hadn't yet got the CRGO line running, so the math is dominated by the move out of a near-zero base rather than the rate the business compounds at scale.

The bet, if you're willing to take it, is that the FY27 capex absorption mirrors the FY23-25 ramp where revenue went from ₹354 Cr to ₹549 Cr at expanding margins. The bet against is that FY26 already revealed the problem — capacity built ahead of demand, working capital absorbing the cash that should have been profit, transformer realisations under pressure from competitive bids on PSU tenders, and CRGO realisations down 20% YoY in Q3 FY26 because the underlying steel price corrected. At ₹302 you're paying about 19x for a business whose forward earnings are uncertain in direction, not just in magnitude. Below ₹260 the math starts to work even if margins only stabilise at 13%; below ₹220 it works on the pre-IPO normalised margin band. The right way to play it is to wait for the next two quarters of evidence and size small even then.

## Summary Verdict

### Why this business?

MEIL is an integrated transformer-components player — they buy CRGO steel (the silicon-iron alloy used to build the magnetic core of every transformer), slit it, stamp it into laminations, wind it into cores, and either sell those cores to other transformer manufacturers (BHEL, Siemens, CG Power, Voltamp are named customers) or assemble them into their own medium-voltage transformers up to 10 MVA. The CRGO and components business is about 70% of revenue, in-house transformers about 23%, and substation EPC about 7%. Where this gets interesting is the timing — India's transmission and distribution build-out is genuinely large (state utilities and Power Grid have planned roughly ₹9 lakh Cr of T&D investment through FY32 under the RDSS and integrated grid programs, with replacement transformer demand alone running into hundreds of thousands of units), and MEIL just doubled its CRGO processing capacity in early FY26 and is mid-way through a transformer plant upgrade to 132 kV / 100 MVA that will let it bid on much larger orders. The question is whether the demand catches up with the supply they've just added, or whether they've front-run the cycle and the next two years look like working-capital pain rather than operating leverage.

### Strengths

- **The CRGO capacity expansion is built and operating.** Annual processing capacity stepped from 16,200 MTPA to 28,000 MTPA during FY26 (per the Q3 FY26 disclosure), already up and running rather than CWIP — meaning the new revenue is gated by demand, not by execution. This is the harder-to-replicate piece of the business — CRGO processing requires precision slitting and lamination equipment plus NABL-certified testing, and India produces only 50,000 tonnes of CRGO domestically against 400,000 tonnes of demand (per GTRI data via Business Standard, Oct 2024). The structural shortage and 30% import gap is real, even though the immediate FY26 quarter is showing realisation pressure.
- **IPO cleaned up the balance sheet cleanly.** Borrowings ₹149 Cr (FY25) → ₹45 Cr (FY26), of which only ₹2 Cr is non-current. Reserves ₹199 Cr → ₹563 Cr. ₹90.66 Cr of unutilised IPO proceeds parked in HDFC Bank fixed deposits per the May 2026 board disclosure — that's roughly 11% of market cap sitting in cash, providing a real floor under the valuation. The CARE Ratings monitoring agency reported "no material deviations" in fund usage.
- **Customer roster is real, even if concentrated.** Named customers include BHEL, Siemens, Voltamp, Crompton Greaves, NTPC, PGCIL, Adani, ReNew Power, AVADA, Shirdi Sai Electrical, and TRIL — a mix of central PSUs, IPP-segment buyers, and listed transformer OEMs. The IPO disclosure says top 10 customers contribute roughly 50% of revenue, which is concentration but not single-customer-dependent.
- **Operating in a regulator-blessed segment.** RDSS has allocated ₹12,585 Cr in FY24-25 alone explicitly for distribution transformer upgrades (61% of the Ministry of Power's budget per industry trade press). India is moving from 277 GW peak demand in FY26 toward a projected 366 GW by FY32 (CEA estimates) — every additional GW requires distribution and step-down transformers somewhere in the chain. The category demand is structural; the question is who captures the order flow.
- **Promoter ownership is high.** 74.79% promoter holding with no pledge disclosed. Family business with Rahul Mangal as MD (35+ years in the industry per the IPO note) and three other family members on the board.

### Concerns

- **The 5-year compounding number is a base effect.** Screener shows 5y profit CAGR of 109% on FY21 PAT of ₹1 Cr — mathematically that's only meaningful as evidence that the company crossed from zero to scale, not that it compounds at 109%. Strip out FY20-FY22 and the FY23-FY26 profit goes ₹25 → ₹21 → ₹47 → ₹43 — a four-year window where profit is roughly flat at ₹40-45 Cr with one good year in between. The headline metric a screener-based investor sees is misleading.
- **FY26 PAT *fell* vs FY25 — first reported year as a listed company.** ₹47.3 Cr → ₹43.2 Cr (-8.7%) on revenue growth of +5.5%. For a company that listed at 24x trailing P/E (FY25 PAT) and pitched a 28% revenue CAGR story, printing a profit decline in the very first reported year is the kind of thing that breaks the IPO narrative. The 46% drop from issue price is the market doing exactly that.
- **Cash conversion is genuinely broken.** Five-year cumulative OCF is roughly ₹-78 Cr against five-year cumulative PAT of roughly ₹140 Cr — OCF/PAT ratio of roughly -0.5x, far below the 0.5x threshold the framework flags as a Phase 0.1 governance concern. FY26 alone: ₹43 Cr accounting profit, ₹101 Cr cash outflow from operations. Working capital cycle ballooned from 119 days to 225-252 days inside twelve months. Debtor days at 129, inventory at 158, payables at only 35. This isn't a one-off — it's a business that's funding its growth on the customers' float and now on the IPO cash. The IPO put ₹122 Cr explicitly into working capital because the operating model demands it.
- **Customer concentration in PSU tender business.** Top 10 customers contribute roughly 50% of revenue. The PSU buyer set (BHEL, NTPC, PGCIL, state DISCOMs through state-level transformer OEMs) is price-sensitive and bid-driven — when steel prices fall, the bid prices fall faster than the input cost. Q3 FY26 CRGO realisation was down 20% YoY despite volumes holding; that's not a one-off product-mix swing, that's competitive pressure passing through.
- **CRGO raw material is import-dependent and exposed to currency and trade-policy swings.** India produces only 12% of its CRGO requirement domestically; the rest is imported from Japan, Korea, and (controversially) China. India has imposed five-year anti-dumping duty on Chinese cold-rolled non-oriented (CRNO) electrical steel but CRGO is currently exempted from these duties through March 2026 — a date that is now four months past. Any reimposition of duty on CRGO imports would compress MEIL's gross margin overnight; conversely, a rupee strengthening against the yen/won would help on the input side. Either direction is outside management control.
- **Working capital expansion is consuming the IPO cash.** ₹122 Cr of IPO proceeds was earmarked for working capital. FY26 working capital grew by roughly ₹150 Cr vs FY25 (combination of receivables, inventory, and shrinking payables). The IPO money intended to fund 18-24 months of growth got consumed in roughly twelve months. If the working capital cycle stays at 225+ days, further growth requires either more equity, more debt, or slower growth — which contradicts the entire IPO pitch.
- **No dividend, no buyback, ESOP plan launched immediately after IPO.** Company has reported four years of profits, paid no dividend, and the May 2026 board meeting that approved FY26 results also approved an ESOP-2025 plan for 15 lakh options. ESOPs themselves aren't problematic, but for a family-controlled business at 74.79% promoter holding, the immediate post-IPO ESOP grant signals where capital is being returned (employee insiders) rather than shareholders.

### How does this actually compound? (one-line summary; full breakdown in Section 3 below)

Revenue compounding comes from a single straightforward source: the CRGO line capacity doubled (16,200 → 28,000 MTPA, already operational) and the transformer plant is being upgraded to a higher voltage class (132 kV / 100 MVA, commissioning Q3 FY27), which mechanically allows MEIL to bid on a wider class of orders. The headline ROCE of 14.7% in FY26 is artificially low because the IPO doubled the equity base while the new capacity hasn't yet generated its run-rate revenue — incremental ROIC on the new lines, if they utilise at all reasonably, should be in the 18-22% range. The compounding stalls if utilisation stays below 60% or if margins stay stuck at 10-12% as competitive pressure on PSU bids continues. See Section 3 for the worked-through math.

### What does the market think — and where do I disagree?

At ₹302 against trailing PAT of ₹43 Cr the market is paying 19x earnings for a company that just printed a year-over-year profit decline. A reverse-DCF at 13% discount and 16x terminal multiple implies the market expects EPS to compound at roughly 12-13% over the next five years. That's not a wild bullish assumption — it's roughly half the IPO-time consensus growth rate (the prospectus implied 25%+ compounding through FY27) and exactly what you'd expect if the consensus has marked down the company from "fast-growing capital goods exporter" to "ordinary regional transformer maker."

Where I push back gently rather than aggressively: the new capacity is real and the demand backdrop is real — RDSS budget at ₹12,585 Cr explicitly for distribution transformers, peak demand projected to grow from 277 GW (FY26) to 366 GW (FY32). MEIL is a credentialed vendor across the relevant PSU customer set. If the new transformer plant goes live in Q3 FY27 as scheduled and absorbs even half its rated capacity by FY28, the FY28 revenue/PAT track should be ₹800 Cr / ₹70-80 Cr — well above the 12% EPS path the market implies. The bull case is plausible.

Where the bull case is wrong: the FY26 P&L and cash flow are pointing the opposite direction. Margins are compressing on competitive bid pressure (CRGO realisation -20% YoY in Q3 FY26 despite volumes flat). Working capital is consuming cash faster than profits are generating it. The IPO proceeds were designed to fund 18-24 months of growth and have been substantially absorbed in twelve months. So the market's 12% implied growth is probably right for the trailing run-rate and probably wrong for the rated capacity — but the gap between today and rated capacity is where 24 months of execution risk sits. Without a concall (the company has not held an investor concall, per Screener) and without sell-side coverage, there's no public update on order book conversion, FY27 capex plan, or response to the FY26 disappointment. The disagreement potential is real; the timeline to see it confirmed is at least two more quarters.

- ❌ **Turnaround:** No — this isn't a turnaround story. There hasn't been a recent crisis to turn around from. The post-IPO drop is the market revising estimates, not a fundamental business breakdown.

Net: roughly 1.5 of 6 triggers active. Below the "2+ triggers firing" rule for a confident entry. This is consistent with the SPECULATIVE 1% sizing — the trigger structure isn't strong enough for a regular BUY position.

## Detailed Analysis

### 1. Business Summary

Mangal Electrical Industries Limited (BSE: 544492, NSE: MEIL) is a transformer-component and electrical capital goods manufacturer founded in 1975 (with the present corporate entity incorporated 2008) and headquartered in Jaipur, Rajasthan. The company operates in two business segments per its reported segment disclosure: (i) manufacturing and trading of electrical transformers, CRGO, electrical accessories and related items; (ii) engineering, procurement and construction (EPC) services for electrical substations.

**Manufacturing footprint (per IPO prospectus and Q3 FY26 update):**
- Five production facilities, all in Rajasthan
- CRGO processing: 28,000 MTPA (expanded from 16,200 MTPA in FY26)
- Transformer assembly: 10.22 lakh KVA installed (range: single-phase 5 KVA up to three-phase 10 MVA)
- Oil-immersed circuit breakers: 75,000 units/year
- Amorphous cores: 2,400 MTPA
- Unit IV expansion at Reengus, Sikar District — under construction for 132 kV / 100 MVA transformer manufacturing, commissioning targeted Q3 FY27

**Revenue mix (FY25 disclosure):**
- CRGO + transformer components: ~70%
- Medium power transformers: ~23%
- Electrical EPC services: ~7%
- Geographic concentration: Gujarat, Rajasthan, Uttar Pradesh together approximately 71% of FY25 revenue
- Export contribution: small (single-digit percentage), per IPO prospectus

**Customer profile (named):** BHEL, Siemens, Voltamp, Crompton Greaves, Shirdi Sai Electrical, TRIL (Transformers and Rectifiers India Ltd), NTPC, PGCIL, Adani, ReNew Power, AVADA. Top 10 customers contribute approximately 50% of revenue (per IPO RHP risk factor disclosure).

**Certifications:** ISO 9001:2015, NABL-certified testing laboratory, PGCIL vendor approval (relevant for HV transformer business), BIS standards compliance.

**Promoter family:**
- Rahul Mangal — Chairman & MD (35+ years industry experience)

### 3. The Compounding Engine — Q&A

**Q1. Where does the profit actually come from, and why does the headline ROCE look mediocre?**

MEIL has roughly ₹70 Cr of fixed assets, ₹9 Cr of CWIP, and a substantial working capital block — total capital employed sits around ₹600 Cr on the FY26 balance sheet (equity ₹591 Cr + borrowings ₹45 Cr − cash and FDs roughly ₹95 Cr — net of cash, real capital employed is closer to ₹500 Cr). FY26 EBIT was roughly ₹70 Cr (operating profit margin 12% on ₹580 Cr revenue). EBIT / capital employed = roughly 14% — that's the 14.7% Screener number. By itself, 14% isn't bad for an Indian capital goods company in capex absorption mode.

The interesting question is what the *incremental* return looks like. Between FY24 and FY26, MEIL deployed roughly ₹150 Cr of additional capital (mostly fixed assets and CWIP at ₹40 Cr, plus working capital expansion of ₹120 Cr). EBIT over the same window went from roughly ₹40 Cr (FY24) to ₹70 Cr (FY26) — incremental EBIT of ₹30 Cr on ₹150 Cr of new capital, which is a 20% incremental return. That's the math the bull case rests on. If you extrapolate forward — the next ₹150-200 Cr of capacity-related investment (transformer plant commissioning, CRGO line ramp, related working capital) generates another ₹30-40 Cr of incremental EBIT — you end up at FY28 EBIT of roughly ₹110-130 Cr against FY26's ₹70 Cr, which translates to FY28 PAT of ₹75-95 Cr. That's the path to the base case in the multi-bagger table.

The math the bear case rests on is different. Most of the incremental capital deployed in FY26 was working capital, not productive capacity — and working capital that doesn't convert to cash isn't capital working, it's capital stuck. If the FY24→FY26 EBIT improvement was driven largely by FY25's outsized 15% margin print (which then mean-reverted in FY26 to 12%), the structural incremental return on capital is closer to 10-12% than 20%. That's not enough to justify the capex MEIL is undertaking, and the growth doesn't compound — it just absorbs cash with diminishing returns.

The honest answer is that we won't know which version is right until FY27 H2, when the new transformer plant either utilises and generates margin or doesn't.

**Q1b. What is MEIL reinvesting in, and how long does the runway last?**

The capex plan is straightforward and well-disclosed. Roughly ₹100 Cr was allocated from IPO proceeds for capex, broken into ₹30 Cr for the new transformer tank line, ₹30-35 Cr for the transformer plant upgrade to 132 kV / 100 MVA, and ₹35 Cr for the CRGO line expansion. That's all single-investment, located at the Unit IV facility in Reengus, Sikar District. The CRGO expansion is done (28,000 MTPA operational). The transformer plant is the major work-in-progress, commissioning targeted Q3 FY27.

The runway, in the conventional sense of "years of high-return reinvestment available," is more about sector demand than about MEIL-specific opportunities. The Indian T&D investment cycle has visible support through FY32 — that's 6-7 years of demand backdrop for distribution transformers, CRGO components, and substation EPC work. Whether MEIL captures share within that demand depends on (a) the new 132 kV plant winning its first orders, which requires PGCIL/state utility certification, and (b) competitive bid behaviour — at current 12% margins on the existing book, the company isn't earning enough to fund the next leg of expansion organically.

Sustainable growth in the conventional formula is reinvestment rate × ROIC. If MEIL retains 100% of profit (it pays no dividend) and reinvests at 14% incremental ROIC, that's 14% organic profit growth — except OCF is negative, so retention is happening on paper rather than in cash. Until cash conversion fixes, the compounding-on-paper isn't compounding-in-reality.

**Q2. What is structural vs cyclical in recent margin compression?**

Three forces are at play in the FY25 → FY26 margin compression from 15% to 12%:
1. **CRGO realisation decline** (-20% YoY in Q3 FY26 per company disclosure). This is partly steel-price pass-through (CRGO is a commodity in international markets) and partly competitive — when the input price falls, customer bid prices fall faster. **Cyclical** if it reverses, **structural** if competitive intensity is structurally elevated.
2. **Capacity utilisation drag on the new CRGO line.** The expanded line at 28,000 MTPA is operating below run-rate — fixed costs of the new equipment are being absorbed against a fraction of the rated tonnes. **Cyclical** (resolves with ramp).
3. **Working capital expansion absorbing financial discipline.** Not a P&L line directly, but the 252-day working capital cycle means receivables financing costs, inventory carrying costs, and stretched supplier payment terms — all flow through the P&L as elevated finance and operating costs. **Structural** unless management actively shortens the cycle.

Best estimate: 50% cyclical / 50% structural. The cyclical pieces should reverse in FY27 if volumes ramp; the structural pieces require management intervention (collection discipline, inventory turns) that has no visible track record.

**Q3. Five-Whys on the FY26 PAT decline:**
1. Why did FY26 PAT fall vs FY25 despite revenue growth? — Operating margin compressed from 15% to 12%.
2. Why did OPM compress? — CRGO realisations dropped 20% YoY in H2 FY26; transformer mix unchanged; capacity addition added overheads ahead of demand.
3. Why did CRGO realisations drop 20%? — Underlying CRGO steel prices in international markets fell; competitive bid pressure passed through faster than the cost side rebased.
4. Why is the bid pressure competitive enough to compress margin faster than cost? — Top 10 customers are roughly 50% of revenue, and the PSU buyer set (BHEL/PGCIL/state DISCOMs) procures on price-led tenders. When commodity input prices fall, the bid market compresses immediately.
5. Why is MEIL more exposed to this than peers? — Voltamp and Shilchar have higher mix of value-added products (specialty transformers, energy-efficiency products) and lower exposure to the commodity CRGO-component portion of the chain. *Result: the moat is thinner than peer comparison suggests. The customer set is concentrated and price-driven, the product mix is commodity-leaning, and management has not articulated a strategy to improve mix.*

**Q4. What does MEIL look like in 2 and 5 years if base case plays?**

*2 years (FY28):* Revenue ₹780-850 Cr, PAT ₹65-75 Cr, OPM stabilising at 13%. New 132 kV transformer plant commissioned and bidding for first orders (PGCIL certification typically takes 2-3 quarters post-commissioning). CRGO line at 70%+ utilisation. Working capital cycle ideally down to 180-200 days from the current 252.

Management's challenge in this window: securing the PGCIL vendor approval for HV transformers (without which the ₹100 MVA / 132 kV plant doesn't have a buyer), shortening the working capital cycle by 50+ days (requires tougher collection terms or different customer mix), and either initiating dividend payments or articulating a clear capital-return policy.

*5 years (FY31):* Revenue ₹1,100 Cr at 12-14% OPM = PAT ₹95 Cr. New transformer plant fully ramped and contributing 25-30% of revenue. CRGO line at run-rate. The company has either become a credible bidder for ₹500 Cr+ transformer orders (PGCIL EHV tenders) or it hasn't. If it has, the rerating from 19x to 22-24x on improved mix is plausible — that's the path to bull case. If it hasn't, MEIL stays a regional medium-voltage player and the multiple compresses toward Voltamp's 24x on a smaller and lower-quality earnings base.

New risks success creates: at ₹1,100 Cr revenue, MEIL is large enough to be benchmarked directly against Voltamp (₹2,000+ Cr) — every dimension of the comparison (margins, ROE, working capital, dividend policy, governance) becomes a peer comparison, and underperformance on any dimension caps the multiple. Capital allocation pressure once cash starts flowing — promoter family controls 74.79%, and the family will eventually want to redirect some of the cash (related-party transactions, real estate investment, dividend skipping in favour of internal funding of family ventures are all plausible failure modes for unlisted-style governance in a listed shell).

### 6. Competitive Landscape

The Indian transformer manufacturing space is fragmented and segmented by voltage class. At the high-voltage end (≥220 kV), BHEL, ABB, Siemens, CG Power, and Hitachi Energy dominate. In the medium-voltage and distribution segment (up to 132 kV), the field opens up considerably — Voltamp, Shilchar, TRIL, Bharat Bijlee, Sudhir Power, Schneider, and dozens of unlisted regional players compete on price-led tenders.

MEIL today plays the medium-voltage transformer market (up to 10 MVA) plus the components business (CRGO and cores). The components business has a different competitive structure — Vilash Transcore and Jay Bee Lamination are direct competitors, plus a long tail of unlisted CRGO slitters and lamination stampers. The components business is more commoditised; the transformer assembly business has more differentiation through PSU vendor approvals and product quality reputation.

The strategic pivot in flight is the move to 132 kV / 100 MVA at the Unit IV plant. If commissioned successfully and approved by PGCIL, this brings MEIL into the next-tier transformer category where the competitive set narrows (TRIL, Voltamp, Sudhir Power, and the major OEMs) and the margin profile improves (PGCIL EHV transformers earn 16-18% OPM versus 10-12% for distribution-class). This is the single most important strategic bet in the company today. Execution risk is high — PGCIL vendor approval cycles run 12-24 months post-commissioning, and the company has never produced HV transformers at this voltage class.

**Competitive risks specific to MEIL:**
- Voltamp and Shilchar with established credentials and stronger margins will fight hard for any HV transformer orders MEIL chases
- Sudhir Power and TRIL have recent CWIP investments expanding their own HV capacity — supply expansion is occurring across the peer set
- Chinese imports — if CRGO anti-dumping is lifted or if cheap Chinese transformers re-enter the market via PSU tender bids, MEIL's component pricing power compresses

### 7. Risks

1. **Cash conversion failure / working capital trap.** FY26 already showed it: ₹43 Cr profit, ₹101 Cr negative OCF. If FY27 prints similar, the IPO cash gets consumed, and either equity dilution or debt return becomes necessary. The single most important risk.
2. **HV transformer plant doesn't get PGCIL approval, or approval delayed by 12+ months past commissioning.** Without this approval, the ₹35-50 Cr invested in the plant doesn't have a buyer. Mitigation: the plant can still serve state DISCOMs and IPP customers at lower realisation.
3. **Sector competitive intensity.** Voltamp, Shilchar, TRIL, Bharat Bijlee, plus the unlisted long tail all chase the same RDSS-funded order flow. MEIL has no obvious structural advantage other than the new capacity.
4. **CRGO raw material disruption.** 57% of materials imported from China per IPO disclosure. Currency moves, trade policy, or anti-dumping reimposition all impact gross margin directly.
5. **Customer concentration in PSU bid market.** Top 10 customers ~50% of revenue, dominated by PSU OEMs and state utility-linked entities. Loss of any major customer or aggressive bid behaviour from a competitor can immediately compress quarterly numbers.
6. **Governance: no concalls, no IR, immediate post-IPO ESOP grant, no dividend.** For a family-controlled business at 74.79% promoter holding, this is not yet a problem but is a yellow flag. Lock-in expiry in Feb 2027 is the date to watch.

## Glossary

- **Amorphous core:** Transformer core made from amorphous (non-crystalline) metal ribbon rather than conventional CRGO laminations. Lower no-load energy losses, used in high-efficiency distribution transformers. MEIL has 2,400 MTPA amorphous core capacity.
- **Anti-dumping duty:** A tariff imposed by an importing country on a foreign product priced below fair market value, intended to protect domestic producers. India has imposed five-year anti-dumping duty on Chinese CRNO electrical steel; CRGO had been exempted through March 2026.
- **BHEL (Bharat Heavy Electricals Ltd):** India's largest power equipment PSU. Major buyer of transformer components and named MEIL customer.
- **CEA (Central Electricity Authority):** Central regulatory and planning body for India's power sector. Issues demand projections used in T&D capex planning.
- **CCC (Cash Conversion Cycle):** Days from buying raw material to collecting cash from customer (= debtor days + inventory days − payable days). MEIL FY26: 129 + 158 − 35 = 252 days.
- **CRGO (Cold-Rolled Grain-Oriented) steel:** Specialty silicon-iron alloy with the grain structure aligned for low magnetic losses. The critical raw material in transformer cores. India produces ~50,000 tonnes/year vs ~400,000 tonnes demand; balance imported.
- **CRNO (Cold-Rolled Non-Grain-Oriented) steel:** Sister product to CRGO with random grain orientation; used in motors and small transformers. Different anti-dumping treatment from CRGO.
- **CWIP (Capital Work in Progress):** Money already spent on building factories or equipment that isn't yet operational. Leading indicator of capacity coming online.
- **D/E (Debt-to-Equity):** Borrowings divided by shareholders' equity. Measure of leverage. MEIL post-IPO: 0.08x — effectively debt-free.
- **DISCOM (Distribution Company):** State-owned electricity distribution utility. Bulk buyer of distribution transformers (5 KVA-10 MVA range). Major end customer for MEIL through PSU OEMs.
- **EBITDA Margin / OPM (Operating Profit Margin):** Operating profit as % of revenue, before interest, tax, depreciation, and amortisation. Measures core operating profitability.
- **EPC (Engineering, Procurement & Construction):** Turnkey project delivery model — vendor handles design, sources equipment, and constructs. MEIL has a substation EPC business contributing ~7% of revenue.
- **ESOP (Employee Stock Option Plan):** Compensation programme granting employees options to buy company shares at a predetermined price. MEIL approved ESOP-2025 plan for 15 lakh options at the May 2026 board meeting.
- **FII / DII:** Foreign Institutional Investors / Domestic Institutional Investors. Mutual funds, pension funds, insurance companies, sovereign wealth funds.
- **FCF (Free Cash Flow):** Operating cash flow minus capex. The cash a business actually generates for shareholders after maintaining and growing the asset base.
- **GIS (Gas-Insulated Switchgear) substation:** Compact substation design using SF6 gas insulation instead of air. Higher cost, used in space-constrained urban locations. Growing share of new substation builds.
- **GTRI (Global Trade Research Initiative):** Indian think tank that publishes trade and industrial policy analysis. Source for the CRGO supply-demand gap data point.
- **HV (High Voltage):** Transformers rated 132 kV and above. Higher-margin segment. MEIL's Unit IV plant is targeting 132 kV / 100 MVA capability for Q3 FY27 commissioning.
- **kV (kilovolt):** Unit of electrical potential (voltage) equal to 1,000 volts. Used to specify transformer rated voltage.
- **KVA (Kilo Volt-Ampere):** Unit of apparent power. MEIL transformer capacity is 10.22 lakh KVA = 1,022 MVA.
- **MVA (Mega Volt-Ampere):** 1,000 KVA. Used for power transformer ratings.
- **MTPA (Metric Tonnes Per Annum):** MEIL CRGO capacity is 28,000 MTPA post the FY26 expansion.
- **NABL (National Accreditation Board for Testing and Calibration Laboratories):** Indian standards body accrediting test labs. MEIL has NABL certification — relevant for transformer quality testing.
- **OFS (Offer for Sale):** Component of an IPO where existing shareholders sell their shares (cash goes to those shareholders, not the company). MEIL's IPO had NO OFS — entire ₹400 Cr was fresh issue.
- **OCF (Operating Cash Flow):** Cash generated by business operations. The "real" profit figure — accounting profit only matters insofar as it converts to OCF. MEIL FY26: OCF -₹101 Cr against PAT +₹43 Cr — the broken cash conversion is the central concern.
- **P/B (Price-to-Book):** Market cap divided by book value of equity. Useful when ROE can normalise — a low P/B on a recovering ROE business signals undervaluation potential.
- **P/E (Price-to-Earnings):** Market cap divided by trailing-twelve-month net profit. The most common valuation multiple.
- **PEG (Price/Earnings to Growth):** P/E divided by forward EPS growth rate. PEG below 1.0 is conventionally cheap; above 2.0 is rich.
- **PGCIL (Power Grid Corporation of India Ltd):** Central transmission utility. Approval as a vendor is the key gate for selling HV transformers in India. MEIL is working toward PGCIL HV-class approval for its new 132 kV plant.
- **PSU (Public Sector Undertaking):** Government-owned enterprise. BHEL, NTPC, PGCIL are major PSU customers for MEIL.
- **RDSS (Revamped Distribution Sector Scheme):** Indian central scheme for distribution sector modernisation including transformer upgrades. ₹12,585 Cr allocated in FY25 alone.
- **RHP (Red Herring Prospectus):** Final IPO offer document filed with SEBI containing detailed company information. Source of MEIL's customer concentration disclosure.
- **ROCE (Return on Capital Employed):** EBIT divided by capital employed. Measures efficiency of all capital deployed regardless of financing.
- **ROE (Return on Equity):** PAT divided by shareholders' equity. Return for owners.
- **T&D (Transmission & Distribution):** The electricity grid — transmission moves bulk power long distances at high voltage, distribution delivers to end consumers at lower voltage. MEIL serves both ends through transformer components and assemblies.
- **TRIL (Transformers and Rectifiers India Ltd):** Listed transformer manufacturer. Customer to MEIL (for components) and partial peer (for finished transformers).
