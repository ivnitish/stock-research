## 1. Philosophy: Why Concentrate

> "The idea of excessive diversification is madness. Wide diversification, which necessarily includes investment in mediocre businesses, only guarantees ordinary results." — Charlie Munger

The math is straightforward. With 41 positions and Rs.15.21L invested, the average position is Rs.37K. A stock doubling from that base adds Rs.37K to the portfolio — a 2.4% gain on total capital. That is not investing; it is collecting ticker symbols.

Concentration forces three disciplines:

1. **Higher conviction threshold.** When a position is 10% of your portfolio, you cannot afford to be vaguely optimistic. You must know the business, the balance sheet, and the bear case.
2. **Meaningful impact.** A 50% gain on a 10% position moves the portfolio 5%. The same gain on a 1% tracking position moves it 0.5% — barely visible, yet it consumed the same research hours.
3. **Reduced monitoring burden.** Tracking 41 stocks means tracking none of them well. Following 10 stocks deeply — reading every quarterly transcript, every annual report, every management interview — is how you develop genuine edge.

The goal is not to minimize risk through diversification. It is to minimize ignorance through concentration.

## 6. Risks of Concentration (Honest Assessment)

Concentration amplifies both returns and mistakes. This section exists to ensure the risks are faced clearly, not hand-waved away.

### Single-Stock Risk
With ICICIAMC at 15-20% and GROWW at 15-20%, the top two positions will represent 30-40% of the portfolio. A regulatory shock to the AMC/fintech sector (new TER caps, licensing changes, UPI monetization rules) could hit both simultaneously.

**Mitigation:** These are different business models (asset management vs. brokerage platform). Correlation is moderate, not perfect. Monitor regulatory developments weekly.

### Sector Concentration
The portfolio leans heavily into:
- Financial services (ICICIAMC, GROWW) — 30-40%
- Manufacturing/engineering (KAYNES, EPACKPEB, KERNEX, BANCOINDIA) — 25-30%

A broad manufacturing slowdown or defense budget cut would hit multiple positions.

**Mitigation:** NEWGEN (software), ANANTRAJ (real estate), and RSYSTEMS (IT services) provide some diversification. Accept the concentration as the price of conviction.

### Liquidity Risk
Several positions (KERNEX, BANCOINDIA, SAKSOFT) are small-caps with thin trading volumes. Exiting a 7-8% position quickly could move the price.

**Mitigation:** At Rs.15L total portfolio, even the largest position is under Rs.3L. This is small enough that liquidity is not a practical constraint for entry or exit.

### Conviction Error
The entire strategy assumes the grading system (out of 25) is accurate. If ICICIAMC deserves a 14/25 instead of 20/25, concentrating into it destroys value.

**Mitigation:** Re-score ICICIAMC every quarter against the standardized scorecard. If the grade drops below 18/25, begin trimming. Never fall in love with a position — update the score based on new data, not on the desire to be right.

### Opportunity Cost
Holding 10 stocks means missing the next multibagger that was never in the portfolio.

**Mitigation:** Maintain a watchlist of 15-20 stocks scored but not held. Review monthly. If a watchlist stock scores above 18/25 and a held stock drops below 15/25, swap. The framework allows rotation without returning to 41-position chaos.

## 8. Rules Going Forward

1. **No new tracking positions.** Use watchlists, not 1-share holdings.
2. **Minimum position size: Rs.50K.** If the conviction is not worth Rs.50K, it is not worth holding.
3. **Maximum positions: 12.** To add a new stock, one must be exited first.
4. **Re-score quarterly.** Every held stock gets re-scored each quarter. Grade drops trigger trim/exit.
5. **Grade A gets the most capital.** If there is a Grade A stock at 5% and a Grade B stock at 20%, something is wrong. Fix it.
