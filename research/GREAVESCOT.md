## Summary Verdict

### Why this business?

Greaves Cotton looks like one company on Screener and is actually four. Two of them are quietly excellent — the legacy single-cylinder diesel engine business that powers most of India's three-wheeler taxi fleet, and Excel Controlinkage, a niche cable and mechanical-control component supplier — together earning ₹430 Cr of segment PBIT on ₹2,493 Cr of revenue in FY26 at roughly 17% segment margins. One of them is the industrial small/large-engine business, smaller and slower, but steadily profitable. The fourth — Greaves Electric Mobility, which sells the Ampere brand of electric scooters and L5 e-rickshaws — has bled ₹182 Cr in FY26 alone, lost ₹223 Cr in FY25, and has cumulatively destroyed several years of consolidated earnings power. The market is pricing the consolidated entity on the ₹35 Cr of reported PAT, which is what shows up after the EV losses eat the diesel earnings.

The core bet here is that Ampere reaches operating breakeven — not market dominance, just breakeven. If GEML's PBIT loss narrows from ₹182 Cr to zero, consolidated PAT moves from ₹35 Cr to roughly ₹200 Cr at the same engine and Excel performance. That is a 5-6x earnings move, which would justify a re-rating from 36x to something closer to 18-22x — and the stock could double. The mechanism doesn't require revolutionary success. It requires Ampere to stop burning cash, which it claims to be moving toward. Whether that claim is credible is the question this note exists to answer.

### Strengths

1. **The standalone engine + Excel business is genuinely solid.** Standalone (excluding GEML) FY26 revenue ₹2,365 Cr, EBITDA ₹320 Cr (13.5% margin), PAT ₹200 Cr, EPS ₹8.59. The Engines segment alone earned ₹376 Cr of PBIT in FY26 on ₹2,241 Cr revenue — 16.8% segment margin, +23% YoY PBIT growth. This is a real cash-generating business with 165 years of OEM relationships (Piaggio, Mahindra, Bajaj 3W passenger vehicles, plus Ligier in Europe for the Euro V+ G499W diesel). Excel Controlinkage (acquired 2023) added ₹252 Cr revenue at ₹54 Cr PBIT (21.3% margin) — actually the highest-margin segment in the group.

2. **Pre-existing physical capability is real and operating.** 9 factories, 20,000+ mechanic network, 7,000+ retail touchpoints, R&D centre at Chhatrapati Sambhajinagar, Shendra plant with CPCB IV+ genset lines commissioned 2024, Ranipet Ampere facility scaled to support 300,000+ units annual capacity. The Ligier Euro V+ engine for European microcars (launched 2025) is a marquee international win — first time Greaves has supplied a Euro-grade engine into a developed market. International revenue moved from 9% to 13% of consolidated revenue in FY26.

3. **Promoter (DBH International) at 55.78%, no pledge, stable for 8 quarters.** The Thapar-linked promoter group has held steady through five years of EV losses without diluting equity or pledging shares. Borrowings are entirely at the GEML subsidiary level (consolidated borrowings ₹326 Cr — but standalone borrowings ZERO). No related-party transaction concerns, unmodified PWC audit opinion, Best Governed Company award FY26. For a multi-segment industrial with a loss-making sub, this is unusually clean.

4. **EV losses are narrowing, not widening.** GEML segment PBIT loss went from ₹223 Cr in FY25 to ₹182 Cr in FY26 (-18% YoY) while revenue grew 19% (₹659 Cr to ₹786 Cr). Per-unit loss is compressing because revenue is growing faster than the cost base. Ampere E2W VAHAN volume +51% YoY in FY26, L5 3W +17%. Market share rose from 3.6% to 4.4%. The trajectory exists; the question is the slope.

5. **Pending GEML IPO is a label-change catalyst.** DRHP filed December 2024 with SEBI. If GEML lists at any reasonable valuation, the embedded EV value becomes price-discoverable and the holdco discount on Greaves Cotton compresses immediately. Even a modest GEML market cap of ₹3,000-4,000 Cr (well below what Ola Electric or unlisted Ather command) would imply Greaves Cotton's 75-80% stake is worth ₹2,250-3,200 Cr — covering most of the current parent market cap before counting any engine business.

### Concerns

1. **The EV business model isn't proven economically.** GEML has been part of Greaves since 2019. After six years and four full product cycles (Primus, Nexus, Magnus Grand, Magnus G Max), the business is still 4.4% market share in E2W against TVS (25%+) and Bajaj (20%+), and has yet to print a profitable quarter. The 51% volume growth in FY26 came against a low base — Indian E2W industry total volume grew 24% in the same period per VAHAN, so Greaves' "outperformance" is roughly 2x industry. That's fine. It is not extraordinary. And it has cost shareholders ~₹600 Cr cumulative losses across FY22-FY26.

2. **FAME III is the assumed catalyst and FAME III isn't notified yet.** The thesis "EV losses narrow because subsidies normalize and scale arrives" requires the Indian government to actually publish FAME III rules. The scheme was supposed to follow FAME II (which expired March 2024). As of May 2026, replacement scheme PM E-DRIVE is operating in fits and starts. Without subsidy clarity, OEM unit economics swing on government decisions made quarter-by-quarter — a regulatory tail Greaves cannot control.

3. **Consolidated ROCE is 10.1%, ROE 8.1% — not the profile of a multi-bagger candidate.** For comparison, Bosch Ltd runs 18-20% ROCE, Cummins India 25%+. Even excluding GEML, standalone ROE is decent (~12%) but not in the league of compounders. The 10-year revenue CAGR is just 8%, profit CAGR is *negative* 3%. The five-year stock price CAGR is 6%. This is not, on its history, a compounding machine. It's a multi-segment industrial whose recent good years (FY16-FY19, ~25% ROCE) have not recurred since the diesel-3W BS-VI transition began in 2020.

4. **Cash flow has been weak. Free cash flow was negative in FY22, FY23, FY24, FY25** (cumulative -₹553 Cr) and only +₹4 Cr in FY26. Operating cash flow has only just turned positive (+₹33 Cr in FY26 consolidated, +₹348 Cr standalone — the gap is GEML). The standalone business does generate cash. The consolidated entity has been funding GEML's losses out of standalone OCF and incremental borrowings (consolidated debt rose from ₹81 Cr to ₹326 Cr in FY26). This is what financing the EV ambition looks like, and it cannot continue indefinitely without either GEML breaking even or a capital event (the planned IPO).

5. **The "engineering solutions company" narrative across 165-year history has shifted three times in five years.** From "diesel 3W leader" pre-2020, to "Ampere-led EV story" 2020-2023, to "Excel-led engineered components" 2023-2024, to "GREAVES.NEXT — energy + mobility + industrial integrated platform" 2025-onwards. Management calls this strategic evolution; the financial result has been segment shifts that haven't converted to durable earnings. The FY26 result is the first year in five with all four segments simultaneously profitable except EV, and even that's still loss-making. The risk: this narrative becomes the next pivot rather than the converging story management claims.

### The Compounding Equation

Greaves Cotton does not compound in the traditional sense — it is not a business where a single engine reinvests at 25% ROIC and grows EPS at 20% for a decade. It is a sum-of-parts setup where the question is whether one segment (EV) stops dragging down the earnings of the other three.

Take the math at face value. Standalone (engines + retail + ER&D services) earned ₹200 Cr PAT on ₹2,365 Cr revenue at 13.5% EBITDA margin. Excel Controlinkage adds another ₹54 Cr of segment PBIT. The industrial small-engine business adds incremental contribution. Together — let's call this the "industrial Greaves" — the run-rate adjusted PAT is plausibly ₹220-240 Cr at FY26 numbers. On 23.3 Cr shares, that's ₹9.5-10.3 of EPS from the industrial side alone. At a 15-18x multiple appropriate for a mid-cap engine manufacturer with diversified OEM relationships, the industrial Greaves alone is worth ₹140-185 per share. The stock at ₹177 is already pricing the industrial business at roughly fair value, with the EV business as a zero-or-positive option.

The compounding from here depends on two unrelated tracks. Track one is Engines + Excel doing what they've already shown they can do — 15-17% revenue growth, ~17% segment margins, expanding international (the Ligier Euro V+ engine is the proof point, with international revenue moving from 9% to 13% of total in FY26). Track two is whether GEML's per-unit economics improve fast enough that its losses turn into profits. Today, GEML loses approximately ₹23 for every ₹100 of revenue at the PBIT level. To get to breakeven, that has to compress to zero. Management's stated path is volume (51% YoY growth narrows fixed cost absorption), mix (the Magnus Grand and Magnus G Max premium scooters carry better margins than the entry Primus), and dealer productivity (per-dealer revenue up 30% in FY26). The arithmetic is plausible. The execution is unproven.

### What does the market think — and where do I disagree?

The market is paying 35.9x trailing P/E on consolidated EPS of ₹4.60. At a 12% discount rate and a 12x terminal multiple, that implies the market expects consolidated EPS to compound at roughly 15-18% from here. The market isn't saying anything explicit about the engine business or the EV business — it's pricing the whole thing as a hybrid mid-cap industrial growth story.

My disagreement is that this is the wrong way to price this stock. The engine business deserves a flat-to-moderate-growth multiple of 14-16x. The EV business deserves either a multiple of zero (if it stays loss-making) or a venture-style optionality value priced separately (if breakeven is in sight). The blended P/E of 36x is what you get when you average a 16x business with a "we'll see" business and call it "high quality." The bet I'm willing to make is on the components, not on the blend. And the components-level bet says: pay 15x on ₹230 Cr of normalized industrial PAT = ₹3,450 Cr (₹148/share for the industrial business), plus give zero-to-modest credit for GEML until proven. That math says ₹148-165 is a fair entry, not ₹177.

Where I might be wrong: if FAME III lands sooner than expected and is generous, GEML could exit FY27 closer to breakeven than I'm modelling, and consolidated EPS could compress fast enough to justify the current price. The asymmetry is just modestly tilted in favour of patience.

### Second-Order Stress Test

**5-Whys on the ROIC engine (Engines segment, the real engine):**

> Why does the Engines segment earn ~17% segment PBIT margins?
> 1. Because Greaves dominates the single-cylinder diesel engine slot for Indian three-wheeler passenger vehicles (Piaggio Ape, Mahindra Alfa, Bajaj 3W passenger fleet).
> 2. Why? → Because BS-VI diesel engine certification, calibration, and after-sales mechanic network in Tier-2/3 India are not commodities — Greaves' 20,000-mechanic network is the largest in this segment and was built over 30 years.
> 3. Why does the mechanic network matter? → Because a 3W operator earning ₹1,000/day cannot afford engine downtime — proximity to certified service is the buying criterion, not engine specifications.
> 4. Why hasn't a competitor replicated this? → Because the market is small relative to the network density required (engines sold per year fit the existing supply base) and BS-VII / electrification ambiguity makes new ICE entrants reluctant to invest.
> 5. Why is that systemic? → Because India's regulatory direction signals a shift away from diesel 3Ws (toward CNG, LPG, and electric). This protects Greaves' incumbent position from new entrants AND limits the engine business's growth runway. The moat exists because the category is in managed decline; investors cannot recreate it but also cannot count on it expanding much beyond inflation + share gains in adjacent applications (4W microcars, marine, small construction).

The fifth answer is honest: this is a defended franchise in a flat-to-shrinking diesel sub-market. That's why Engines segment revenue went from ₹1,827 Cr (FY25) to ₹2,241 Cr (FY26) — a healthy 23% — but the multi-year CAGR is just 7-8%. The moat is durable in protecting margins; it is not durable as a growth engine.

**The world in 2 years if base case plays out:**

By FY28, the engine business is doing ₹2,800-3,000 Cr revenue at 16% segment margins, GEML revenue is ₹1,200-1,400 Cr (FY26 was ₹786 Cr) with PBIT loss narrowing to ₹60-90 Cr from ₹182 Cr today, Excel is ₹350-400 Cr at 22% margins. Consolidated PAT lifts to ₹160-200 Cr, EPS to ₹7-9. The GEML IPO has likely happened, providing both capital and price discovery for the EV segment. Management's agenda has shifted from "prove EV can work" to "scale EV profitably and decide what to do with cash from engine business" — the hardest decision they will face is capital allocation discipline once the obvious investment thesis (EV losses) is past. They have not navigated a "what do we do with excess cash" problem in this company's recent history.

**What success itself triggers:**

The biggest risk of the thesis working is that Ampere becoming a 7-8% market share E2W player at breakeven attracts the consolidation that competitor majors have been waiting for — TVS, Bajaj, Hero are all closer to break-even at higher market shares and have the scale to ride out price wars. If FAME III subsidies normalize, the rebid happens at TVS/Bajaj/Hero scale, not Ampere scale. The other risk: a successful GEML IPO removes the listed-entity-funding-EV-burn pattern, but it also removes the call option on EV scaling. Greaves Cotton becomes a more pure-play industrial with a stake in a separately-listed EV company — that's a value clarifying event, not necessarily a value-creating one.

## 1. Business Summary

Greaves Cotton is a 165-year-old engineering company headquartered in Mumbai (registered office in Aurangabad), majority-owned by the Thapar-linked DBH International Investments (55.78%). It manufactures four broad categories of products through its standalone entity and consolidated subsidiaries: (a) single- and multi-cylinder diesel engines for three-wheelers, microcars, marine, agriculture, and fire-fighting equipment; (b) electric two-wheelers and L5 electric three-wheelers under the Ampere brand via Greaves Electric Mobility Limited (GEML, 75-80% subsidiary, separately filed for IPO December 2024); (c) automotive components — cables, control levers, gear shifters — via Excel Controlinkage Pvt Ltd (80% subsidiary as of August 2025, acquired April 2023); (d) engineering R&D services via Greaves Technologies Limited (GTL); plus genset systems and aftermarket retail. FY26 consolidated revenue ₹3,437 Cr, +18% YoY. The standalone diesel-engine business (now organisationally relabelled as "Mobility Solutions" + "Energy Solutions" + "Industrial Solutions") earns operating margins in the 13-14% range; the EV business has historically destroyed value. The combined picture is masked by reporting consolidated.

## 3. Compounding Engine — Q&A

### Q0. Operating Leverage Test — does growth create margin leverage?

- Revenue growth (FY26 vs FY25): 18%
- EBITDA margin: 4.7% (FY25) → 7.0% (FY26) — **+230 bps expansion**
- **Operating leverage present?** Yes, at the consolidated level, driven by GEML loss narrowing rather than core margin expansion. Standalone EBITDA margin moved more modestly from 13.1% → 13.5%.
- Capacity utilization: Not disclosed segment-wise. Engine capacity is fragmented across 9 factories — not a single-plant utilisation story. GEML Ranipet runs significantly below its ~300k-unit capacity (FY26 E2W VAHAN volume ~62k units, so utilisation ~20%).
- CWIP trend: ₹43 Cr (FY25) → ₹30 Cr (FY26) consolidated — declining, indicating capex cycle is winding down or projects are being capitalised faster than new ones start.

### Q1. Is incremental ROIC genuinely high (>20%) — and is it structural, not cyclical?

Engine business (segment data, FY26): PBIT ₹376 Cr on segment assets ₹1,227 Cr = 30.7% pre-tax ROCE at the segment level. This is structurally high because the diesel 3W engine business is asset-light relative to revenue — most capex is incremental tooling for new engine variants, not greenfield plants. Excel: PBIT ₹54 Cr on segment assets ₹489 Cr = 11% — lower because the Excel acquisition is still being integrated and asset base includes recent goodwill.

Consolidated ROIC math (incremental, 3-year delta):
- NOPAT FY23: ₹70 Cr × (1 - 0.38) = ₹43 Cr; FY26: ₹35 Cr × (1 - 0.69) ≈ ₹11 Cr; or using EBIT (₹239 - 109 D&A) = ₹130 Cr × 0.75 = ₹98 Cr
- Capital Employed FY23: ₹1,615 Cr; FY26: ₹1,825 Cr
- Incremental ROIC (FY26 vs FY23) is essentially zero on PAT basis, modest on EBIT basis. **The headline consolidated ROIC is broken because of GEML losses.** The segment-level engine business runs at 30%+ ROCE; the consolidated number averages it with a -25% ROCE EV business.

What would break the engine moat: BS-VII (next emission norm, likely 2027-28) — engine certification cost spikes again, like BS-VI did. Or India accelerating 3W electrification beyond what current penetration trends suggest. Both are structural risks, not cyclical.

### Q2. How much capital can be redeployed at these returns — what's the reinvestment runway?

Engine business reinvestment runway is limited. The diesel 3W market is shrinking in volume (CNG and electric taking share); Greaves has stable revenue here because its OEM relationships are deep, but cannot deploy meaningful new capital at 30% ROCE — the unit volume isn't growing. Adjacencies (microcar engines via Ligier, marine engines via fire-fighting and small marine OEMs, agriculture small engines) are where reinvestment is happening — international revenue at 13% of total in FY26, growing toward management's stated focus area.

GEML reinvestment is the opposite case: large runway, but ROIC is currently negative. Management's investment plan calls for ₹500-700 Cr capex over the medium term (slide 14 of Q4 FY26 IP), much of it directed at GEML capacity, product development, and dealer network. If GEML reaches breakeven, this capital deployed at scale could earn at industry-average ~10-12% segment ROCE eventually. That's neither high-quality compounding nor disastrous — it's industrial-average.

Net assessment: the runway is medium. Not 3 years (too short), not 15 years (no platform business). 5-7 years if the EV bet works; 3-5 years if it doesn't.

### Q3. What's the implied compounding math?

Standalone reinvests modestly (capex ~₹65 Cr/year, depreciation ~₹50 Cr — net new capex ~₹15-20 Cr), retains roughly 80% of standalone PAT. At ₹200 Cr standalone PAT, retained earnings ~₹160 Cr deployed at marginal ~15% ROCE = ₹24 Cr incremental earnings = 12% standalone earnings growth. Stable but not exceptional.

Consolidated math is dominated by GEML breakeven trajectory rather than reinvestment economics. If GEML loss narrows from -₹182 Cr to zero over 3 years, consolidated EPS moves from ₹4.60 to ~₹10 (assuming standalone PAT grows at 10%). That's a 30% EPS CAGR, but it's a recovery, not a compounding rate that can be extended.

Implied total return over 5 years (base case): EPS ₹4.60 → ₹7.5 (10% CAGR), exit multiple 20x → ₹150. -15% from CMP. **The compounding math says don't pay 36x for this profile.**

**Smell test:** No, sustainable 25%+ CAGR is not in the cards. The 5-year EPS growth profile is recovery + modest engine growth = ~10-12% CAGR. Even in the bull scenario where GEML breaks even, it's a one-time level shift, not a compounding curve.

### Q4. What breaks the thesis?

**The compounding breaks if:** GEML losses widen rather than narrow over the next 4 quarters, OR the Engines segment loses a major OEM contract (Piaggio, Mahindra 3W, or Bajaj 3W passenger) to a competing engine supplier, OR FAME III is notified at terms that materially reduce the Indian E2W TAM.

**Leading indicator I'd see before the stock price tells me:** Quarterly segment data — specifically (a) GEML segment PBIT loss as a percentage of GEML segment revenue, and (b) Engines segment PBIT as percentage of Engines revenue. If (a) widens or (b) compresses below 14%, the thesis is breaking in real time before the stock reacts.

## 6. Competitive Landscape

### Market Position

Greaves operates in three competitive arenas with very different dynamics. In **single-cylinder diesel engines for Indian 3W passenger vehicles**, Greaves is the dominant supplier (market share difficult to pin down due to OEM-specific contracts, but estimated 50-60% of certified diesel 3W engines on Indian roads) — direct competitors are Lombardini (Greaves' own historical JV partner from 1976), Kohler, and small Chinese imports. In **electric two-wheelers**, Greaves' Ampere ranks #6 with 4.4% market share, well behind Bajaj (~22%), TVS (~25%), Ola Electric (~18%), Ather (~12%), and Hero Vida (~6%). In **mechanical control components (cables, gear shifters)** through Excel, the competitive set is fragmented across small auto-ancillary suppliers; Excel commands premium pricing on niche OEM contracts.

### Structural Advantages vs Peers

1. **Mechanic + retail network for diesel 3W aftermarket:** 20,000+ certified mechanics across India and 7,000+ retail outlets. This is not easily replicated — it was built over 30 years of OEM partnership with Piaggio and Mahindra. Quantified effect: aftermarket parts and service contribute ~28% of consolidated revenue (Energy Solutions segment) at higher margins than OEM engine sales — the network monetises across both new and existing fleet.

2. **Marquee international engine supply (Ligier Euro V+):** Greaves became the first Indian engine maker to supply a Euro-grade diesel engine to a European microcar OEM (Ligier, France) starting FY26. International revenue moved from 9% to 13% of consolidated. Replication barrier: Euro V+ certification cycle is 18-24 months; the relationship was nurtured for 3-4 years before first commercial supply.

3. **Excel Controlinkage niche position:** Mechanical control cables and levers for automotive transmission — Excel earns 21% segment PBIT margin, the highest in the group, on a product where Indian OEMs have limited supplier alternatives. Replication barrier: certified supplier list for OEM transmission components is tightly controlled; new entrants take 3-5 years to qualify.

**Source:** Screener.in consolidated data Apr-May 2026; Q4 FY26 Investor Presentation (May 6, 2026); audited consolidated financial results Q4 FY26 + FY26; Trendlyne technical snapshot; multiple secondary press sources for context.

#### The setup

Greaves Cotton is a sum-of-parts arbitrage trade dressed up as a growth stock. The standalone diesel engine + Excel Controlinkage businesses earn ~₹200 Cr in PAT at a 13.5% EBITDA margin and a 30%+ segment ROCE — genuinely good unit economics. The market is paying 36x consolidated trailing P/E because consolidated EPS (₹4.60) is depressed by Greaves Electric Mobility Limited (GEML), the Ampere EV subsidiary, which lost ₹182 Cr at the segment PBIT line in FY26 on ₹786 Cr of revenue.

#### Segment data (the analytical core)

| Segment | FY25 Rev | FY25 PBIT | FY26 Rev | FY26 PBIT | YoY |
|---------|---------|-----------|---------|-----------|-----|
| Engines | 1,828 | 305 (16.7%) | 2,241 | 377 (16.8%) | PBIT +23% |
| EV & Other Vehicles | 659 | -223 | 786 | -182 | Loss -18% |
| Excel (cables) | 268 | 68 (25.5%) | 252 | 54 (21.3%) | PBIT -21% |
| Others | 224 | -25 | 211 | -8 | Loss -68% |

The EV loss narrowed from -33.9% margin to -23.2% margin while revenue grew 19%. That's the bull case mechanism — operating leverage on a still-loss-making business as it scales toward breakeven. The bear case is that 18%/year improvement is too slow, and the absolute loss number stays meaningful into FY28.

#### Why I'm cautious despite the segment math being attractive

1. **EV competitive intensity is high and Ampere is sub-scale.** Market share 4.4% versus TVS/Bajaj at 22-25% each. Even strong volume growth (51% YoY in FY26) just keeps up with industry growth from a low base.

2. **FAME III isn't notified.** The subsidy framework that the entire Indian E2W TAM depends on has been pending replacement since FAME II expired March 2024. PM E-DRIVE is an interim measure. Without clarity, OEM unit economics swing on government decisions.

3. **Consolidated track record is poor.** 10-year EPS CAGR is -6%. Stock has compounded 2% per year. The multi-segment narrative has shifted three times in five years — from diesel 3W leader to Ampere story to Excel-led engineered components to GREAVES.NEXT integrated platform.

4. **At ₹177, the asymmetry is bad.** Upside to bull case +54%, downside to bear case -73%, asymmetry ratio 0.74x. The math only works below ₹140.

#### What would change my mind

(a) GEML quarterly PBIT loss confirmed <₹25-30 Cr (annualised <₹100 Cr) — implies trajectory toward breakeven by FY28.
(b) FAME III notified at terms similar to or better than FAME II, with implementation clarity.
(c) GEML IPO completes at ≥₹4,000 Cr equity valuation — provides capital relief and price discovery.
(d) Stock at ₹120-135 absent any of the above — pure value entry where industrial Greaves alone supports the price.

#### Promoter and governance

DBH International at 55.78%, flat 8 quarters. No pledge. PWC unmodified audit. ICSI Best Governed Company award FY26. Auditor confirms no material misstatements. Cash flow has turned positive at consolidated level in FY26 (+₹33 Cr OCF) — first time in 4 years. Standalone OCF strong at ₹348 Cr. Standalone debt = zero. Consolidated debt ₹326 Cr is GEML/GFL funded. Governance is clean; the question is purely operating execution.

#### Final call

WATCHLIST. Track GEML quarterly segment PBIT. Buy only if (a)-(d) triggers fire. If buying, treat as SPECULATIVE position at 1.5-2% portfolio weight maximum, with hard exits on segment-data deterioration.

## Glossary — Key Industry Terms

**Ampere**
Greaves Cotton's electric scooter brand, manufactured by subsidiary Greaves Electric Mobility Limited (GEML, ~75-80% owned by Greaves Cotton). Acquired in 2019. Products include the Primus (entry), Nexus (mid), Magnus Grand and Magnus G Max (premium) electric scooters, plus L5-category electric three-wheelers under the "Greaves 3 Wheelers" sub-brand including the ELTRA. Ampere ranks #6 in Indian E2W market with 4.4% share as of FY26.

**BS-VI / BS-VII (Bharat Stage emission norms)**
India's vehicle emission standards equivalent to Euro norms. BS-VI is current (effective April 2020); BS-VII is the next stage, expected 2027-28. Each transition imposes significant R&D + capex cost on engine manufacturers — the BS-VI transition was a major driver of Greaves' FY21-FY22 margin collapse. Diesel engines face stricter limits than gasoline.

**CWIP (Capital Work in Progress)**
Money spent on capacity that isn't operational yet — buildings under construction, machinery being installed. When CWIP is rising quarter-over-quarter, the company is investing in future capacity; when it falls, projects are completing (the asset moves from CWIP to fixed assets) or capex is winding down. For Greaves, CWIP has been declining (₹43 Cr → ₹30 Cr consolidated) indicating completion of the current capex cycle rather than acceleration.

**Diesel 3W / Three-Wheeler**
The auto-rickshaw segment used widely in Indian urban transport — passenger (Piaggio Ape, Mahindra Alfa, Bajaj RE) and cargo (small commercial vehicle variant). Most diesel 3Ws use single-cylinder engines in the 400-600cc range, almost all certified by Greaves Cotton or Lombardini. The category is in managed decline as CNG and electric variants take share.

**Excel Controlinkage**
Subsidiary of Greaves Cotton (acquired April 2023, stake raised from 70% → 80% in August 2025). Makes mechanical control components — cables, levers, gear shifters — for automotive transmissions. Differentiated by tight OEM qualification cycles (3-5 years), which protects margins. Excel earns 21% segment PBIT margin — the highest in the Greaves group.

**FAME II / FAME III / PM E-DRIVE**
Indian government subsidy schemes for electric vehicles. FAME II ran April 2019 – March 2024 with direct subsidies on E2W purchases. FAME III is the proposed successor, repeatedly delayed since 2024. PM E-DRIVE is the interim scheme operating in fits and starts in 2025-26. Indian E2W OEM economics — including Ampere — depend materially on these subsidy structures because Indian consumers buy E2W primarily on payback period to existing ICE 2W, and subsidy is what makes that payback acceptable.

**GEML (Greaves Electric Mobility Limited)**
The electric mobility subsidiary of Greaves Cotton, ~75-80% owned. Houses the Ampere E2W brand, the L5 3W brand, and includes step-down subsidiaries MLR Auto Ltd and Bestway Agencies. Filed DRHP for IPO in December 2024 with SEBI. FY26 revenue ₹786 Cr, segment PBIT loss -₹182 Cr.

**GFL (Greaves Finance Limited)**
Wholly-owned NBFC subsidiary providing EV financing under the "evfin" brand. AUM ₹521 Cr (May 2026). Live with 11 EV OEM partnerships including Ather, Suzuki, Simple, Ultraviolette. Small relative to parent but provides ecosystem completeness for the EV thesis.

**L5 Three-Wheeler**
Indian motor vehicle classification for three-wheeled motor vehicles with seating for 4+ passengers including driver, or with gross weight 1000kg+. The Ampere ELTRA and similar electric 3W passenger vehicles fall in this category. Distinct from L3 (smaller two-wheelers) and L7 (heavier quadricycles). L5 is the main electric "e-rickshaw" passenger 3W category in India.

**Ligier (Euro V+ engine supply)**
French manufacturer of microcars and license-required-free quadricycles (sold in France, Italy, Spain as urban runabouts). Greaves Cotton launched the G499W diesel engine for Ligier in 2025 — first Indian-made Euro V+ certified engine supplied to a European OEM. Symbolises Greaves' move beyond legacy Indian 3W engines into European 4W microcar applications.

**PBIT (Profit Before Interest and Tax)**
Segment-level operating profit before deducting interest expense and tax. Used in segment reporting to compare profitability of business units without distortion from how each is financed. For Greaves, segment PBIT is the cleanest read on which segments are creating value (Engines, Excel) versus which are destroying it (GEML).

**Segment PBIT margin**
PBIT divided by segment revenue. For Engines = ₹377 / ₹2,241 = 16.8% in FY26. For GEML = -₹182 / ₹786 = -23.2%. A negative margin means the segment is losing money before tax and interest — operationally unprofitable, not just leveraged.

**VAHAN**
Indian government's centralised vehicle registration database run by the Ministry of Road Transport and Highways. Provides authoritative monthly volume data on vehicle registrations by category (E2W, ICE 2W, L5 3W, etc). The standard source for EV market share calculations in India.
