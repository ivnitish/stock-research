## Summary Verdict

### Why this business?

India has exactly one private-sector onshore E&P company with a 30-year operating history, zero debt,
and a promoter who just consolidated his personal holding entity into the listed vehicle — signalling
long-term commitment to the business rather than an exit plan. Antelopus Selan Energy is that company.
After nearly a decade of flat production hovering around 1,000–1,100 boepd, Q3 FY26 showed 1,498 boepd
— a 35% YoY jump driven by two newly acquired fields (Elao and Cambay 50% PI, both acquired FY25).
The bet is straightforward: India's private E&P sector is underinvested, Cambay Basin fields have more
recoverable reserves than current production implies, and a net-cash company with a 70% promoter stake
has the balance sheet to drill aggressively without dilution. What the market is not yet pricing is the
volume growth trajectory; what I'm betting on is that 2,000+ boepd becomes visible within 12–18 months
and re-rates this from a stagnant royalty to a growth E&P.

### Strengths

- **Net cash balance sheet (₹94.5 Cr), zero meaningful debt** — rare in E&P; company can fund well-drilling from internal accruals without equity dilution or expensive debt
- **Volume inflection confirmed:** Q3 FY26 production at 1,498 boepd (+35% YoY) — first sustained uptick after years of ~1,100 boepd; new fields (Elao, Cambay 50%) are the visible driver
- **Promoter alignment — 69.94% holding, stable** — recent merger with Antelopus Energy Pvt Ltd (promoter's private entity) signals consolidation into the listed vehicle, not an exit
- **Private sector operator with lower political risk than ONGC/OIL** — no PSU pricing distortions, government mandate complexity, or bureaucratic field development delays
- **PAT doubled YoY (FY25 ₹74 Cr vs FY24 ₹32.7 Cr)** — partially crude-price driven but volume growth is the structural component that persists

### Concerns

- **100% crude-price dependency with no hedge** — ONGC Q3 FY26 realization was $61.63/bbl (-15% YoY); at $55/bbl, ANTELOPUS earnings likely halve. There is no mechanism to escape this.
- **Mature basin, small scale:** Cambay Basin is a mature hydrocarbon province — all fields are incremental development plays, not exploration upside. At 1,500 boepd, operating leverage is limited; a single unproductive well materially impacts numbers.
- **No dividends despite consistent profitability** — promoter retaining all capital in the business is defensible, but with no disclosed reinvestment ROI data, this reads as capital hoarding rather than disciplined reinvestment. Needs monitoring.
- **PSC contract extension risk** — Production Sharing Contracts with GoI have finite terms; aging contracts approaching expiry create regulatory uncertainty on field operatorship.

### The Compounding Equation

Antelopus Selan's compounding engine is not ROIC-driven in the traditional sense — it is a commodity
volume story with a cost structure that is largely fixed. The business earns its returns through the
spread between crude realization (benchmarked to Brent, typically at a 10–15% discount) and a
relatively low lifting cost. At current production of ~1,500 boepd and crude at $70/bbl, operating
profit per barrel is approximately $25–30/bbl after lifting costs of ~$40–45/bbl — implying EBITDA of
roughly ₹130–150 Cr annualized. PAT tracks at 50–55% of EBITDA given minimal depreciation and zero
interest expense. The reinvestment story is well-drilling: each new well in Cambay costs ₹8–15 Cr and
can add 30–80 boepd incremental production. With ₹94.5 Cr net cash and growing internal accruals, the
company can fund 5–8 new wells per year without external capital. If those wells sustain production
above 2,000 boepd through FY27, intrinsic earnings power reaches ₹120–130 Cr — at which point this
trades at 15–16x forward PE, reasonable for a growing E&P. The runway is not 10 years — Cambay is a
mature basin and ceiling production is probably 3,000–4,000 boepd before significant new field
acquisitions are needed. That's a 3–5 year window of high-confidence growth, not a decade-long compounder.

### What does the market think — and where do I disagree?

At ₹570 and ₹74 Cr FY25 PAT, the market is paying 27.4x trailing PE — which implies it is pricing in
either a sustained crude recovery to $75–80/bbl or continuation of FY26's volume growth trajectory, but
not both simultaneously. The forward PE of ~18x (annualizing Q3 FY26 net profit of ₹28.5 Cr = ~₹114
Cr annualized) is more reasonable but assumes no crude deterioration. Where the market may be
underweighting: the new field (Elao + Cambay 50%) production ramp is still early — Q3 FY26 showed the
jump, but Q4 FY26 and Q1 FY27 will confirm whether the 1,500 boepd floor holds. If it does, forward
estimates will be revised up by sell-side, and the stock re-rates. The market is currently treating this
as a commodity trade (correct) without valuing the volume growth optionality (the disagreement). The gap
is roughly 20–25 percentage points of earnings growth between what trailing PE implies (flat or modest
growth, ~8%) and what the new field ramp suggests (~30–40% FY26E growth, normalizing to ~15% beyond).

## 1. Business Summary

Antelopus Selan Energy Ltd (formerly Selan Exploration Technology Ltd) is India's oldest private-sector
onshore E&P company, incorporated in 1992. The company operates producing fields in the Cambay Basin,
Gujarat: Bakrol (~500 boepd crude), Karjisan (~503 boepd gas), Lohar (~59 boepd crude), and two
recently acquired fields — Elao and Cambay (50% participating interest, FY25). Crude is sold to domestic
refineries at Brent-benchmarked prices (typically 10–15% discount); gas from Karjisan is sold to local
industry at GoI-administered domestic gas prices. In August 2025, the company merged with Antelopus Energy
Pvt Ltd (the promoter's private E&P entity) and rebranded — consolidating the promoter's oil and gas
assets under a single listed vehicle. The merger is structurally positive: it eliminates a related-party
competing entity and aligns promoter economics entirely with minority shareholders.

## 3. Compounding Engine — Q&A

### Q1. Is incremental ROIC genuinely high (>20%) — and is it structural, not cyclical?

This is where the ANTELOPUS thesis runs into its fundamental constraint. In commodity E&P, ROIC is
structurally determined by crude price × production volume, not by management skill or competitive
positioning. At $70/bbl crude and 1,500 boepd production, annualized revenue is approximately ₹280–300
Cr. With operating costs (lifting + G&A) of roughly ₹130–150 Cr, EBITDA is ~₹130–150 Cr, and PAT after
minimal D&A and zero interest is ~₹80–100 Cr. Against net invested capital (fixed assets + working
capital, ex-cash) of approximately ₹350–400 Cr, that is ROCE of 22–28% at $70/bbl.

The critical word is "at $70/bbl." At $55/bbl, the same math produces PAT of ₹30–40 Cr and ROCE of
~10% — not a compounder. At $80/bbl, PAT approaches ₹130–150 Cr and ROCE exceeds 35%. ROIC is high
but entirely cyclical, not structural. The incremental ROIC on new well-drilling is more stable: at
$65–70/bbl, a new well costing ₹12 Cr adding 50 boepd generates ~₹18–20 Cr annual incremental EBITDA
— payback under 1 year, implying incremental ROIC of 100%+ on drilling capex. This well-level economics
IS structural and supports aggressive drilling investment. What breaks at low crude is the base business
ROIC, not the well-level incremental return.

---

### Q2. How much capital can be redeployed at these returns — what's the reinvestment runway?

The reinvestment opportunity is well-drilling in existing and newly acquired blocks. Antelopus has
₹94.5 Cr of net cash today and generates ₹70–110 Cr FCF annually at $65–70/bbl crude. At ₹12–15 Cr
per well, that funds 6–8 new wells per year — which, at 30–50 boepd per well success rate, could add
200–400 boepd annually. Starting from 1,500 boepd, a sustained 200–300 boepd annual increment reaches
2,500–3,000 boepd by FY28–29 — roughly doubling current production.

The binding constraint is not capital — it is reservoir quality. Cambay Basin is mature. Not every
well will be productive. Industry success rates for development wells in mature basins run at 60–70%.
The real reinvestment runway question is: how many drillable locations exist in the current block
licenses? Management has not disclosed a proven reserve-to-production ratio or well inventory, which
is a transparency gap. The newly acquired Elao and Cambay (50% PI) fields add 3–5 years of drilling
inventory. Beyond that, new block acquisitions (OALP rounds, farm-ins) are needed to extend runway
past FY29. Reinvestment rate runs at approximately 40–60% of earnings — disciplined, not aggressive.

**Runway estimate:** 3–5 years of high-confidence volume growth (to ~2,500–3,000 boepd), then uncertain.

---

### Q3. What's the implied compounding math?

At a reinvestment rate of 50% of earnings and incremental ROIC of 22–25% on invested capital (blended
crude/volume assumption), intrinsic value compounds at approximately 11–12% per year — below the 20%
threshold for a multi-bagger. The EPS growth can temporarily spike to 30–40% in FY26 because it is
starting from a low base (FY24 crude was weaker) and because new fields are ramping. But terminal steady-
state growth once production plateaus at 2,500–3,000 boepd is mid-single-digit to low-double-digit,
determined almost entirely by crude price trajectory, not management reinvestment quality.

- Entry multiple ~27x trailing (18x forward) → exit multiple 15x at FY28 maturity = **de-rating headwind**
- PAT CAGR of 18–20% over 2 years (base case, volume + crude stable) → net EPS growth ~10–12% after re-rating compression
- **Implied total return: 1.0–1.2x in 2 years at current price** — uninspiring

**Smell test:** No. At ₹570, ANTELOPUS does not pass the multi-bagger smell test. It is a commodity recovery play with a volume kicker — potentially a 30–50% trade over 12–18 months if crude recovers, not a 3–5x compounder.

---

### Q4. What breaks the thesis?

**The compounding breaks if:** Crude oil falls sustainably below $58/bbl, collapsing the spread between realization and lifting costs and making well-drilling economically marginal — at which point ANTELOPUS reverts to a cash-burning, slowly depleting asset.

**Leading indicator I'd see before the stock price tells me:** ONGC quarterly crude realization falling below $60/bbl for two consecutive quarters, combined with ANTELOPUS management pausing new well-drilling capex (visible in BSE quarterly capex disclosures). The capex pause would confirm management sees the same deteriorating economics.

## 6. Competitive Landscape

### Market Position

Antelopus Selan Energy operates exclusively in onshore Cambay Basin, Gujarat — India's oldest oil-producing
province. Private-sector onshore E&P is a tiny segment: ONGC dominates Indian E&P with 70%+ of domestic
production; Oil India holds another significant share. Private players (Vedanta/Cairn, HOEC, Antelopus)
together account for under 10% of domestic production. At ~1,500 boepd, Antelopus is a micro-operator —
too small to influence prices, too niche to attract large institutional interest. Its competitive position
within private E&P is defined by its PSC license portfolio, not by operational excellence.

### Structural Advantages vs Peers

1. **Zero-debt balance sheet:** No E&P company — public or private — operates with zero debt at scale. Antelopus's net-cash position (₹94.5 Cr) means no refinancing risk even at $50/bbl crude. Vedanta's E&P arm carries significant debt; HOEC has historically been capital-constrained. This translates to 2–3% cost advantage on capital deployed in new wells vs leveraged peers.

2. **Cambay Basin operational knowledge:** 30+ years in a specific basin creates proprietary sub-surface data (seismic, well logs, reservoir modeling) that is not replicable by a new entrant. This modestly improves well success rates vs an operator without basin history.

3. **Private-sector operational flexibility:** Unlike ONGC/OIL, Antelopus can move faster on contracting, drilling decisions, and asset acquisitions without public-sector bureaucracy. The Elao and Cambay block acquisitions (FY25) demonstrate this agility.

### Competitive Dynamics

- **Market share trend:** Irrelevant in commodity E&P — no one "wins" market share; production depends entirely on reserves and wells drilled.
- **Primary competitive threat:** Crude price collapse — not a competitor but the only meaningful risk to economics. At $55/bbl, all private E&P operators face the same margin compression.
- **Key vulnerability:** Small production scale means single field events (well workovers, equipment failures) materially impact quarterly numbers. Karjisan gas field (~503 boepd equivalent) is priced at GoI-administered domestic gas prices — a regulatory risk distinct from crude.
- **Policy tailwind:** India's OALP (Open Acreage Licensing Policy) has made new block acquisitions easier for private players — Antelopus can bid for new Cambay Basin acreage as existing fields mature.
