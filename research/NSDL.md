# NSDL (National Securities Depository) — Recently Listed, Market Pricing the Wrong Comparison

**Status:** LISTED · NSE/BSE (544467) · Recent IPO
**Quality Score:** 16/25 (Grade B)
**Classification:** Quality compounder at fair valuation; structural underdog to CDSL on margins
**Last Updated:** 2026-05-10 | **CMP:** ₹875 | **Mcap:** ₹17,503 Cr | **P/E:** 46.1x | **P/B:** 7.39x

> **Recommendation:** Tracking position 1.0-1.5% at current levels. NSDL is the larger depository in India by every infrastructure metric — 65,391 depository participant service centres versus CDSL's 18,918, materially higher custody value, more issuers, more active instruments — yet earns 28% operating margin against CDSL's 51%. The market is pricing the gap as structural and probably right to do so: CDSL's retail concentration (Zerodha, Groww, every major discount broker defaults to CDSL) is the higher-margin business, and NSDL's institutional mix (FIIs, mutual funds, custody for large issuers) is heavier and less profitable per customer. The investment question is not whether NSDL becomes CDSL — it won't — but whether NSDL's institutional moat compounds at 15-18% earnings growth durably enough to justify a 46x P/E. At current trailing PEG of roughly 3x, the answer requires forward EPS growth of 25%+ for two years to bring PEG below 2x. That is plausible but not guaranteed. Add at sub-₹750 if forward growth confirms; trim or rotate to CDSL on any operating margin deterioration.

## Summary Verdict

### Why this business?

NSDL is one of only two licensed securities depositories in India — a 30-year regulated duopoly with CDSL — and the larger of the two on every infrastructure metric: more DP service centres, more custody value, more issuers, more active instruments. Every dematerialised share, bond, mutual fund unit, or government security in India sits on the books of either NSDL or CDSL; SEBI has not granted a third licence in 25+ years. NSDL earns revenue from account maintenance fees, transaction charges, IPO and corporate-action fees, and adjacent services through its NDML subsidiary (e-KYC, account aggregator infrastructure, financial education). The core asset is the network itself — once a broker, bank, mutual fund or large issuer integrates with NSDL, the technical and operational cost of moving to CDSL is prohibitive, which is why customer relationships hold for decades and why the duopoly economics are durable. The investment question is not whether NSDL is a quality business — it is, with a debt-free balance sheet, 17% ROE, 23% ROCE, and a regulatory monopoly inside its segment. The question is whether it can grow earnings fast enough to justify a 46x trailing P/E with a structural 23-percentage-point operating margin gap to CDSL that the customer mix (institutional-heavy versus retail-heavy) makes essentially unfixable.

### First principles — the physical earnings mechanism

NSDL will earn more money in FY29 than today because of three observable mechanisms already in motion:

1. **Indian equity AUM compounds at 15-18% per year, mechanically pulling custody-fee revenue along with it.** Custody value is currently ~₹400 lakh Cr and grows in line with market capitalisation. Custody fees are charged at a few basis points of value per year — as AUM compounds, so does this revenue line without NSDL having to do anything new. Evidence in motion: 5-year revenue CAGR of 27% has tracked equity AUM growth almost perfectly.
2. **NDML adjacent revenue (e-KYC, account aggregator, ICT services) grows 25-30% per year off a base of ~10-12% of consolidated revenue**, lifting consolidated margin as the mix shifts toward higher-margin services. Evidence in motion: account aggregator user base growing 80-100% YoY industry-wide per RBI data; NSDL's NDML is one of three licensed AA operators.
3. **Operating leverage on existing technology and operations infrastructure** — depository operations are largely fixed-cost; incremental institutional customers and incremental transaction volume drop through at high marginal contribution. Evidence in motion: 5-year revenue CAGR 27% running ahead of 5-year cost-base growth ~22% — leverage *should* show up in margins eventually, even if the gap to CDSL persists.

The bear-case version requires either (a) retail demat account growth concentrating entirely with CDSL so equity AUM growth doesn't translate to NSDL revenue, or (b) operating leverage failing to materialise so margins stay stuck at 28%. Both are real risks; neither is the base case. If both happen simultaneously, NSDL becomes a 10% earnings grower stuck at 25x P/E — the bear scenario in the multi-bagger math table below.

### What NSDL is, and what just changed

NSDL is the larger of India's two securities depositories, set up in 1996 by NSE, IDBI Bank, and UTI as the country's first electronic depository. It went public in 2025 (Aug 2025 IPO completed; ₹4,000+ Cr offer) after years of regulatory pressure to dilute the founding shareholders down to non-promoter levels. As of FY26, NSDL holds the largest market share in custody value, has the most issuers, the most active instruments, and the most service centres of any depository in India. CDSL — its only competitor — is smaller on infrastructure but dominant in retail demat accounts.

What changed in 2025-26 is the listing itself. Pre-IPO, NSDL was a private utility owned by a consortium of large financial institutions (NSE, IDBI, SBI, HDFC Bank, Deutsche Bank, and several others). Post-IPO, it trades as a shareholder-controlled business with public float majority and no defined promoter — closer to BSE's structure than to a typical promoter-controlled franchise. The first FY26 annual results (filed May 2026) printed revenue ₹1,530 Cr and PAT ₹380 Cr. The 5-year revenue CAGR is 27%; 5-year profit CAGR is 15%. The gap between revenue and profit growth is the central operating story — NSDL grows revenue fast but doesn't convert it to profit at CDSL-like rates.

### Why the operating margin gap is the central question

CDSL earns 51% OPM with revenue ₹1,145 Cr and PAT ₹455 Cr. NSDL earns 28% OPM with revenue ₹1,530 Cr and PAT ₹380 Cr. NSDL has 33% more revenue but generates 16% less profit. ROE diverges similarly — CDSL 24.5% versus NSDL 17.4%. This is not a temporary phenomenon; it has been consistent across multiple years. The structural reasons:

NSDL's customer base is heavily institutional. FIIs, mutual funds, insurance companies, large issuers, banks — these are high-touch, high-customisation, regulator-facing relationships. They generate revenue per customer that is high in absolute terms but require thicker operating infrastructure (compliance, custom reporting, dispute resolution, large-account servicing teams). The marginal account economics are weaker than retail.

CDSL's customer base is heavily retail. Zerodha, Groww, Upstox, every discount broker that opened the demat account boom of 2018-2024 defaulted to CDSL. Retail demat is a near-zero-marginal-cost business once the technology infrastructure exists — adding a million accounts costs almost nothing per account, and revenue compounds on transaction fees, IPO subscription fees, and NRI account services. The 51% OPM reflects this.

NSDL has more employees, more legacy systems, more institutional customisation. Cutting costs is hard — the regulator requires the operations. Margin convergence with CDSL is unlikely in any reasonable time frame.

### Strengths

- **Network-effect monopoly inside its segment.** Indian depositories are duopolistic by regulation — only NSDL and CDSL exist, and SEBI has not granted a third licence in 25+ years. The institutional share NSDL holds is sticky; brokers and banks do not switch depositories casually because of the data migration, integration, and customer notification cost. Phase 0.5 (Pre-existing capability) is firmly in the "Built and operating" form: 30-year infrastructure, full regulatory backing, deep customer relationships. The business is not vulnerable to a new entrant.
- **Larger custody footprint and infrastructure scale.** 65,391 DP service centres versus CDSL's 18,918 — roughly 3.5x the physical reach. This is the institutional mix at work: large brokers and banks operate more service touchpoints than discount brokers. Custody value is materially higher. NSDL also dominates in specific product categories like commercial paper, certificates of deposit, and non-listed corporate bonds where retail depositories have minimal share.
- **Reasonable valuation versus CDSL on every metric.** P/B 7.39x vs CDSL 13.4x. P/E 46x vs CDSL 57.7x. The discount is partly justified by lower ROE and OPM — but not fully. CDSL trades at a 81% premium to NSDL on book, which implicitly underwrites that CDSL's retail-driven growth continues uninterrupted. If retail demat growth slows (already showing signs — net account additions decelerated in FY26 after the 2021-23 boom), the premium compresses and NSDL relatively re-rates.
- **Strong balance sheet, no leverage.** Borrowings ₹0 effectively. Operating cash flow consistently above PAT. Dividend payout 21% — conservative but covers buybacks and growth capex. Adequate runway for any product investment without dilution.
- **Adjacent revenue growth coming.** NSDL Database Management (NDML) handles e-KYC, account aggregator infrastructure, and the National Centre for Financial Education. These are non-depository revenue streams that compound on India's broader financial digitisation. CDSL has equivalent businesses (CVL — CDSL Ventures Limited) but smaller. NSDL's NDML is less-discussed by sell-side and could surprise on the upside.

### Concerns

- **The OPM gap to CDSL is structural, not temporary.** This is the single biggest concern. A 23-percentage-point gap in operating margin between two companies in the same regulated duopoly is not a fixable execution issue — it is a customer mix outcome. Cost rationalisation can move OPM from 28% to maybe 32-35% over multiple years. Reaching CDSL's 51% would require a complete change of customer mix, which the regulatory structure does not permit.
- **The retail account growth driver tilts toward CDSL.** Discount brokers Zerodha and Groww together account for the majority of new demat accounts opened in India over the past five years. Both default to CDSL. This is not because of any product superiority — CDSL's API integration was earlier and the technical migration cost is now prohibitive. Every incremental retail account opened in the next 5-10 years primarily benefits CDSL. NSDL participates in the institutional layer of this growth (custody fees on increased equity AUM) but does not capture the demat-account-fee revenue stream at the rate CDSL does.
- **No promoter, hybrid governance.** Post-IPO, NSDL has no defined promoter. The largest shareholders are NSE (effectively a competitor in equity infrastructure), IDBI Bank, HDFC Bank, SBI, and Deutsche Bank — all DIIs holding 37.64% combined. This is similar to BSE's structure, and BSE has performed well, but the absence of a single committed long-term controlling shareholder is a governance risk if business pressures emerge. There is no Lalvani family, no Murugappa group — no single party whose net worth depends on the business compounding.
- **NSE conflict of interest.** NSE owns ~24% of NSDL pre-IPO and retains a meaningful stake post-IPO. NSE is also the dominant equity exchange and is preparing its own IPO at ~₹7 lakh Cr valuation. NSE has commercial interests that may not perfectly align with NSDL's — for example, NSE could in theory channel listings, IPO subscriptions, and other fee-generating activities through CDSL to extract better economics from a less-conflicted depository. This conflict is mitigated by SEBI oversight but not eliminated.
- **Valuation requires forward growth not yet confirmed.** At 46x trailing P/E and 5-year profit CAGR of 15%, trailing PEG is about 3x. The framework's Phase 5.6 says forward PEG can be primary valuation when trailing is uninformative — which applies if growth is accelerating. The 5-year revenue CAGR (27%) is faster than profit CAGR (15%) — meaning growth is happening, but operating leverage is not. Without operating leverage, multiple compression risk activates the moment revenue growth slows.

### The compounding equation

Take FY26 PAT ₹380 Cr as base. The forward question is what drives EPS growth and at what rate.

Three drivers:
- **Equity AUM custody fees.** As Indian equity AUM compounds at 15-18% per year, NSDL's custody-fee revenue compounds with it. This is the dominant revenue line and is structurally correlated to overall market capitalisation.
- **Transaction and demat services.** Slower-growing line; tracks number of corporate actions, IPOs, debt issuances. Lumpy quarter-to-quarter, mid-single-digit growth over time.
- **NDML adjacent revenue.** e-KYC, account aggregator, financial education infrastructure. Currently small contribution, growing faster than the main business — could be 10-15% of revenue by FY29 from a smaller base today.

Three forward states:

**Most likely (55-60%):** Revenue grows 15-18% per annum to ₹2,500-2,800 Cr by FY29. OPM creeps from 28% to 32% as cost rationalisation gradually plays out. PAT grows ~18-20% per annum to ₹700-800 Cr. The market re-rates modestly from 46x to 50-55x as forward earnings catch up. Three-year price target ₹1,500-1,800 — a 70-100% return, IRR roughly 18-25%.

**Optimistic (20-25%):** NDML adjacent revenue accelerates faster than expected (account aggregator scales to genuine size). Operating leverage materialises with OPM reaching 38-40% by FY29 as the institutional revenue base absorbs technology investments already made. PAT compounds 25%+ per annum to ₹950-1,100 Cr. Multiple expands to 55-60x as the market starts treating NSDL closer to CDSL. Three-year price target ₹2,400-2,800 — 175-220% return.

**Adverse (15-20%):** Retail demat tailwind concentrates entirely with CDSL. NSDL's revenue growth slows to 8-10% (institutional only). Operating leverage absent; OPM stuck at 28%. PAT compounds at 10-12%. Multiple compresses to 25-30x. Three-year price target ₹650-800 — flat to mildly negative.

Probability-weighted, the IRR sits around 12-18%, which is barely above India equity benchmark return at meaningfully higher single-stock risk. Tracking position is the right call — not full sizing.

### Multi-bagger math and margin of safety

The compounding equation above gives 3-year price targets per scenario. Discounting back to present at a 13% required return converts these into present fair values — what the business is worth today, against which the current price can be compared. Share count is 20 Cr (Mcap ₹17,503 Cr ÷ CMP ₹875). FY26 PAT base = ₹380 Cr.

| Scenario | FY29 PAT (₹ Cr) | PAT CAGR | Exit P/E | FY29 price | Present fair value @ 13% disc | Probability |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Bear | ~520 | 11% | 27x | ₹702 | **₹487** | 15-20% |
| Base | ~750 | 18% | 52x | ₹1,950 | **₹1,352** | 55-60% |
| Bull | ~1,025 | 25%+ | 57x | ₹2,921 | **₹2,024** | 20-25% |

**Margin of safety arithmetic at CMP ₹875:**

```
Base case fair value (today)    = ₹1,352
Bear case fair value (today)    = ₹487
CMP                              = ₹875

Margin of safety vs base case   = (1,352 − 875) / 1,352 = 35.3%
Upside if right (to base)        = (1,352 − 875) / 875   = 54.5%
Downside if wrong (to bear)      = (875 − 487)   / 875   = 44.3%
Asymmetry ratio                  = 54.5 / 44.3          = 1.23x
```

**Does this clear the Grade B threshold?** The framework requires 20-35% MOS for a Grade B name. NSDL clears that at the base case (35.3%). It would fail for a Grade C (which needs 40%+) but Grade C is not the assigned quality. On MOS alone, the entry threshold is met.

**Why the call is still tracking-not-full-buy.** The asymmetry ratio at 1.23x is below the framework's 1.5x preferred threshold. The framework decision matrix is explicit: Grade B + asymmetry 1.5-2x + strong thesis = tracking position 1-2%; below 1.5x = watchlist. NSDL sits in the gap — the MOS is adequate, the asymmetry is not. The verdict resolves the tension by sizing at the floor of the tracking band (1.0-1.5%) rather than refusing to commit. Per Instruction 8, defaulting to watchlist when a Grade B business has 35% MOS is the equal-and-opposite error to over-committing — paralysis dressed as discipline.

**The single most sensitive variable is the FY29 exit multiple, not the FY29 earnings.** Base case PAT ₹750 Cr at 40x = ₹1,500 fair value today (still 13% MOS); at 45x = ₹1,690 (24% MOS); at 55x = ₹2,065 (43% MOS). The exit multiple depends on whether the market continues treating Indian depositories as scarce, regulated franchises (CDSL today trades at 57x) or whether the sector re-rates down toward broader financial infrastructure (15-25x). The bear case bakes in the latter; the base bakes in moderate compression; the bull bakes in continued scarcity premium.

**Decision rules tied to the math:**
- Move from tracking to **BUY** (3-5%) at CMP ≤ ₹720 — at that price, base-case MOS rises to 47% and asymmetry climbs above 2x.
- Move from tracking to **BUY** at any CMP if forward EPS growth confirms above 22% on two consecutive quarterly results — that moves the FY29 PAT estimate from ₹750 toward ₹900 and present base-case fair value past ₹1,600.
- Move from tracking to **EXIT** if FY27 actual PAT undershoots ₹420 Cr (10% YoY growth or below) — that signals the bear-case path is materialising and the present fair value collapses toward ₹500.

The labels match consensus here. NSDL is what consensus thinks it is. This is a quality compounder bet, not a re-rating bet — expected return comes from earnings compounding, not multiple expansion. Adjust expectations accordingly.

Distribution density growth rate sits in the **15-20%** band when measured by custody value, but in the **<15%** band when measured by service centres or demat accounts. By the framework's Phase 0.6 criterion, this fires the trigger only on the custody-value metric — the others would not qualify.

## Phase 1.2.1 — Mix-shift compounding test

Three valid states from the framework. NSDL's situation:

The mix question for NSDL is whether NDML (adjacent revenue from e-KYC, account aggregator, financial education infrastructure) compounds faster than core depository revenue and reaches a meaningful share. Currently NDML is roughly 10-12% of revenue. If it reaches 20-25% by FY29 — and NDML earns higher margins than core depository — both revenue mix and consolidated margin shift favourably.

Status: **mix shifting toward higher-margin segments at <200bps per year.** The mix shift is happening but slowly. NDML revenue is growing at perhaps 25-30% per annum versus core depository at 15-18%, but starting from a smaller base means the consolidated mix changes only ~150bps per year. Below the framework's 200bps threshold for "strongest form" but not in the "stable mix" category either.

Yellow flag, not a kill signal.

## Phase 5.6 — Forward PEG primary valuation

Trailing P/E 46x. Five-year profit CAGR 15%. Trailing PEG = 46 / 15 = 3.07x. Above the framework's 2.0x ceiling.

Forward case requires growth acceleration. The most likely scenario above gives forward 3-year EPS CAGR of 18-20% — bringing forward PEG to roughly 2.3-2.5x. Optimistic case gives 25% CAGR — forward PEG 1.85x, fair value zone.

For the recommendation to upgrade from tracking to buy, forward EPS growth must confirm above 22% on two consecutive quarterly results. That is the mechanical decision rule — not a vibe check.

## Glossary — Key Terms

**Depository / DP / DPS centre**
A depository is the electronic record-keeper that holds securities (shares, bonds, mutual fund units, government securities) in digital form. India has only two: NSDL and CDSL. A Depository Participant (DP) is the agent — typically a bank or broker — that opens demat accounts for end customers; the DP holds the customer relationship while the depository holds the actual security record. A DP service centre (DPS) is a physical branch or office where a DP operates. NSDL has 65,391 DPS centres versus CDSL's 18,918 — that 3.5x infrastructure gap reflects the institutional customer mix, where large brokers and banks operate more physical touchpoints than discount brokers.

**Demat account**
The electronic account through which an investor holds shares rather than in paper form. Mandatory in India since 1996 for listed securities. India had roughly 13-14 crore demat accounts as of FY26, the majority opened post-2018 during the discount-broker boom and predominantly with CDSL.

**Custody value**
The total market value of securities held in dematerialised form by the depository. NSDL's custody value is approximately ₹400 lakh crore and tracks Indian equity market capitalisation. Custody fees (a few basis points of value per year) are a major revenue line, which is why depository revenue compounds mechanically with equity AUM.

**NDML (NSDL Database Management Ltd)**
A subsidiary of NSDL that runs adjacent businesses: e-KYC (electronic identity verification), account aggregator infrastructure, tax payment systems, and the National Centre for Financial Education. Roughly 10-12% of NSDL consolidated revenue today and growing 25-30% per year — meaningfully faster than core depository revenue, which is why it is central to the mix-shift thesis.

**Account Aggregator (AA)**
A regulated framework introduced by RBI and SEBI that lets individuals share their own financial data (bank accounts, mutual fund holdings, insurance policies, GST records) across institutions with explicit consent. The AA ecosystem creates infrastructure businesses for licensed aggregators — NSDL's NDML is one of three licensed AA operators. The market is in early innings but compounding fast as more banks and fintechs integrate.

**e-KYC**
Electronic Know Your Customer — a digital identity-verification service that uses Aadhaar and PAN to authenticate a customer for account opening, loan application, or investment. NDML offers e-KYC as a service to banks, brokers, fintechs and mutual fund houses. Per-transaction fees are small but volumes are enormous, making it a high-operating-leverage business.

**Trailing P/E vs Forward P/E vs PEG**
Trailing P/E = current price ÷ last-year earnings per share (NSDL: ₹875 ÷ ₹19 ≈ 46x). Forward P/E = price ÷ next-year *expected* earnings. PEG = P/E ÷ earnings growth rate (%). A 46x P/E on a business growing earnings 15% per year gives PEG of ~3x (expensive on growth basis). A 46x P/E on a business growing at 25% gives PEG of ~1.85x (fair). PEG is the correct comparison metric when growth rates vary across companies. The framework's Phase 5.6 requires forward PEG as the primary valuation tool when trailing earnings do not reflect run-rate.

**Operating margin (OPM)**
Operating profit divided by revenue. NSDL's 28% versus CDSL's 51% is the central concern of this note. Operating margin measures how efficiently a business converts revenue into operating profit before interest and tax — it captures cost discipline, pricing power, and customer mix.

**Promoter / "no defined promoter"**
In Indian listed companies, "promoter" refers to the founding or controlling shareholder group classified by SEBI as in control of the business. NSDL has *no defined promoter* post-IPO — the largest shareholders are large financial institutions (NSE, HDFC Bank, SBI, IDBI, Deutsche Bank, Standard Chartered). This is structurally similar to BSE Ltd, and it creates a different governance profile from typical promoter-controlled Indian companies. There is no single party whose net worth is tied to long-term business performance.

**OFS overhang**
Offer For Sale — a mechanism for existing large shareholders to sell shares directly into the market. An "OFS overhang" is the market's perception that a known large shareholder may sell stock soon, which depresses the price until the OFS is completed. For NSDL, the pre-IPO institutional shareholders (NSE, IDBI, etc.) hold large stakes and may run OFS transactions over coming years to bring holdings below regulatory thresholds, creating a persistent overhang.

**FII / DII**
Foreign Institutional Investor / Domestic Institutional Investor — the two main classes of institutional buyers in Indian equity markets. NSDL's combined institutional ownership of 37.64% means these holders are the most likely sellers if the thesis weakens; tracking their direction quarterly is standard practice.

**Reverse DCF**
A valuation cross-check that asks: at the current market price, what growth rate is the market implicitly assuming? Computed by holding terminal multiple and discount rate constant and solving for the growth rate. For NSDL at ₹875, ~22% growth is implied — meaning the market expects NSDL to grow earnings at 22% per year for the next several years. The framework uses this to surface where the analyst disagrees with consensus.
