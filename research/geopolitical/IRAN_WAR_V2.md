### What Happened: The April 8 Ceasefire

On Day 40 (April 8, 2026), the US and Iran agreed to a **2-week ceasefire**, brokered by Pakistan — one hour before Trump's self-imposed escalation deadline expired.

**Terms as publicly stated:**
- Iran opens the Strait of Hormuz completely and immediately
- US suspends bombing and strikes on Iran for 2 weeks
- Formal negotiations begin in Islamabad on April 10 (US delegation: JD Vance, Steve Witkoff, Jared Kushner)
- Lebanon explicitly **excluded** — Netanyahu insisted; active Israeli strikes on Hezbollah resumed immediately
- Iran claims the deal is based on its 10-point plan (lift all sanctions, withdraw all US forces from the region, Iranian control over Hormuz)
- Trump calls Iran's 10-point plan a "workable basis on which to negotiate" — deliberately non-committal

Both sides declared victory. Iran says it forced the US to accept its terms. Trump says Iran agreed to open Hormuz under US pressure.

We never weighted that. **Key learning: never write off a mediator from one failure — track their incentive, not just their last outcome.**

### Will the Ceasefire Hold?

#### Game Theory

The negotiating gap is enormous. Iran's 10-point plan demands lifting all sanctions and withdrawing all US forces. Trump's "workable basis" commits to nothing. These are not compatible positions for a 2-week negotiation.

The "both sides claim victory" framing creates a **loss aversion trap in Islamabad**: any concession now looks like admitting you lied about winning. IRGC hardliners cannot drop the 10-point plan without losing face domestically. Trump cannot agree to full sanctions removal and force withdrawal — Congress would block it. Neither side can back down without looking like they lied to their own people.

**The Lebanon separability problem is a structural flaw in the deal.** Netanyahu explicitly excluded Lebanon, and Israeli strikes resumed the hour the ceasefire was announced. Iran cannot stand by while Hezbollah — its primary proxy network — is being destroyed. Any Iranian response (even through proxy) = ceasefire violation. This is a design trap, not a design flaw.

**Russia's incentives are unchanged.** They continue feeding satellite intelligence, profiting from high oil, and keeping US attention diverted from Ukraine. They were not at the Islamabad table. They will not be.

#### Behavioral Economics

**Optimism bias in markets:** The ceasefire is visible and salient. The reasons it might fail (Lebanon, Russia, negotiating gap) are invisible and distributed. Markets will reduce the oil risk premium too fast. The endowment effect means Iran treats the Hormuz opening as a loan, not a concession — re-closing it requires no admission of failure.

**Reactive devaluation:** Whatever the US offers in Islamabad will be dismissed by IRGC hardliners as insufficient; whatever Iran proposes will be seen by Trump's team as maximalist posturing. This is structural to adversarial negotiations — documented in every major Middle East peace process since 1973.

#### Historical Base Rates

| Conflict | First ceasefire | Cycles before final deal |
|---|---|---|
| Iran-Iraq War (1980-88) | Failed | 9+ ceasefire attempts over 8 years |
| 1973 Yom Kippur | Broke within hours | 2 attempts |
| 2014 Gaza | Failed | 9 attempts before one held |

| June 2025 12-Day War | Held 7 months | 1 — then became this war |

Base rate: first ceasefires in comparable conflicts with ongoing subsidiary wars (Lebanon), irreconcilable public positions, and no enforcement power break or stall in ~60-65% of cases within the first ceasefire window.

## Part 2: New Development — The Twelve-Day War Precedent

Before the 2026 war, there was a prior Iran-Israel conflict few analysts are referencing: the **Twelve-Day War** (June 13-24, 2025).

- Israel struck Iran's military and nuclear facilities in a surprise attack
- Iran retaliated — but within 12 days, a ceasefire was brokered by the **US and Qatar**
- The ceasefire held (with initial violations) until February 28, 2026 — when the 2026 war broke it

**Why this matters profoundly:**

1. **Iran learned from the 12-Day War.** In June 2025, Iran fought conventionally and accepted a ceasefire within 12 days. This time, they deployed the Hormuz blockade immediately — a strategic weapon they held back in 2025. The 2026 strategy is deliberately designed to NOT end in 12 days.

2. **Second wars are always harder to end than first wars.** The credible commitment problem is worse — both sides already know the other will break agreements (Iran broke the June 2025 ceasefire by allowing 2026 to happen; the US broke it by launching the Feb 28 strikes).

3. **Qatar's mediation role is compromised.** Qatar brokered the June 2025 ceasefire but was then attacked by Iran (first GCC member struck). Qatar is less able to mediate from a neutral position now.

4. **The US entered this war with overconfidence.** The 12-Day War may have convinced the US that Iran would fold quickly again. That miscalculation is one reason Trump's "4-week timetable" was so wrong.

**Base rate update:** The June 2025 ceasefire had Iran accepting a deal within 12 days. The 2026 war has now gone 35+ days with Iran rejecting all proposals. This isn't the same Iran — it's an Iran that has hardened its strategy specifically to avoid the outcome of the first war.

## Part 3: The Critical Blind Spot — India-US Trade Deal (Feb 2, 2026)

This is the most important thing the March 21 analysis missed, and it fundamentally changes India's vulnerability profile.

### What Happened

On **February 2, 2026** — just 26 days before the Iran war started — PM Modi struck a trade deal with Trump:

- The US agreed to cut tariffs on India from 50% to 18%
- In exchange, India committed to **stop purchasing Russian Federation oil**
- This was announced by both the White House and confirmed by Al Jazeera

### Why This Is a Problem

India had been the world's second-largest buyer of Russian oil since the Ukraine war, at deep discounts. This Russian oil supply was India's primary way of reducing its Hormuz dependence and managing import costs.

**The timing is deeply problematic:**

```
Feb 2:  India-US trade deal signed — India commits to stop Russian oil
Feb 28: Iran war begins — Strait of Hormuz effectively blocked
        India's primary non-Hormuz bypass (Russian oil) now politically off-limits
```

India is now forced to source crude from:
- US crude (more expensive, longer shipping distances)
- West African crude (Nigeria, Angola — more expensive, infrastructure constraints)

**What this changes for India:**

| Factor | With Russian Oil | Without Russian Oil |
|--------|-----------------|---------------------|
| Import cost premium vs Brent | -15 to -25% discount | At par or premium |
| Hormuz dependence | Reducible to ~30% | Harder to reduce below 40-50% |
| India's oil import bill increase (vs pre-war) | Partly manageable | Fully exposed to Brent spike |
| CAD impact at $110 Brent | ~2.5-3% of GDP | ~3-3.5% of GDP |

The March 21 analysis stated "India increased non-Hormuz sourcing to 70%." This needs revision. India did increase alternative sourcing — but the Russian oil component is now constrained by the Feb 2 political commitment. The actual non-Hormuz freely-available supply is lower than the 70% headline suggests.

### The Second-Order Implication

India made this commitment assuming it could use the US oil supply as a substitute for Russian oil. But US crude also transits through global shipping networks and takes longer to reach India. At $110+ oil, the cost of pivoting away from discounted Russian crude is substantial — adding further pressure to the CAD, the rupee, and fiscal subsidies.

## Part 5: The Trump April 1 Speech — What It Signals

Trump's primetime speech on April 1 was closely watched for signals of de-escalation. It gave none:

**Key statements:**
- War is "nearing completion" (contradicted by all external evidence)
- Will hit Iran "extremely hard" over next 2-3 weeks
- Threatened to destroy "each and every one of their electric generating plants... probably simultaneously" if no deal
- "Discussions are ongoing" (Iran denied this the same day)

**What to read into the speech:**

1. **"Nearing completion" is political rhetoric, not operational reality.** Trump has said this repeatedly. Iran shot down a US F-15E on April 3 — two days after the speech.

2. **The electric grid threat is an escalation ladder signal.** Striking power grids is a step up from striking military and nuclear infrastructure. It's the kind of strike that produces civilian suffering and global condemnation. Trump putting it on the table signals either: (a) genuine frustration and willingness to escalate, or (b) a negotiating bluff designed to extract concessions. Iran has to treat it as real.

3. **The firing of Army Chief of Staff by Hegseth during Week 5** is an internal disruption signal — civilian-military friction during an active war is never a sign of smooth strategy execution.

4. **Iran's response:** Vowed "more crushing, broader, and more destructive" attacks. Not a de-escalation signal.

**Net reading:** The speech hardened the Scenario C (Escalation) probability. The door to negotiation is formally still open, but the room has been cleared of furniture.

## Part 10: What This Means for Indian Investors (Munger Lens)

The Munger framework asks: **is the intrinsic value of the business permanently impaired, or is the stock price temporarily depressed by macro fear?**

For the vast majority of high-quality Indian businesses, the answer is: **temporarily depressed.** An oil shock — even a prolonged one — does not permanently destroy the earnings power of a well-run consumer goods company, a private bank with a clean book, or a quality IT services firm.

**The opportunities are in businesses where:**
1. The stock is pricing in the escalation scenario (Scenario C, 25-30%) but the business only needs the prolonged-conflict scenario (Scenario B) to be fine
2. The oil cost impact is transient and passable (not structural)
3. The balance sheet is strong enough to survive 12-18 months of elevated costs
4. Management has demonstrated pricing power in past inflationary cycles

**The traps to avoid:**
1. Airlines — structurally destroyed in any scenario where oil stays above $100 for 6+ months
2. OMCs (Indian Oil, BPCL, HPCL) — subsidy burden is political, not commercial
3. Highly leveraged companies — rising rates + rupee depreciation = double squeeze on debt servicing
4. Companies with large Hormuz-dependent supply chains and no pricing power

**The timing:** The best entry points for long-term investors will likely come during the Q1 FY27 earnings season (July-August 2026) when the damage is maximally visible but the war's eventual resolution is also becoming clearer. This is when the market tends to sell good businesses at bad-business prices.
