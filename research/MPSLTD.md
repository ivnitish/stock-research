## Summary Verdict

### Why this business?

MPS takes the raw manuscripts that academic and scientific publishers receive and turns them into finished, discoverable, hosted content — copy-editing, typesetting into XML, building the journal websites that host them, and increasingly the eLearning courseware around them. It has done this for the world's large Scientific-Technical-Medical (STM) publishers since 1970, and it does it at extraordinary economics: 39% return on capital employed, 31% return on equity, 8-day working capital, and almost no debt. The catch is that it barely reinvests — it pays out roughly 90-95% of profit as dividends most years — so the business doesn't compound by ploughing cash back into itself. It compounds by harvesting that cash and buying small, often distressed, publishing-technology and eLearning businesses cheaply, integrating them, and lifting their margins. The bet is on the Arora family's capital allocation and on the durability of margins in a world where AI can now do some of the content-production work MPS charges for.

### Strengths

- **Elite, capital-light economics that are getting better, not worse.** ROCE 39%, ROE 31% (FY26), operating margin expanded from 24% (FY20) to 31% (FY26) and stepped to 34% in the June-2026 quarter. Free cash flow essentially equals net profit (FY26 OCF ₹197 Cr vs PAT ₹173 Cr). [Source: Screener.in, 24-Jul-2026]
- **Pre-existing capability — the HighWire platform plus a 55-year STM content pipeline.** MPS owns HighWire (acquired 2021), one of the last independent journal-hosting platforms after Elsevier and Wiley bought the competing platforms; it runs an XML/content production pipeline embedded in the largest publishers' workflows; and it holds Tata Interactive Systems (2018) and Liberate (2023) in eLearning. These are operating assets generating revenue today, not capability management still has to build. [Source: mpslimited.com press releases; Screener.in]
- **Owner-operator with a real capital-allocation record.** Promoter (ADI BPO / the Arora family) holds 68.34%, stable across the last four quarters, no pledge disclosed. Founder Nishith Arora completed a clean succession to son Rahul Arora (Chairman & CEO), who bought HighWire out of distress reportedly cheaply and turned it profitable — the margin trajectory is the evidence. [Source: Screener.in shareholding, 24-Jul-2026; mpslimited.com]
- **Cash returned, not hoarded.** ~90-95% payout in most years (FY25 95%, FY24 108%), ~3% dividend yield at CMP — shareholders get paid while they wait, and the balance sheet never bloats. [Source: Screener.in payout history]

The thesis depends on Rahul Arora continuing to find and integrate cheap deals — a finite, two-sided runway, not a self-sustaining compounding machine.

Not through reinvestment — payout is too high for that. It compounds by earning ~30% ROE on a small retained base, then deploying accumulated cash and occasional debt into cheap bolt-on acquisitions whose margins it then lifts, with AI-driven productivity now adding a margin tailwind on top. Growth is capital-allocation-driven and lumpy, not a smooth reinvestment runway.

### Second-Order Stress Test

**5-Whys on the ROIC engine:**

> Why does this business earn ~39% return on capital today?
> 1. It sells content-production and platform services at high margin with almost no fixed capital tied up (8-day working capital, asset-light).
> 2. Why? → Its costs are mostly Indian labour billed to Western publishers at a large arbitrage, and the platform (HighWire) is software with near-zero marginal cost.
> 3. Why can it hold that pricing? → Its work is embedded in publishers' production workflows — XML pipelines, hosting, editorial standards — so switching vendor means re-engineering a live journal operation.
> 4. Why is switching so costly for the publisher? → The content, metadata, and hosting are integrated into how the journal is discovered and cited; disruption risks the publisher's own product quality and SEO.
> 5. Why is that durable? → It is a switching cost embedded in the customer's infrastructure — structural, not a contract MPS controls. **But the fifth answer has an asterisk: the labour-arbitrage layer (step 2) is a cost advantage MPS controls, and it is exactly what AI attacks.** So the platform/switching-cost moat is durable; the content-services margin is the fragile part.

Revenue is roughly ₹880-950 Cr (from ₹768 Cr FY26), EBITDA margin holds near 33-34% helped by AI productivity in content production, and PAT is ~₹215-230 Cr. ## Deep Dives

### A. Does generative AI kill MPS or feed it?

This is the only question that matters for the valuation, so it deserves being spelled out plainly. About 60% of MPS's revenue is "Research Solutions" — taking a scientist's submitted manuscript and turning it into a published, hosted article. Physically that means copy-editing the language, marking it up into structured XML so it is machine-readable and searchable, laying it out, and hosting it on a journal platform. A large chunk of that is exactly the kind of structured, rules-based language work that large language models are now good at. So the bear case writes itself: if a publisher can run manuscripts through an AI pipeline for a fraction of what MPS charges, MPS's pricing collapses and its 30%+ margins with it.

The reason it is not that simple is where the value actually sits. First, MPS is not selling raw typing — it is selling a guaranteed, standards-compliant output integrated into a live publishing workflow, with the liability and quality control that a publisher will not casually hand to an unsupervised model on content that must be citable and permanent. Second, the platform layer — HighWire, the journal-hosting software — is software, not labour; AI does not commoditize the hosting relationship, and MPS is one of the last independent platforms after the big competitors were bought by publishers who are now conflicted rivals to their own customers. Third, and most important, the June-2026 margin print of 34% is the tell that so far AI is helping MPS, not hurting it: if AI were compressing pricing, margins would be falling, not stepping up. The most likely near-term reality is that MPS uses AI to do the content work with fewer people and pockets the difference as margin — which is a beneficiary, not a victim, dynamic.

The honest conclusion is that this is genuinely two-sided and cannot be resolved from outside the company. That is precisely why it belongs in the valuation as a cap on the multiple rather than as a settled bull or bear point. You underwrite it by refusing to pay a full price for a margin structure whose durability you cannot yet verify — hence buy on the pullback, and watch the one number (Research Solutions organic revenue) that will tell you which way it broke before the margin does.

### B. Why "high ROE" and "low growth" coexist here

It looks like a contradiction: a business earning 31% on equity should be able to grow fast. It doesn't, because it chooses to pay almost everything out. Growth from within a business is capped at the return on equity multiplied by the fraction of profit retained. MPS earns ~31% but retains only ~5-10% of profit in a normal year, so its self-funded organic growth ceiling is low-single-digit — roughly 2-3%. Everything above that in the reported numbers came from two other places: acquisitions (Tata Interactive lifted FY19, Liberate lifted FY25) and margin expansion (24% to 34% over six years). This is why the framing "reinvestment compounder" is wrong for MPS and why the growth is lumpy — it arrives in acquisition-shaped steps, not a smooth curve. It also means the durability of the story rests on two finite things: management continuing to find cheap deals, and margins holding. Neither is a self-sustaining flywheel, which is why this is a Grade B compounder rather than a Grade A one despite the elite returns on capital.

## 3. Compounding Engine — Q&A

### Q0. Operating Leverage Test — does growth create margin leverage?

- Revenue growth (TTM ~10%; Q1 FY27 +20% YoY)
- EBITDA margin now vs 3 years ago: 31% (FY23) → 34% (Q1 FY27); FY20 24% → FY26 31% (+700bps over 6 years)
- **Operating leverage present?** Yes — Q1 revenue +20% but PAT +43%, margin expanding faster than sales.
- Capacity utilization: n/a (asset-light services); the "capacity" is people, and AI is raising output per person.
- CWIP trend: negligible (₹0 Cr FY26) — no physical capex cycle.

### Q1. Is incremental ROIC genuinely high (>20%) — and structural, not cyclical?

Yes on headline ROCE (39% FY26), and the structural reason is real: MPS bills Western publishers for content and platform work performed largely with Indian labour and software, tying up almost no capital (8-day working capital, no inventory, minimal fixed assets in the core). On retained capital the returns are very high. The nuance is that including acquisition goodwill, incremental ROIC over FY23→FY26 is closer to ~20% — the deals add capital to the denominator. The structural driver (labour arbitrage + embedded switching costs + a software platform) is durable on the platform side; the arbitrage side is what AI could erode. What breaks it: AI compressing content-services pricing faster than MPS can convert AI into its own cost saving.

### Q2. How much capital can be redeployed at these returns — the reinvestment runway?

Very little organically — that is the defining feature. With ~90% payout, MPS retains only ₹10-20 Cr of the ~₹170-190 Cr it earns, so the self-funded runway is small. The real "runway" is the acquisition pipeline: how many HighWire- or Liberate-type assets exist that MPS can buy cheaply and improve. That is genuine but finite and opportunistic — it cannot be modeled as a steady reinvestment rate. The binding constraint is deal availability and price discipline, not capital.

### Q3. What's the implied compounding math?

Organic growth ceiling ≈ retention (~3-10%) × ROE (31%) ≈ 2-3% self-funded, plus mid-single-digit organic revenue in the existing book, plus margin expansion, plus episodic M&A. Blended, the last three years delivered ~15% PAT CAGR. A base case of ~14% forward is therefore anchored to observed history, not invented: mid-single-digit organic + partial margin retention + one accretive bolt-on over the period. Bull 20% requires margins to hold at 34% and another good acquisition; bear 8% is organic-only with AI pricing pressure. These rates feed the Multi-Bagger Math table.

### Q4. What breaks the thesis?

**The compounding breaks if:** generative AI compresses pricing on Research Solutions faster than MPS converts AI into internal cost savings — turning a margin tailwind into a revenue headwind.
**Leading indicator I'd see before the stock price tells me:** Research Solutions organic (ex-acquisition) revenue turning flat-to-negative for two consecutive quarters while EBITDA margin also rolls over.

## Glossary — Key Industry Terms

**STM publishing (Scientific, Technical, Medical)**
The segment of academic publishing covering research journals in science, technology, and medicine (e.g. Elsevier, Wiley, Springer Nature). MPS's core customers. Characterized by sticky, workflow-embedded vendor relationships and content that must be permanent and citable — which is why publishers don't casually switch production vendors or hand the work to unsupervised AI.

**Content production / XML typesetting**
Turning a submitted manuscript into a finished, machine-readable published article: language editing, structuring the text into XML (a tagged format that lets software search, link, and display it), and laying it out. This is ~60% of MPS revenue and the part most exposed to AI automation.

**Journal-hosting platform (HighWire)**
The software that hosts a publisher's journals online — where articles live, are discovered, searched, and cited. MPS acquired HighWire in 2021. Because it is software rather than labour, and one of the last platforms independent of the big publishers, it is the AI-resilient, annuity-like part of the business.

**eLearning / Corporate Learning (Tata Interactive Systems, Liberate)**
Digital courseware and training-content development for corporate and educational clients. MPS's faster-growing but more competitive and fragmented segment, built via acquisition.

**Payout ratio / retention ratio**
The fraction of profit paid out as dividends (payout) versus kept in the business (retention). MPS pays out ~90-95%, so it retains little — which is why its self-funded organic growth is low despite a 31% ROE, and why growth comes from acquisitions instead.
