## 0. First Principles Investment Logic (Added 2026-04-11)

Most investors list nuclear companies. The first principles approach asks: **what does it physically take to build one 700 MW PHWR, and who can supply it in India?**

### The Qualification Moat — The Real Edge

Nuclear component suppliers require NPCIL/AERB qualification that takes **3-5 years** to obtain. This is not a brand moat or scale moat — it is a **regulatory monopoly**. Once qualified, you are on a short list of approved suppliers for a ₹14-23 lakh Cr programme spanning 21 years.

The localisation mandate makes this more powerful. India currently imports 60-70% of reactor components. The SHANTI Bill + fleet mode execution (10 identical PHWRs built simultaneously) creates the same dynamic as defence indigenisation post-2016: the government will mandate 60-70% domestic content to reduce import dependence and build a domestic supply chain. Companies that are **already qualified** when this mandate arrives cannot be displaced by new entrants for 3-5 years minimum.

**The sequence the market hasn't fully priced:**
```
India needs 91 GW new nuclear (8.8 → 100 GW by 2047)
→ ₹14-23 lakh Cr capex over 21 years
→ Fleet mode = standardised components = predictable, recurring orders
→ Localisation mandate coming (precedent: defence indigenisation)
→ AERB/NPCIL qualification = 3-5 year barrier = de facto monopoly
→ MTAR, Walchandnagar, KSB, Patels Airtemp already qualified
→ Market hasn't priced the localisation mandate yet
→ Buy the qualified suppliers before tenders hit
```

### What to Monitor (Leading Indicators)

| Signal | What it means | Lead time before revenue |
|--------|--------------|-------------------------|
| DAE tender database — new qualification notices | Government opening a new component category to private suppliers | 12-18 months |
| NPCIL annual report — domestic content % | Localisation mandate tightening | 6-12 months |
| SHANTI Bill implementation rules | Private sector entry rules finalised | Opens new demand |
| Westinghouse/EDF JV announcements | Foreign vendors need Indian partners for AP1000/EPR localisation | 12-24 months |
| Fleet mode procurement notices | Batch orders for 10 PHWRs simultaneously — 10x order size vs single plant | Immediate |

## 1. The Theme: Why Nuclear, Why Now?

India has announced one of the most ambitious nuclear expansion programmes in history:

| Metric | Current | Target | Timeline | Multiple |
|--------|---------|--------|----------|----------|
| Installed nuclear capacity | 8.8 GW | 100 GW | By 2047 | **11x** |
| Intermediate target | 8.8 GW | 22.4 GW | By 2031-32 | 2.5x |
| Under construction now | — | 13.6 GW | Various stages | — |
| Nuclear share of total power | ~2% | ~15-20% (est.) | By 2047 | 8-10x |

**Why this is different from past announcements:**
- **SHANTI Bill 2025** — for the first time in India's history, private companies can own and operate nuclear plants (49% equity cap). This breaks the NPCIL monopoly.
- **Budget allocation:** ₹20,000 Cr for Small Modular Reactors (SMRs) alone
- **Liability reform:** Removal of supplier "right of recourse" — this was THE blocker for international vendors (Westinghouse, GE). Now resolved.
- **Fleet mode execution:** 10 identical 700 MW PHWRs being built simultaneously to reduce cost through learning curves
- **Repurposing old thermal plants:** Government will use retired coal plant land for nuclear — removes land acquisition bottleneck

**The maths of the opportunity:**
- Adding ~91 GW of new capacity (from 8.8 GW to 100 GW)
- At ₹15,000-25,000 Cr per GW (indigenous to foreign-assisted)
- **Total capital expenditure: ₹14-23 lakh Cr ($170-280 billion) over 21 years**
- Even the intermediate target (22.4 GW by 2032) implies ~₹2 lakh Cr of spending in the next 6 years

This is comparable to India's entire defence capital budget for 5 years. The companies positioned in this supply chain will see order books swell over the next decade.

## 2. Capital Cost Breakdown — Where Does the Money Go?

For every ₹15,000 Cr spent on 1 GW of nuclear capacity, here's how it splits:

| Component | % of Capital Cost | ₹ Cr per GW (at ₹15,000 Cr) | What's included |
|-----------|------------------|------------------------------|-----------------|
| **Onsite Labour & Construction** | 25% | 3,750 | Installation, assembly, civil construction workforce |
| **Mechanical Equipment** | 16% | 2,400 | Pumps, valves, piping, heat exchangers |
| **Nuclear Steam Supply System (NSSS)** | 12% | 1,800 | Reactor core, pressure vessels, steam generators |
| **Electrical & Generating Equipment** | 12% | 1,800 | Turbines, generators, power distribution |
| **Construction Materials** | 12% | 1,800 | High-grade steel, specialised concrete, containment structures |
| **Project Management** | 10% | 1,500 | Engineering, design, site supervision |
| **Instrumentation & Control** | 8% | 1,200 | Software, monitoring systems, control room tech |
| **Fuel & Other Services** | 5% | 750 | Initial fuel load (~3%), miscellaneous |

**Key insight for stock picking:** The first four categories (Labour + Mechanical + NSSS + Electrical) account for **65% of capex**. Companies positioned here have the most revenue leverage.

## 3. Value Chain → Sectors → Listed Companies

### Tier 1: Direct Nuclear Supply Chain (Highest Leverage)

These companies have direct nuclear orders or certifications. Revenue impact is immediate and large.

#### 3a. Nuclear Steam Supply System (NSSS) & Core Components — 12% of capex

The "nuclear island" — reactor core, pressure vessels, steam generators. Requires specialised certifications (ASME N-Stamp, AERB approval). Very high barriers to entry.

| Company | Ticker | What they supply | Moat / Position | Nuclear revenue % (est.) |
|---------|--------|-----------------|-----------------|-------------------------|
| **Larsen & Toubro** | LT | Steam generators, end shields, heavy forgings | **50%+ market share** in nuclear steam generators. First Indian company with ASME N-Stamp certification. | 2-3% of total (large company, nuclear is one segment) |
| **Walchandnagar Industries** | WALCHANDAG | Calandrias, end shields, dump tanks for PHWRs + Fast Breeders | Legacy DAE partner. Deep institutional relationships. | 30-40% (est.) — nuclear is a core segment |
| **MTAR Technologies** | MTARTECH | Fuel machining heads, coolant assemblies, high-precision reactor components | Niche specialist in precision engineering. | 15-20% (nuclear + space + defence) |

**Investability filter:** L&T is a mega-cap where nuclear is a small %. Walchandnagar and MTAR have higher revenue leverage but need fundamental analysis (ROCE, governance, order book visibility).

#### 3b. Turbine Island & Electrical Systems — 12% of capex

Converting steam to electricity. Turbine generators, electrical distribution, balance of plant.

| Company | Ticker | What they supply | Position | Nuclear leverage |
|---------|--------|-----------------|----------|-----------------|
| **BHEL** | BHEL | Turbine generator sets | **50% of India's current nuclear capacity** equipped by BHEL. Recently won ₹10,800 Cr order for 6 turbine islands. | Medium — nuclear is growing segment within thermal/power |
| **Power Mech Projects** | POWERMECH | Civil works on turbine island packages | Entered nuclear space with Kaiga contract | Low-Medium — one contract so far |

#### 3c. Engineering, Construction & Civil Works — 25% of capex (largest single category)

Building the physical plant — containment structures, reactor buildings, civil engineering.

| Company | Ticker | What they supply | Position | Nuclear leverage |
|---------|--------|-----------------|----------|-----------------|
| **Hindustan Construction Co** | HCC | Nuclear plant construction | **Built 60%+ of India's nuclear capacity.** The dominant nuclear constructor. | High — nuclear is a core competency |
| **Megha Engineering (MEIL)** | Unlisted | EPC for reactor construction | Won Kaiga Units 5 & 6 EPC contract | — |

#### 3d. Pumps, Valves, Forgings & Specialised Equipment — 16% of capex

Critical components with nuclear certifications. High barriers, low competition.

| Company | Ticker | What they supply | Moat | Nuclear leverage |
|---------|--------|-----------------|------|-----------------|
| **KSB Ltd** | KSB | Primary coolant pumps | **Only approved supplier** for primary coolant pumps in Indian nuclear reactors | Medium — nuclear is one segment |
| **Patels Airtemp** | PATELSAIR | Nuclear-grade heat exchangers, pressure vessels | **One of only 3 Indian firms** with N-NPT authorisation | High — already in our watchlist! |
| **Venus Pipes & Tubes** | VENUSPIPES | High-grade SS pipes for reactor cooling | Growing supplier, nuclear is new segment | Low-Medium |

**Key find: PATELSAIR is already on our watchlist** (C+ 13/25, CMP ₹219, base case ₹444). Nuclear orders could be a significant re-rating catalyst if they materialise. The N-NPT authorisation is a genuine moat — only 3 Indian firms have it.

## 4. The Madhu Kela Framework Applied

Madhu Kela's approach: find themes where the government is committed to spend (irreversible policy), then find companies with:
1. **Monopoly or near-monopoly position** in a critical supply chain link
2. **Certification moats** (nuclear certifications take 3-5 years to get)
3. **Small enough that the order book moves the needle** (L&T won't 2x because of nuclear; Walchandnagar might)
